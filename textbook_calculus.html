Chapter 1

Limits & Continuity

A limit answers the question: what value does a function approach as the input gets arbitrarily close to some point? This idea is the foundation of all of calculus — without it, derivatives and integrals have no rigorous meaning. In engineering, limits describe what happens to a signal as frequency goes to infinity. In physics, they capture what a particle's position approaches as time steps shrink to zero. Continuity is the minimum requirement for a function to be physically reasonable: no teleportation, no sudden jumps.

Diagram — secant lines approach tangent as h → 0

What a limit is. The limit $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L$ says: as $x$ gets arbitrarily close to $a$ (from either side), the output $f(x)$ gets arbitrarily close to $L$. The crucial point is that $x$ never actually reaches $a$ — the limit describes approach, not arrival. This is what makes limits useful at places where a function is undefined, like $\dfrac{\sin x}{x}$ at $x = 0$.

One-sided limits. Sometimes a function approaches different values from the left and the right. We write $\lim_{x\to a^-}$ for the left-hand limit and $\lim_{x\to a^+}$ for the right-hand limit. The two-sided limit $\lim_{x\to a}$ exists only when both one-sided limits exist and are equal.

Continuity. A function is continuous at $a$ when three conditions all hold: $f(a)$ is defined, the limit exists, and they agree: $\lim_{x\to a} f(x) = f(a)$. Intuitively, a continuous function can be drawn without lifting your pen. Polynomials, trig functions, and exponentials are continuous everywhere on their domains; rational functions break continuity wherever the denominator is zero.

Limits at infinity. $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty} f(x)$ asks what value $f$ settles toward as $x$ grows without bound. For rational functions, compare degrees: if the numerator's degree is less than the denominator's, the limit is $0$; if equal, the limit is the ratio of leading coefficients; if greater, the limit is $\pm\infty$.

L'Hôpital's Rule. When a limit produces the indeterminate form $0/0$ or $\pm\infty/\infty$, differentiate the numerator and denominator separately (not as a quotient), then re-evaluate. Repeat if the result is still indeterminate.

Key limit facts
$$\lim_{x\to a} x^n = a^n \qquad \lim_{x\to 0} \frac{\sin x}{x} = 1 \qquad \lim_{x\to\infty} \frac{1}{x} = 0$$
Worked example — evaluating $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to 3} \dfrac{x^2 - 9}{x - 3}$
Direct substitution gives $0/0$indeterminate form
$\dfrac{x^2-9}{x-3} = \dfrac{(x-3)(x+3)}{x-3} = x + 3$factor and cancel; valid since $x \ne 3$
$\displaystyle\lim_{x\to 3}(x+3) = 6$now substitute directly
Worked example — L'Hôpital on $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to 0} \dfrac{\sin x}{x}$
$0/0$ form; differentiate top and bottom separatelyL'Hôpital condition met
$= \displaystyle\lim_{x\to 0} \dfrac{\cos x}{1} = \cos 0 = 1$derivative of $\sin x$ is $\cos x$; derivative of $x$ is $1$
Worked example — $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty} \dfrac{3x^2 + 1}{5x^2 - 2}$
Degrees equal (both 2); limit = ratio of leading coefficientsdegree rule for rational functions
$= \dfrac{3}{5}$divide every term by $x^2$ and let $x\to\infty$

Chapter 2

The Derivative — Definition

The derivative is the instantaneous rate of change — the slope of the curve at a single point. Speed is the derivative of position; current is the derivative of charge; marginal cost is the derivative of total cost. Every optimization problem (maximizing profit, minimizing drag, finding the fastest route) requires finding where the derivative equals zero. Without the derivative, calculus cannot analyze anything that changes.

Diagram — tangent line at a point defines the derivative

What it measures. The derivative of $f$ at $x$ is the instantaneous rate of change of $f$ at that point. Think of average speed: drive 120 miles in 2 hours and your average speed is 60 mph. But your speedometer shows your speed right now — that is the derivative. As the time interval shrinks to zero, the average rate becomes the instantaneous rate.

The limit definition. To find the instantaneous rate formally, start with the slope of a secant line through $(x, f(x))$ and $(x+h, f(x+h))$, then let $h \to 0$ so the secant becomes a tangent line:

Derivative — limit definition
$$f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h}$$

If this limit exists, $f$ is differentiable at $x$. Geometrically, $f'(a)$ equals the slope of the tangent line at $(a, f(a))$.

Notation. All of the following mean "the derivative of $f$ with respect to $x$": $$f'(x) \qquad \frac{df}{dx} \qquad \frac{dy}{dx} \qquad Df(x)$$ Leibniz notation $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ is especially useful because it explicitly names both the output ($y$) and the input ($x$) being varied, which matters when multiple variables are in play.

The tangent line. Once you have the slope $f'(a)$, the equation of the tangent line at $(a, f(a))$ is found with point-slope form: $y - f(a) = f'(a)(x - a)$.

Worked example — derivative of $x^2$ from the definition
$\dfrac{(x+h)^2 - x^2}{h} = \dfrac{x^2 + 2xh + h^2 - x^2}{h}$expand $(x+h)^2$
$= \dfrac{2xh + h^2}{h} = 2x + h$cancel $h$ (valid since $h \ne 0$)
$\displaystyle\lim_{h\to 0}(2x + h) = 2x$let $h\to 0$; confirms the power rule
Worked example — tangent line to $y = x^2$ at $(1, 1)$
$f'(x) = 2x$; $f'(1) = 2$slope of tangent at $x = 1$
$y - 1 = 2(x - 1) \Rightarrow y = 2x - 1$point-slope form

Chapter 3

Basic Differentiation Rules

Computing a derivative from the limit definition every time would make calculus unusable in practice. The differentiation rules — power rule, sum rule, constant multiple — turn that limit process into a mechanical procedure. With them, you can differentiate any polynomial in seconds. Every physics simulation, every control system, every financial model uses these rules thousands of times per calculation without thinking about the limit definition.

Diagram — f(x) and f′(x) on the same axes

The limit definition works, but computing it from scratch every time would be tedious. These rules are proven once from the definition and then used freely. Together they let you differentiate any polynomial — and most functions you'll encounter — in seconds.

The Power Rule

What it is. To differentiate $x$ raised to any constant power $n$, bring the exponent down as a multiplier and reduce the exponent by one:

Power rule
$$\frac{d}{dx}[x^n] = nx^{n-1}$$

Why it works. The limit definition for $x^n$ produces a binomial expansion: $(x+h)^n = x^n + nx^{n-1}h + (\text{terms with } h^2, h^3, \ldots)$. After subtracting $x^n$ and dividing by $h$, all terms with $h^2$ or higher vanish as $h \to 0$, leaving exactly $nx^{n-1}$.

When to use it. The power rule works for any real exponent — positive integers ($x^3$), fractions ($x^{1/2} = \sqrt{x}$), and negatives ($x^{-1} = 1/x$). Rewrite roots and fractions as powers first.

How to use it.

  1. Rewrite any root or fraction as a power: $\sqrt{x} = x^{1/2}$, $\; 1/x^3 = x^{-3}$.
  2. Multiply the entire coefficient by the exponent: the new coefficient is $n \times (\text{old coefficient})$.
  3. Reduce the exponent by one: $n \to n - 1$.
  4. Simplify: rewrite negative or fractional exponents if needed.
Worked example — $\dfrac{d}{dx}[x^5]$
$= 5x^{5-1} = 5x^4$bring exponent down; reduce by 1
Worked example — $\dfrac{d}{dx}[\sqrt{x}]$
$\sqrt{x} = x^{1/2}$rewrite as a power
$\dfrac{d}{dx}[x^{1/2}] = \tfrac{1}{2}x^{-1/2}$power rule: bring down $\frac{1}{2}$, reduce exponent
$= \dfrac{1}{2\sqrt{x}}$rewrite $x^{-1/2} = 1/x^{1/2}$
Worked example — $\dfrac{d}{dx}\!\left[\dfrac{1}{x^3}\right]$
$\dfrac{1}{x^3} = x^{-3}$rewrite as negative exponent
$\dfrac{d}{dx}[x^{-3}] = -3x^{-4} = -\dfrac{3}{x^4}$power rule; rewrite result

The Constant Rule

What it is. The derivative of any constant is zero:

Constant rule
$$\frac{d}{dx}[c] = 0$$

Why it makes sense. A constant function $f(x) = c$ is a horizontal line. Horizontal lines have slope zero — so the derivative (which measures slope) is zero everywhere. Formally: the numerator of the limit definition becomes $f(x+h) - f(x) = c - c = 0$, so the limit is zero regardless of $h$.

In practice. Whenever you see a bare number with no $x$ attached — $-7$, $\pi$, $e$, $100$ — its derivative is $0$. This is why constant terms "disappear" when you differentiate.

The Constant Multiple Rule

What it is. A constant coefficient factors straight through the derivative:

Constant multiple rule
$$\frac{d}{dx}[c \cdot f(x)] = c \cdot f'(x)$$

Why it works. Multiplying $f$ by $c$ stretches every output by $c$. That stretches the rise of every secant line by $c$ while the run stays the same, so every slope is scaled by $c$ — including the limit slope, the derivative.

In practice. You can always pull a constant coefficient out to the front, differentiate the rest, then multiply back. For example: $\dfrac{d}{dx}[7x^3] = 7 \cdot \dfrac{d}{dx}[x^3] = 7 \cdot 3x^2 = 21x^2$.

The Sum & Difference Rule

What it is. Differentiation distributes over addition and subtraction — you can differentiate term by term:

Sum & difference rule
$$\frac{d}{dx}[f(x) \pm g(x)] = f'(x) \pm g'(x)$$

Why it works. The limit of a sum is the sum of the limits (a standard limit law). Applying this to the definition of the derivative: the numerator splits as $[f(x+h) \pm g(x+h)] - [f(x) \pm g(x)] = [f(x+h)-f(x)] \pm [g(x+h)-g(x)]$, and dividing by $h$ and taking the limit gives $f'(x) \pm g'(x)$.

In practice. With polynomials, differentiate each term independently. The constant rule, constant multiple rule, and power rule handle every term, so the sum rule is what lets you process the whole polynomial in one pass.

Worked example — differentiate $f(x) = 4x^3 - 7x^2 + 2x - 9$
$\dfrac{d}{dx}[4x^3] = 12x^2$constant multiple × power rule
$\dfrac{d}{dx}[-7x^2] = -14x$constant multiple × power rule
$\dfrac{d}{dx}[2x] = 2$ and $\dfrac{d}{dx}[-9] = 0$power rule on $x^1$; constant rule
$f'(x) = 12x^2 - 14x + 2$sum/difference rule combines the terms
Worked example — differentiate $g(x) = \dfrac{5}{x^2} + 3\sqrt{x}$
Rewrite: $5x^{-2} + 3x^{1/2}$convert to power notation first
$\dfrac{d}{dx}[5x^{-2}] = 5 \cdot (-2)x^{-3} = -\dfrac{10}{x^3}$constant multiple × power rule
$\dfrac{d}{dx}[3x^{1/2}] = 3 \cdot \tfrac{1}{2}x^{-1/2} = \dfrac{3}{2\sqrt{x}}$constant multiple × power rule
$g'(x) = -\dfrac{10}{x^3} + \dfrac{3}{2\sqrt{x}}$sum rule

Derivatives of Basic Functions

These are proven from the limit definition or known identities. Memorize them — they appear everywhere:

Exponential & logarithm
$$\frac{d}{dx}[e^x] = e^x \qquad \frac{d}{dx}[\ln x] = \frac{1}{x} \quad (x > 0)$$

$e^x$ is unique: it is its own derivative. The slope of $y = e^x$ at every point equals its height there.

Trigonometric functions
$$\frac{d}{dx}[\sin x] = \cos x \qquad \frac{d}{dx}[\cos x] = -\sin x$$ $$\frac{d}{dx}[\tan x] = \sec^2 x \qquad \frac{d}{dx}[\sec x] = \sec x \tan x$$

The derivative of sine is cosine, and the derivative of cosine introduces a minus sign — they chase each other around a four-step cycle: $\sin \to \cos \to -\sin \to -\cos \to \sin$.

Worked example — differentiate $h(x) = e^x + 5\sin x - 3\ln x$
$\dfrac{d}{dx}[e^x] = e^x$$e^x$ is its own derivative
$\dfrac{d}{dx}[5\sin x] = 5\cos x$constant multiple rule
$\dfrac{d}{dx}[-3\ln x] = -\dfrac{3}{x}$constant multiple rule
$h'(x) = e^x + 5\cos x - \dfrac{3}{x}$sum rule

Chapter 4

Product, Quotient & Chain Rules

Real functions are built by combining simpler ones — multiplied, divided, or composed. The product rule handles $f(x) \cdot g(x)$; the quotient rule handles $f(x)/g(x)$; the chain rule handles $f(g(x))$. Without the chain rule, you could not differentiate $\sin(x^2)$ or $e^{-x^2}$ — but those are exactly the forms that appear in signal processing, the Gaussian distribution, and wave equations. Together these three rules unlock differentiation of virtually every function you will encounter.

Diagram — chain rule as function composition f∘g

The basic power, sum, and constant rules handle simple expressions, but most real-world functions are built by combining simpler ones — multiplying them, dividing them, or plugging one inside another. Three rules cover these cases.

The Product Rule

What it is. When two differentiable functions $u(x)$ and $v(x)$ are multiplied together, the derivative of their product is not simply $u' \cdot v'$. Instead:

Product rule
$$\frac{d}{dx}[u \cdot v] = u'v + uv'$$

Why it exists. Think about how a small change $\Delta x$ affects the product $u \cdot v$. If $u$ grows by $\Delta u$ and $v$ grows by $\Delta v$, the new product is $(u + \Delta u)(v + \Delta v) = uv + u\,\Delta v + v\,\Delta u + \Delta u\,\Delta v$. Subtracting the original $uv$ and dividing by $\Delta x$, the $\Delta u\,\Delta v$ term vanishes as $\Delta x \to 0$, leaving $u\,\frac{dv}{dx} + v\,\frac{du}{dx}$. In words: each factor gets differentiated in turn while the other is held fixed, and the results are added.

When to use it. Reach for the product rule any time your function is written (or can be written) as two distinct parts multiplied together — for example $x^3 \sin x$, $e^x \ln x$, or $(x^2 + 1)(x - 4)$. If both parts are simple polynomials you could expand first, but the product rule works either way.

How to use it.

  1. Label the two factors: call one $u$ and the other $v$.
  2. Differentiate each one separately: find $u'$ and $v'$.
  3. Plug into the formula: $u'v + uv'$.
  4. Simplify if possible.
Worked example — $\dfrac{d}{dx}[x^2 \sin x]$
$u = x^2, \quad v = \sin x$identify the two factors
$u' = 2x, \quad v' = \cos x$differentiate each separately
$= u'v + uv' = 2x\sin x + x^2\cos x$apply the formula
Worked example — $\dfrac{d}{dx}[e^x \ln x]$
$u = e^x,\; u' = e^x \quad v = \ln x,\; v' = 1/x$both factors, both derivatives
$= e^x \ln x + e^x \cdot \dfrac{1}{x} = e^x\!\left(\ln x + \dfrac{1}{x}\right)$$u'v + uv'$; factor out $e^x$

The Quotient Rule

What it is. When one function is divided by another, the derivative follows a specific pattern — again, not simply $u'/v'$:

Quotient rule
$$\frac{d}{dx}\!\left[\frac{u}{v}\right] = \frac{u'v - uv'}{v^2}$$

Why it exists. You can derive the quotient rule from the product rule by writing $u/v = u \cdot v^{-1}$ and applying the chain rule to $v^{-1}$, giving $u'v^{-1} + u \cdot (-v^{-2})v' = \dfrac{u'v - uv'}{v^2}$. The denominator is squared because shrinking $v$ makes the fraction grow faster than $v$ changes — the $v^2$ in the denominator captures that accelerated sensitivity.

When to use it. Use the quotient rule when your function is a fraction where both the numerator and denominator depend on $x$ — $\dfrac{x^2}{\sin x}$, $\dfrac{e^x}{x+1}$, $\dfrac{\ln x}{x^2}$. If only the numerator contains $x$ (denominator is a constant), the constant multiple rule is simpler.

How to use it. A common mnemonic is "low d-high minus high d-low, all over low squared" — where "high" is the numerator and "low" is the denominator.

  1. Identify $u$ (numerator) and $v$ (denominator).
  2. Find $u'$ and $v'$.
  3. Compute $u'v - uv'$ (numerator of the answer).
  4. Divide by $v^2$.
  5. Simplify.
Worked example — $\dfrac{d}{dx}\!\left[\dfrac{x^2}{\sin x}\right]$
$u = x^2,\; u' = 2x \quad v = \sin x,\; v' = \cos x$numerator and denominator
$= \dfrac{2x\sin x - x^2\cos x}{\sin^2 x}$$\frac{u'v - uv'}{v^2}$
$= \dfrac{x(2\sin x - x\cos x)}{\sin^2 x}$factor $x$ from numerator
Worked example — $\dfrac{d}{dx}\!\left[\dfrac{\ln x}{x^2}\right]$
$u = \ln x,\; u' = 1/x \quad v = x^2,\; v' = 2x$identify parts
$= \dfrac{(1/x)x^2 - (\ln x)(2x)}{x^4} = \dfrac{x - 2x\ln x}{x^4}$apply formula; simplify numerator
$= \dfrac{1 - 2\ln x}{x^3}$cancel one factor of $x$

The Chain Rule

What it is. The chain rule handles composite functions — a function plugged inside another function. If $y = f(g(x))$, think of $g(x)$ as the "inner" function and $f$ as the "outer" function:

Chain rule
$$\frac{d}{dx}[f(g(x))] = f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x)$$

Equivalently, with $u = g(x)$: $\;\dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{dy}{du} \cdot \dfrac{du}{dx}$

Why it exists. Suppose $u$ changes at rate $du/dx$ when $x$ changes, and $y$ changes at rate $dy/du$ when $u$ changes. Then the rate at which $y$ changes with respect to $x$ is the product of those rates — like gears: if gear A turns twice as fast as gear B, and gear B turns three times as fast as gear C, then gear A turns six times as fast as gear C. The chain rule is that multiplication of rates.

When to use it. Use the chain rule when you can see a function inside another function: $\sin(x^2)$, $e^{3x}$, $\sqrt{x^2+1}$, $\ln(\cos x)$. The telltale sign is that if you tried to evaluate the function at a number, you would perform two steps — compute the inner part first, then feed it into the outer part.

How to use it.

  1. Identify the outer function $f$ and the inner function $g(x)$.
  2. Differentiate the outer function, leaving the inner function in place: $f'(g(x))$.
  3. Multiply by the derivative of the inner function: $g'(x)$.
  4. Simplify.
Worked example — $\dfrac{d}{dx}[\sin(x^2)]$
outer: $f(u) = \sin u$; inner: $g(x) = x^2$peel apart the composition
$f'(u) = \cos u$; $g'(x) = 2x$differentiate each piece separately
$= \cos(x^2) \cdot 2x = 2x\cos(x^2)$chain rule: outer derivative × inner derivative
Worked example — $\dfrac{d}{dx}[(x^2+1)^3]$
outer: $f(u) = u^3$; inner: $g(x) = x^2 + 1$identify layers
$f'(u) = 3u^2$; $g'(x) = 2x$power rule on each
$= 3(x^2+1)^2 \cdot 2x = 6x(x^2+1)^2$substitute back; multiply
Worked example — $\dfrac{d}{dx}[e^{x^2}]$
outer: $e^u$; inner: $x^2$; inner derivative: $2x$$e^u$ differentiates to itself
$= e^{x^2} \cdot 2x = 2xe^{x^2}$chain rule

Combining the Rules

Real functions often require two or more rules at once. The key is to work from the outside in: identify the outermost operation first, apply its rule, and then handle any inner products, quotients, or compositions as you go.

Worked example — $\dfrac{d}{dx}[x^2 e^{x^2}]$ (product + chain)
Product rule: $u = x^2$, $v = e^{x^2}$outermost structure is a product
$u' = 2x$; $v' = e^{x^2} \cdot 2x$ (chain rule on $v$)chain rule needed for $e^{x^2}$
$= 2x \cdot e^{x^2} + x^2 \cdot 2xe^{x^2} = 2xe^{x^2}(1 + x^2)$$u'v + uv'$; factor

Chapter 5

Implicit Differentiation

Not every curve can be written as $y = f(x)$. A circle like $x^2 + y^2 = 25$ gives two values of $y$ for most $x$; a folium or a lemniscate has even more complex branching. Implicit differentiation lets you find the slope at any point on such curves without solving for $y$ explicitly — which is often impossible. In economics, budget constraints and production functions are typically given implicitly, and implicit differentiation gives marginal rates of substitution without needing an explicit solution.

Diagram — tangent to a circle (implicit curve x²+y²=r²)

Why we need it. The rules in Chapters 3 and 4 work when $y$ is explicitly written as a function of $x$, like $y = x^2 + 3$. But many curves — circles, ellipses, foliums — are described by an equation mixing $x$ and $y$ where isolating $y$ is impossible or produces multiple branches. Implicit differentiation handles all of these without ever solving for $y$.

The key idea. Treat $y$ as a hidden function of $x$. Every time you differentiate a term involving $y$, apply the chain rule: $\dfrac{d}{dx}[y^n] = ny^{n-1}\dfrac{dy}{dx}$. The $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ factor appears because $y$ depends on $x$. After differentiating both sides, collect all $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ terms on one side and solve algebraically.

Step-by-step strategy:

  1. Differentiate both sides of the equation with respect to $x$.
  2. For any $y$ term, attach a $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ factor (chain rule).
  3. Expand or simplify — use product/quotient rules on mixed $xy$ terms.
  4. Move all $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ terms to one side; everything else to the other.
  5. Factor out $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ and divide.
Worked example — circle $x^2 + y^2 = 25$. Find $dy/dx$.
$\dfrac{d}{dx}[x^2] + \dfrac{d}{dx}[y^2] = \dfrac{d}{dx}[25]$differentiate both sides term by term
$2x + 2y\,\dfrac{dy}{dx} = 0$power rule on $x^2$; chain rule on $y^2$ adds $dy/dx$
$\dfrac{dy}{dx} = -\dfrac{x}{y}$isolate $dy/dx$; answer contains both $x$ and $y$
Worked example — $x^3 + y^3 = 6xy$. Find $dy/dx$.
$3x^2 + 3y^2\,\dfrac{dy}{dx} = 6y + 6x\,\dfrac{dy}{dx}$power rule left; product rule on $6xy$ right
$3y^2\,\dfrac{dy}{dx} - 6x\,\dfrac{dy}{dx} = 6y - 3x^2$collect $dy/dx$ terms on left
$\dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{6y - 3x^2}{3y^2 - 6x} = \dfrac{2y - x^2}{y^2 - 2x}$factor and simplify
Worked example — deriving $\dfrac{d}{dx}[\arctan x] = \dfrac{1}{1+x^2}$
Let $y = \arctan x$, so $\tan y = x$rewrite in implicit form
$\sec^2 y \cdot \dfrac{dy}{dx} = 1$differentiate both sides; chain rule on $\tan y$
$\dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{1}{\sec^2 y} = \cos^2 y$isolate $dy/dx$
$= \dfrac{1}{1 + \tan^2 y} = \dfrac{1}{1+x^2}$identity $\sec^2 y = 1 + \tan^2 y$; substitute $\tan y = x$

Chapter 6

Related Rates

Two quantities change over time, linked by a geometric or physical relationship. You know how fast one changes; you need the rate of the other. This is the problem of related rates: a ladder sliding down a wall, water draining from a cone-shaped tank, the shadow of a streetlamp growing as you walk away. In radar tracking, the measured range rate (how fast the distance changes) must be converted to the angular rate (how fast the antenna must rotate) — a classic related-rates problem.

Diagram — ladder sliding down a wall

What they are. Related rates problems ask: given that one quantity changes at a known rate, how fast is a related quantity changing? The two quantities are linked by a geometric or physical formula — differentiate that formula with respect to time $t$, and every variable grows a $d/dt$ factor via the chain rule. Then substitute the known values to find the unknown rate.

Problem-solving strategy:

  1. Draw a diagram and label all quantities that change with time.
  2. Write an equation relating those quantities (area, volume, Pythagorean theorem, etc.).
  3. Differentiate both sides with respect to $t$. Each variable picks up a $d(\,)/dt$ factor via the chain rule.
  4. Substitute the known rates and the known instantaneous values.
  5. Solve for the unknown rate.
Worked example — inflating sphere: $dV/dt = 2\;\text{cm}^3/\text{s}$. Find $dr/dt$ when $r = 3\;\text{cm}$.
Relationship: $V = \dfrac{4}{3}\pi r^3$volume of a sphere
$\dfrac{dV}{dt} = 4\pi r^2 \dfrac{dr}{dt}$differentiate both sides w.r.t. $t$; chain rule on $r^3$
$2 = 4\pi(3)^2 \dfrac{dr}{dt} = 36\pi\,\dfrac{dr}{dt}$substitute known rate and $r = 3$
$\dfrac{dr}{dt} = \dfrac{2}{36\pi} = \dfrac{1}{18\pi}\;\text{cm/s}$solve for unknown rate
Worked example — ladder sliding down a wall. Ladder 10 ft long; base slides out at $2\;\text{ft/s}$. Find how fast the top is sliding down when the base is 6 ft from the wall.
$x^2 + y^2 = 100$, where $x$ = base distance, $y$ = heightPythagorean theorem; ladder length constant
$2x\dfrac{dx}{dt} + 2y\dfrac{dy}{dt} = 0$differentiate w.r.t. $t$
When $x = 6$: $y = \sqrt{100 - 36} = 8$find $y$ at the given instant
$2(6)(2) + 2(8)\dfrac{dy}{dt} = 0 \Rightarrow \dfrac{dy}{dt} = -\dfrac{3}{2}\;\text{ft/s}$substitute; negative means the top is sliding down

Chapter 7

Applications of Derivatives

The derivative answers three of the most important practical questions in mathematics: where is a function increasing or decreasing, where does it reach its maximum or minimum, and when does it change its curvature (concavity)? Optimization is everywhere — engineers minimize material costs subject to constraints, economists maximize utility or profit, pharmacologists find the dose that maximizes therapeutic effect while staying below toxicity. Every such problem reduces to finding where a derivative is zero.

Diagram — critical points: local max, min, and inflection

Linear Approximation & Differentials

The idea. Near $x = a$, the tangent line is the best straight-line approximation to $f$ — and because a line is trivial to evaluate, it lets you estimate $f(x)$ for $x$ close to $a$ without recomputing $f$ itself. This linear approximation (or linearization) is written $L(x)$.

Differentials. Write $dx$ for a small change in $x$. The differential $dy = f'(x)\,dx$ approximates the actual change $\Delta y = f(x + dx) - f(x)$ — the approximation improves as $dx \to 0$.

Linear approximation & differential
$$L(x) = f(a) + f'(a)(x - a) \qquad dy = f'(x)\,dx \approx \Delta y$$
Worked example — approximate $\sqrt{4.1}$ by linearizing at $a = 4$
$f(x) = \sqrt{x}$, $f'(x) = \dfrac{1}{2\sqrt{x}}$; $f(4) = 2$, $f'(4) = \dfrac14$pick $a = 4$ since it's a nearby perfect square
$L(4.1) = 2 + \dfrac14(4.1 - 4) = 2 + 0.025 = 2.025$tangent-line approximation
Actual value: $\sqrt{4.1} \approx 2.0248$accurate to within $0.0002$ here

Maxima & Minima

Critical points occur where $f'(x) = 0$ or $f'$ is undefined — the only places a local max or min can occur. A critical point is a local maximum if $f''(x) < 0$ and a local minimum if $f''(x) > 0$ (second derivative test).

Absolute extrema on a closed interval. The Extreme Value Theorem guarantees that a function continuous on a closed interval $[a,b]$ attains an absolute maximum and an absolute minimum somewhere on that interval. To find them, use the candidates test: evaluate $f$ at every critical point inside $(a,b)$ and at both endpoints — the largest value is the absolute maximum, the smallest is the absolute minimum.

Second derivative test
$$f'(c) = 0 \text{ and } f''(c) > 0 \Rightarrow \text{local min} \qquad f''(c) < 0 \Rightarrow \text{local max}$$
Worked example — critical points of $f(x) = x^3 - 3x$
$f'(x) = 3x^2 - 3 = 0 \Rightarrow x^2 = 1 \Rightarrow x = \pm 1$set derivative to zero
$f''(x) = 6x$; $f''(1) = 6 > 0$ (min); $f''(-1) = -6 < 0$ (max)second derivative test
Worked example — absolute extrema of $f(x) = x^3 - 3x$ on $[-2, 3]$
Critical points $x = \pm 1$ (found above); interval endpoints $x = -2, 3$candidates test needs both
$f(-2) = -2$, $f(-1) = 2$, $f(1) = -2$, $f(3) = 18$evaluate $f$ at every candidate
Absolute max $= 18$ at $x = 3$; absolute min $= -2$ (tied at $x=-2$ and $x=1$)largest / smallest of the candidate values

The Mean Value Theorem

The Mean Value Theorem (MVT) states that if $f$ is continuous on $[a,b]$ and differentiable on $(a,b)$, there exists $c \in (a,b)$ such that $f'(c) = \dfrac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}$ — somewhere on the interval, the instantaneous rate of change equals the average rate of change. When $f(a) = f(b)$, this is Rolle's Theorem: there must be a point where $f'(c) = 0$.

Worked example — MVT for $f(x) = x^2$ on $[1,3]$
$\dfrac{f(3)-f(1)}{3-1} = \dfrac{9-1}{2} = 4$average rate of change
$f'(c) = 2c = 4 \Rightarrow c = 2$MVT guarantees $c = 2 \in (1,3)$

Curve Sketching — Increasing/Decreasing & Concavity

The first derivative test. Where $f'(x) > 0$, $f$ is increasing; where $f'(x) < 0$, $f$ is decreasing. At a critical point, if $f'$ changes from positive to negative, that point is a local maximum; if it changes negative to positive, it's a local minimum. If $f'$ doesn't change sign, the point is neither — this makes the first derivative test work even when the second derivative test is inconclusive (e.g. $f''(c) = 0$).

Concavity and inflection points. Where $f''(x) > 0$, the graph is concave up (bends upward, like a cup); where $f''(x) < 0$, it's concave down (bends downward, like a dome). A point where concavity switches — and $f''$ is zero or undefined there — is an inflection point.

Sketching a curve
$$f'(x) > 0 \Rightarrow \text{increasing} \qquad f'(x) < 0 \Rightarrow \text{decreasing}$$ $$f''(x) > 0 \Rightarrow \text{concave up} \qquad f''(x) < 0 \Rightarrow \text{concave down}$$
Worked example — sketch $f(x) = x^3 - 3x^2$
$f'(x) = 3x^2 - 6x = 3x(x-2)$; critical points at $x = 0, 2$set $f' = 0$
$f' > 0$ on $(-\infty,0)$; $f' < 0$ on $(0,2)$; $f' > 0$ on $(2,\infty)$sign chart from the factored form
$x=0$ is a local max ($f'$ goes $+\to-$); $x=2$ is a local min ($f'$ goes $-\to+$)first derivative test
$f''(x) = 6x - 6 = 0 \Rightarrow x = 1$; $f'' < 0$ for $x<1$, $f'' > 0$ for $x>1$concavity switches at $x=1$
$x = 1$ is an inflection pointconcavity changes sign there

Applied Optimization

Problem-solving strategy:

  1. Draw a diagram and label the variables.
  2. Identify the quantity to optimize (the objective) and write a formula for it.
  3. Find a constraint equation relating the variables, and use it to rewrite the objective as a function of a single variable.
  4. Determine the domain from the problem's physical restrictions.
  5. Find critical points of the objective function and apply the candidates test (or first/second derivative test) to identify the max or min.
Worked example — maximize the area enclosed by 200 ft of fencing
Let $x, y$ be the rectangle's sides; constraint $2x + 2y = 200 \Rightarrow y = 100 - x$fencing forms the perimeter
$A(x) = x(100-x) = 100x - x^2$, for $0 < x < 100$objective in terms of one variable
$A'(x) = 100 - 2x = 0 \Rightarrow x = 50$; $A''(x) = -2 < 0$, so this is a maxcritical point + second derivative test
$y = 100 - 50 = 50$: a $50\times 50$ square, area $2500\;\text{ft}^2$solve for the other dimension

Newton's Method

The idea. To find a root of $f$, start with a guess $x_0$ and repeatedly follow the tangent line down to the $x$-axis — each tangent line's $x$-intercept becomes the next, better guess. Newton's Method converges very fast (roughly doubling the number of correct digits each step) whenever the initial guess is reasonably close to the root and $f'$ isn't near zero there.

Newton's method — iteration formula
$$x_{n+1} = x_n - \frac{f(x_n)}{f'(x_n)}$$
Worked example — approximate $\sqrt{2}$ as a root of $f(x) = x^2 - 2$, starting at $x_0 = 1$
$f'(x) = 2x$; $x_1 = 1 - \dfrac{1^2 - 2}{2(1)} = 1 + \dfrac12 = 1.5$first iteration
$x_2 = 1.5 - \dfrac{1.5^2 - 2}{2(1.5)} = 1.5 - \dfrac{0.25}{3} \approx 1.41667$second iteration
$x_3 \approx 1.41667 - \dfrac{1.41667^2 - 2}{2(1.41667)} \approx 1.41421$third iteration — already matches $\sqrt2 = 1.41421\ldots$ to 5 digits

Chapter 8

The Definite Integral

Area under a curve is one of the oldest problems in mathematics — ancient Egyptians approximated areas using rectangles. The definite integral formalizes this: it is the exact limit of ever-finer rectangle approximations. In physics, the area under a force-displacement curve is work; under a velocity-time curve is displacement; under a power-time curve is energy. In probability, it gives the probability that a random variable falls in an interval. The integral is the mathematical meaning of "accumulated total."

Diagram — Riemann sum rectangles approach the exact area

The definite integral $\displaystyle\int_a^b f(x)\,dx$ represents the signed area between $y = f(x)$ and the $x$-axis on $[a,b]$. It is defined as a limit of Riemann sums: $\displaystyle\int_a^b f(x)\,dx = \lim_{n\to\infty} \sum_{i=1}^n f(x_i^*)\Delta x$.

Properties of definite integrals
$$\int_a^b [f \pm g]\,dx = \int_a^b f\,dx \pm \int_a^b g\,dx$$ $$\int_a^b cf\,dx = c\int_a^b f\,dx \qquad \int_a^a f\,dx = 0 \qquad \int_a^b f\,dx = -\int_b^a f\,dx$$
Worked example — $\displaystyle\int_0^1 x^2\,dx$
$= \left[\dfrac{x^3}{3}\right]_0^1 = \dfrac{1}{3} - 0 = \dfrac{1}{3}$antiderivative, then evaluate at bounds

Chapter 9

Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

The Fundamental Theorem reveals that differentiation and integration are inverse operations — one of the most profound discoveries in mathematics. Without it, computing definite integrals would require building and summing rectangles every time. With it, $\int_a^b f(x)\,dx = F(b) - F(a)$ reduces a limit of infinite sums to two function evaluations. This connection between the derivative (local) and the integral (global) is what makes calculus the right language for physics: forces integrate to impulse, accelerations integrate to velocity, velocities integrate to displacement.

Diagram — antiderivative F(x) with ∫ₐᵇf = F(b) − F(a)

Why it's fundamental. The FTC reveals that differentiation and integration are inverse operations — they undo each other. Before the FTC, computing areas required painful Riemann-sum limits. After the FTC, you just need an antiderivative and two plugged-in values. It is arguably the most important theorem in all of calculus.

Part 1 — integration creates a differentiable function. Define a new function by integrating $f$ from a fixed point $a$ up to a variable upper limit $x$: $$F(x) = \int_a^x f(t)\,dt$$ Then $F'(x) = f(x)$. In other words, the derivative of the accumulated area function gives back the original integrand. Every continuous function is guaranteed an antiderivative.

Part 2 — evaluating definite integrals. If $F$ is any antiderivative of $f$ (meaning $F'(x) = f(x)$), then:

FTC Part 2
$$\int_a^b f(x)\,dx = F(b) - F(a) \equiv \Big[F(x)\Big]_a^b$$

The constant of integration $+C$ cancels when you subtract $F(a)$ from $F(b)$, so you can ignore it for definite integrals.

Antiderivative table (basic)
$$\int x^n\,dx = \frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1} + C \quad (n \ne -1) \qquad \int \frac{1}{x}\,dx = \ln|x| + C$$ $$\int e^x\,dx = e^x + C \qquad \int \sin x\,dx = -\cos x + C \qquad \int \cos x\,dx = \sin x + C$$ $$\int \sec^2 x\,dx = \tan x + C \qquad \int \frac{1}{1+x^2}\,dx = \arctan x + C$$
Worked example — $\displaystyle\int_1^4 \sqrt{x}\,dx$
$\sqrt{x} = x^{1/2}$; antiderivative is $\dfrac{x^{3/2}}{3/2} = \dfrac{2}{3}x^{3/2}$reverse power rule (add 1 to exponent, divide)
$= \left[\dfrac{2}{3}x^{3/2}\right]_1^4 = \dfrac{2}{3}(4^{3/2}) - \dfrac{2}{3}(1^{3/2})$FTC Part 2: evaluate at bounds and subtract
$= \dfrac{2}{3}(8) - \dfrac{2}{3}(1) = \dfrac{16}{3} - \dfrac{2}{3} = \dfrac{14}{3}$$4^{3/2} = (\sqrt{4})^3 = 8$
Worked example — FTC Part 1: $\dfrac{d}{dx}\displaystyle\int_0^x \sin(t^2)\,dt$
$= \sin(x^2)$FTC Part 1: derivative of the accumulation function is the integrand with $t$ replaced by $x$
Worked example — $\displaystyle\int_1^e \frac{1}{x}\,dx$
Antiderivative of $\dfrac{1}{x}$ is $\ln|x|$from the antiderivative table
$= \big[\ln x\big]_1^e = \ln e - \ln 1 = 1 - 0 = 1$$\ln e = 1$; $\ln 1 = 0$

Chapter 10

Integration Techniques

Most real-world integrals don't match the basic power-rule antiderivatives — a substitution, an integration by parts, or a partial-fraction decomposition is needed first. These techniques transform unfamiliar integrands into recognizable forms. Substitution ($u = g(x)$) is the inverse of the chain rule; integration by parts is the inverse of the product rule. In signal processing, convolution integrals require these techniques; in probability theory, expectation values are often integrals that need reduction before evaluation.

Diagram — u-substitution transforms the integrand

Substitution ($u$-sub): let $u = g(x)$, $du = g'(x)\,dx$. Rewrite the integral in terms of $u$, integrate, substitute back.

Integration by parts: $\displaystyle\int u\,dv = uv - \int v\,du$. Choose $u$ to be the part that simplifies when differentiated (LIATE: logarithm, inverse trig, algebraic, trig, exponential).

Integration by parts
$$\int u\,dv = uv - \int v\,du$$
Worked example — $\displaystyle\int 2x(x^2+1)^3\,dx$ (substitution)
$u = x^2+1$, $du = 2x\,dx$$u$-sub
$\displaystyle\int u^3\,du = \dfrac{u^4}{4} + C = \dfrac{(x^2+1)^4}{4} + C$integrate then back-substitute
Worked example — $\displaystyle\int x^2 e^x\,dx$ (integration by parts, twice)
First: $u = x^2$, $dv = e^x\,dx$ → $x^2 e^x - 2\displaystyle\int xe^x\,dx$by parts
Second: $\displaystyle\int xe^x\,dx = xe^x - e^x$by parts again
$= x^2 e^x - 2(xe^x - e^x) + C = e^x(x^2 - 2x + 2) + C$combine
Worked example — $\displaystyle\int_1^{\infty} x^{-2}\,dx$ (improper integral)
$= \lim_{b\to\infty}\left[-x^{-1}\right]_1^b = \lim_{b\to\infty}\!\left(-\dfrac{1}{b} + 1\right) = 1$use a limit for the infinite bound

Chapter 11

Applications of Integration

Integration computes more than area: it computes volumes of revolution, arc lengths of curves, the work done by a variable force, the average value of a function, and the center of mass of a solid. The same definite integral $\int_a^b$ appears in fluid pressure calculations (force from hydrostatic pressure on a dam face), in population models (total organisms born over a period), and in economics (consumer and producer surplus in supply-demand models).

Diagram — area between two curves

Integration measures accumulated quantities: area, volume, arc length, work, probability. The common thread is always the same — slice the region into thin pieces, write the approximate contribution of one slice, then integrate to add infinitely many slices exactly.

Area Between Two Curves

If $f(x) \geq g(x)$ on $[a,b]$, a thin vertical slice at position $x$ has height $f(x) - g(x)$ and width $dx$. Integrating those slices gives the total area:

Area between curves
$$A = \int_a^b \bigl[f(x) - g(x)\bigr]\,dx$$

Finding the limits. If the bounds aren't given, set $f(x) = g(x)$ and solve for the intersection points — those become $a$ and $b$. If the curves cross within the interval, split the integral at each crossing and always subtract the lower curve from the upper.

Worked example — area between $y = x$ and $y = x^2$
Set $x = x^2 \Rightarrow x(x-1) = 0 \Rightarrow x = 0,\, 1$find intersection points for limits
$x \geq x^2$ on $[0,1]$ (check: at $x=0.5$, $0.5 > 0.25$)identify top curve
$\displaystyle\int_0^1 (x - x^2)\,dx = \left[\dfrac{x^2}{2} - \dfrac{x^3}{3}\right]_0^1 = \dfrac{1}{2} - \dfrac{1}{3} = \dfrac{1}{6}$integrate the difference

Volumes of Revolution — Disk & Washer Methods

Rotate a region around the $x$-axis. Each thin slice sweeps out a disk (or, if there's a hole, a washer). The radius of the disk at position $x$ is $f(x)$, giving area $\pi[f(x)]^2$, so:

Disk method (solid)
$$V = \pi \int_a^b [f(x)]^2\,dx$$
Washer method (region between two curves)
$$V = \pi \int_a^b \bigl([f(x)]^2 - [g(x)]^2\bigr)\,dx$$
Worked example — volume from rotating $y = \sqrt{x}$ on $[0,4]$ around the $x$-axis
$V = \pi \displaystyle\int_0^4 (\sqrt{x})^2\,dx = \pi\int_0^4 x\,dx$disk method; $(\sqrt x)^2 = x$
$= \pi\left[\dfrac{x^2}{2}\right]_0^4 = \pi \cdot 8 = 8\pi$FTC Part 2

Average Value of a Function

The average value of $f$ on $[a,b]$ is the total accumulated value divided by the length of the interval:

Average value
$$f_{\text{avg}} = \frac{1}{b-a}\int_a^b f(x)\,dx$$
Worked example — average of $f(x) = x^2$ on $[0, 3]$
$f_{\text{avg}} = \dfrac{1}{3-0}\displaystyle\int_0^3 x^2\,dx = \dfrac{1}{3}\left[\dfrac{x^3}{3}\right]_0^3$average value formula
$= \dfrac{1}{3} \cdot \dfrac{27}{3} = \dfrac{1}{3} \cdot 9 = 3$evaluate; the average height of the parabola over $[0,3]$ is 3

Chapter 12

Sequences, Series & Sigma Notation

Many functions that have no elementary closed form can still be computed to any desired precision using power series. The Taylor series $\sum a_n(x-c)^n$ expresses functions like $e^x$, $\sin x$, and $\ln(1+x)$ as infinite polynomials — which is exactly how calculators and computers evaluate them. Convergence tests determine whether a series settles to a finite sum or diverges to infinity. In numerical analysis and signal compression, series are truncated to a finite number of terms that approximate the function to the required accuracy.

Diagram — partial sums converging to the series limit S

A sequence is an ordered list of numbers $\{a_n\}$. A series is a sum of sequence terms. Sigma notation compactly represents sums: $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{N} a_n = a_1 + a_2 + \cdots + a_N$.

A geometric series $\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} ar^n$ converges to $\dfrac{a}{1-r}$ when $|r| < 1$. A power series represents functions as infinite sums; the Taylor series is the most important example.

Common summation formulas
$$\sum_{n=1}^{N} n = \frac{N(N+1)}{2} \qquad \sum_{n=1}^{N} n^2 = \frac{N(N+1)(2N+1)}{6}$$ $$\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} ar^n = \frac{a}{1-r} \quad (|r| < 1)$$
Worked example — $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{4} n$
$= 1 + 2 + 3 + 4 = 10$or: $N(N+1)/2 = 4(5)/2 = 10$
Worked example — $\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \left(\tfrac{1}{2}\right)^n$
$a = 1$, $r = 1/2$; sum $= \dfrac{1}{1 - 1/2} = 2$geometric series formula; $|r| = 1/2 < 1$
