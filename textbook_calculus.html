Chapter 1

Limits & Continuity

The limit $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L$ means $f(x)$ approaches $L$ as $x$ approaches $a$ (but does not need to equal $f(a)$). A function is continuous at $a$ if the limit equals the function value: $\lim_{x\to a} f(x) = f(a)$.

Limit at infinity: if the degree of the numerator is less than the degree of the denominator, the limit as $x \to \infty$ is $0$. L'Hôpital's Rule: if a limit yields $0/0$ or $\pm\infty/\infty$, differentiate numerator and denominator separately.

Key limit facts
$$\lim_{x\to a} x^n = a^n \qquad \lim_{x\to 0} \frac{\sin x}{x} = 1 \qquad \lim_{x\to\infty} \frac{1}{x} = 0$$
Worked example — $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to 2} x^2$
$= 2^2 = 4$polynomial; substitute directly
Worked example — $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty} \dfrac{1}{x}$
As $x \to \infty$, $1/x \to 0$numerator degree 0 < denominator degree 1
Chapter 2

The Derivative — Definition

The derivative of $f$ at $x$ is the instantaneous rate of change: $$f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h}$$ Geometrically, $f'(a)$ is the slope of the tangent line to $y = f(x)$ at $x = a$. The tangent line at $(a, f(a))$ is $y - f(a) = f'(a)(x - a)$.

Worked example — tangent line to $y = x^2$ at $(1, 1)$
$f'(x) = 2x$; $f'(1) = 2$ (slope)power rule
$y - 1 = 2(x - 1) \Rightarrow y = 2x - 1$point-slope form
Chapter 3

Basic Differentiation Rules

These rules let us differentiate without using the limit definition directly.

Power, constant, and sum rules
$$\frac{d}{dx}[x^n] = nx^{n-1} \qquad \frac{d}{dx}[c] = 0 \qquad \frac{d}{dx}[cf] = cf'$$ $$\frac{d}{dx}[f \pm g] = f' \pm g'$$
Derivatives of basic functions
$$\frac{d}{dx}[e^x] = e^x \qquad \frac{d}{dx}[\ln x] = \frac{1}{x}$$ $$\frac{d}{dx}[\sin x] = \cos x \qquad \frac{d}{dx}[\cos x] = -\sin x$$
Worked example — differentiate $3x^2 + 2x - 5$
$= 6x + 2$power rule on each term; constant vanishes
Worked example — differentiate $x^{1/2}$
$\dfrac{d}{dx}[x^{1/2}] = \tfrac{1}{2} x^{-1/2} = \dfrac{1}{2\sqrt{x}}$power rule; rewrite negative exponent
Chapter 4

Product, Quotient & Chain Rules

Product rule
$$\frac{d}{dx}[uv] = u'v + uv'$$
Quotient rule
$$\frac{d}{dx}\!\left[\frac{u}{v}\right] = \frac{u'v - uv'}{v^2}$$
Chain rule
$$\frac{d}{dx}[f(g(x))] = f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x)$$
Worked example — differentiate $x^2 \sin x$ (product rule)
$u = x^2, \; v = \sin x$; $u' = 2x, \; v' = \cos x$identify parts
$= 2x\sin x + x^2\cos x$$u'v + uv'$
Worked example — differentiate $(x^2+1)^3$ (chain rule)
$= 3(x^2+1)^2 \cdot 2x = 6x(x^2+1)^2$outer rule × inner derivative
Chapter 5

Implicit Differentiation

When $y$ is defined implicitly by an equation in $x$ and $y$, differentiate both sides with respect to $x$, applying the chain rule to any $y$ term (writing $dy/dx$), then solve for $dy/dx$.

Worked example — $x^2 + y^2 = 25$. Find $dy/dx$.
$2x + 2y\,\dfrac{dy}{dx} = 0$differentiate both sides w.r.t. $x$
$\dfrac{dy}{dx} = -\dfrac{x}{y}$solve for $dy/dx$
Worked example — $\arctan(x)$. Find $dy/dx$ via implicit differentiation.
$\tan y = x \Rightarrow \sec^2 y \cdot \dfrac{dy}{dx} = 1$differentiate implicitly
$\dfrac{dy}{dx} = \cos^2 y = \dfrac{1}{1+x^2}$$\sec^2 y = 1 + \tan^2 y = 1 + x^2$
Chapter 6

Related Rates

Related rates problems find how fast one quantity changes given the rate of change of a related quantity. Differentiate the geometric or physical relationship with respect to time $t$, then substitute the known values.

Worked example — sphere: $V = \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3$, $dV/dt = 2$. Find $dr/dt$ when $r = 3$.
$\dfrac{dV}{dt} = 4\pi r^2 \dfrac{dr}{dt}$differentiate w.r.t. $t$; chain rule
$2 = 4\pi(9)\dfrac{dr}{dt} \Rightarrow \dfrac{dr}{dt} = \dfrac{2}{36\pi} = \dfrac{1}{18\pi}$substitute $r = 3$; solve
Chapter 7

Applications of Derivatives

Critical points occur where $f'(x) = 0$ or $f'$ is undefined. A critical point is a local maximum if $f''(x) < 0$ and a local minimum if $f''(x) > 0$ (second derivative test).

The Mean Value Theorem (MVT) states that if $f$ is continuous on $[a,b]$ and differentiable on $(a,b)$, there exists $c \in (a,b)$ such that $f'(c) = \dfrac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}$.

Second derivative test
$$f'(c) = 0 \text{ and } f''(c) > 0 \Rightarrow \text{local min} \qquad f''(c) < 0 \Rightarrow \text{local max}$$
Worked example — critical points of $f(x) = x^3 - 3x$
$f'(x) = 3x^2 - 3 = 0 \Rightarrow x^2 = 1 \Rightarrow x = \pm 1$set derivative to zero
$f''(x) = 6x$; $f''(1) = 6 > 0$ (min); $f''(-1) = -6 < 0$ (max)second derivative test
Worked example — MVT for $f(x) = x^2$ on $[1,3]$
$\dfrac{f(3)-f(1)}{3-1} = \dfrac{9-1}{2} = 4$average rate of change
$f'(c) = 2c = 4 \Rightarrow c = 2$MVT guarantees $c = 2 \in (1,3)$
Chapter 8

The Definite Integral

The definite integral $\displaystyle\int_a^b f(x)\,dx$ represents the signed area between $y = f(x)$ and the $x$-axis on $[a,b]$. It is defined as a limit of Riemann sums: $\displaystyle\int_a^b f(x)\,dx = \lim_{n\to\infty} \sum_{i=1}^n f(x_i^*)\Delta x$.

Properties of definite integrals
$$\int_a^b [f \pm g]\,dx = \int_a^b f\,dx \pm \int_a^b g\,dx$$ $$\int_a^b cf\,dx = c\int_a^b f\,dx \qquad \int_a^a f\,dx = 0 \qquad \int_a^b f\,dx = -\int_b^a f\,dx$$
Worked example — $\displaystyle\int_0^1 x^2\,dx$
$= \left[\dfrac{x^3}{3}\right]_0^1 = \dfrac{1}{3} - 0 = \dfrac{1}{3}$antiderivative, then evaluate at bounds
Chapter 9

Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

The FTC has two parts:

Part 1: If $F(x) = \displaystyle\int_a^x f(t)\,dt$, then $F'(x) = f(x)$. Every continuous function has an antiderivative.

Part 2: If $F$ is any antiderivative of $f$, then $\displaystyle\int_a^b f(x)\,dx = F(b) - F(a)$.

Antiderivative table (basic)
$$\int x^n\,dx = \frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1} + C \quad (n \ne -1) \qquad \int \frac{1}{x}\,dx = \ln|x| + C$$ $$\int e^x\,dx = e^x + C \qquad \int \sin x\,dx = -\cos x + C \qquad \int \cos x\,dx = \sin x + C$$
Worked example — $\displaystyle\int_1^e \frac{1}{x}\,dx$
$= \big[\ln x\big]_1^e = \ln e - \ln 1 = 1 - 0 = 1$antiderivative of $1/x$ is $\ln x$
Chapter 10

Integration Techniques

Substitution ($u$-sub): let $u = g(x)$, $du = g'(x)\,dx$. Rewrite the integral in terms of $u$, integrate, substitute back.

Integration by parts: $\displaystyle\int u\,dv = uv - \int v\,du$. Choose $u$ to be the part that simplifies when differentiated (LIATE: logarithm, inverse trig, algebraic, trig, exponential).

Integration by parts
$$\int u\,dv = uv - \int v\,du$$
Worked example — $\displaystyle\int 2x(x^2+1)^3\,dx$ (substitution)
$u = x^2+1$, $du = 2x\,dx$$u$-sub
$\displaystyle\int u^3\,du = \dfrac{u^4}{4} + C = \dfrac{(x^2+1)^4}{4} + C$integrate then back-substitute
Worked example — $\displaystyle\int x^2 e^x\,dx$ (integration by parts, twice)
First: $u = x^2$, $dv = e^x\,dx$ → $x^2 e^x - 2\displaystyle\int xe^x\,dx$by parts
Second: $\displaystyle\int xe^x\,dx = xe^x - e^x$by parts again
$= x^2 e^x - 2(xe^x - e^x) + C = e^x(x^2 - 2x + 2) + C$combine
Worked example — $\displaystyle\int_1^{\infty} x^{-2}\,dx$ (improper integral)
$= \lim_{b\to\infty}\left[-x^{-1}\right]_1^b = \lim_{b\to\infty}\!\left(-\dfrac{1}{b} + 1\right) = 1$use a limit for the infinite bound
Chapter 11

Applications of Integration

Area between curves: if $f(x) \geq g(x)$ on $[a,b]$, the area between them is $\displaystyle\int_a^b [f(x) - g(x)]\,dx$.

Volumes by disks: rotating $y = f(x)$ around the $x$-axis gives $V = \pi\displaystyle\int_a^b [f(x)]^2\,dx$.

Worked example — area between $y = x$ and $y = x^2$ on $[0,1]$
$y = x \geq y = x^2$ on $[0,1]$$x \geq x^2$ for $0 \leq x \leq 1$
$\displaystyle\int_0^1 (x - x^2)\,dx = \left[\dfrac{x^2}{2} - \dfrac{x^3}{3}\right]_0^1 = \dfrac{1}{2} - \dfrac{1}{3} = \dfrac{1}{6}$integrate the difference
Worked example — $\displaystyle\int_0^{\pi} \sin x\,dx$ (area under sine)
$= \big[-\cos x\big]_0^{\pi} = (-\cos\pi) - (-\cos 0) = 1 + 1 = 2$antiderivative of $\sin x$ is $-\cos x$
Chapter 12

Sequences, Series & Sigma Notation

A sequence is an ordered list of numbers $\{a_n\}$. A series is a sum of sequence terms. Sigma notation compactly represents sums: $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{N} a_n = a_1 + a_2 + \cdots + a_N$.

A geometric series $\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} ar^n$ converges to $\dfrac{a}{1-r}$ when $|r| < 1$. A power series represents functions as infinite sums; the Taylor series is the most important example.

Common summation formulas
$$\sum_{n=1}^{N} n = \frac{N(N+1)}{2} \qquad \sum_{n=1}^{N} n^2 = \frac{N(N+1)(2N+1)}{6}$$ $$\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} ar^n = \frac{a}{1-r} \quad (|r| < 1)$$
Worked example — $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{4} n$
$= 1 + 2 + 3 + 4 = 10$or: $N(N+1)/2 = 4(5)/2 = 10$
Worked example — $\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \left(\tfrac{1}{2}\right)^n$
$a = 1$, $r = 1/2$; sum $= \dfrac{1}{1 - 1/2} = 2$geometric series formula; $|r| = 1/2 < 1$
