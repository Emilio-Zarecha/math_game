Chapter 1

Angles & Measurement

Angles are the language of rotation and oscillation — from the spin of a gyroscope to the phase of an AC current. Two units of measurement exist because they serve different masters: degrees are convenient for human intuition, while radians are natural for mathematics. The derivative of $\sin x$ equals $\cos x$ only when $x$ is measured in radians; every formula in calculus and physics that involves circular motion silently assumes radians. Mastering the conversion between the two is the entry ticket to all that follows.

An angle is formed by two rays sharing a common endpoint (vertex). Angles measured in degrees (°) use 360° for a full rotation. Radians measure angles by arc length on the unit circle: a full rotation is $2\pi$ radians.

Chapter 2

The Unit Circle

The right-triangle definitions of sine and cosine break down outside the range 0°–90° — a triangle can't have an angle of 200°. The unit circle fixes this by turning every angle into a point on a circle: as $\theta$ grows beyond 90°, the point simply keeps moving around the circle, and its coordinates continue to define $(\cos\theta, \sin\theta)$ for any angle. This is the move that transforms trig from a tool for solving triangles into the mathematics of waves, oscillations, and anything that repeats — the heartbeat of signal processing and wave mechanics alike.

The unit circle has radius 1 and center at the origin. For any angle $\theta$, the point $(\cos\theta, \sin\theta)$ lies on the circle. Key exact values are listed below. Angles in the remaining quadrants follow by symmetry using reference angles.

Exact values at key angles

$\theta = 0$ (0°): $\cos\theta = 1$, $\sin\theta = 0$
$\theta = \pi/6$ (30°): $\cos\theta = \dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$, $\sin\theta = \dfrac{1}{2}$
$\theta = \pi/4$ (45°): $\cos\theta = \dfrac{\sqrt{2}}{2}$, $\sin\theta = \dfrac{\sqrt{2}}{2}$
$\theta = \pi/3$ (60°): $\cos\theta = \dfrac{1}{2}$, $\sin\theta = \dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$
$\theta = \pi/2$ (90°): $\cos\theta = 0$, $\sin\theta = 1$

Chapter 3

Right Triangle Trigonometry

Right triangles appear wherever a measurement is indirect. You can't walk to the top of a cliff to measure its height — but you can stand at a known horizontal distance and measure the angle of elevation. SOH-CAH-TOA converts one measurable quantity (an angle plus one side) into any other. This is the engine behind navigation, architecture, GPS triangulation, and the pixel-to-world coordinate transforms used in computer vision. Mastering these six ratios is mastering the geometry of indirect measurement.

For an acute angle $\theta$ in a right triangle, label the sides relative to $\theta$: opposite (opp), adjacent (adj), and hypotenuse (hyp). The six ratios give the six trig functions (SOH-CAH-TOA).

Diagram — right triangle
SOH-CAH-TOA and reciprocals
$$\sin\theta = \frac{\text{opp}}{\text{hyp}} \qquad \cos\theta = \frac{\text{adj}}{\text{hyp}} \qquad \tan\theta = \frac{\text{opp}}{\text{adj}}$$ $$\csc\theta = \frac{\text{hyp}}{\text{opp}} \qquad \sec\theta = \frac{\text{hyp}}{\text{adj}} \qquad \cot\theta = \frac{\text{adj}}{\text{opp}}$$
Worked example — right triangle: opp = 3, hyp = 5. Find all six trig ratios.
adj $= \sqrt{5^2 - 3^2} = 4$Pythagorean theorem
$\sin\theta = 3/5, \;\cos\theta = 4/5, \;\tan\theta = 3/4$SOH-CAH-TOA
$\csc\theta = 5/3, \;\sec\theta = 5/4, \;\cot\theta = 4/3$reciprocals
Worked example — hyp = 10, angle = 30°. Find the adjacent side.

Chapter 4

Trig Functions of Any Angle

A pendulum swings past vertical. An electric generator rotates full circles. A sound wave oscillates through positive and negative values. None of these are confined to the 0°–90° window of the right-triangle world. This chapter generalizes sine, cosine, and tangent to every angle by anchoring them to a point on the terminal side rather than to a triangle side — allowing the same functions to model any rotation or oscillation, positive or negative, large or small. It is also where the sign patterns (ASTC) that determine whether a value is positive or negative in each quadrant become essential.

For any angle $\theta$ in standard position, let $(x, y)$ be a point on its terminal side at distance $r = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}$ from the origin. Then $\sin\theta = y/r$, $\cos\theta = x/r$, $\tan\theta = y/x$.

The reference angle $\hat\theta$ is the acute angle between the terminal side and the $x$-axis. Signs of trig functions by quadrant: Q1 all +, Q2 sin +, Q3 tan +, Q4 cos +. Mnemonic: All Students Take Calculus.

Signs by quadrant (ASTC)
$$\text{Q1: all} + \quad \text{Q2: sin} + \quad \text{Q3: tan} + \quad \text{Q4: cos} +$$
ASTC — signs by quadrant (point (x, y) at distance r from origin)
Worked example — find $\sin\theta$ given $\cos\theta = -4/5$ in Q2
$\sin^2\theta = 1 - \cos^2\theta = 1 - 16/25 = 9/25$Pythagorean identity
$\sin\theta = +3/5$positive in Q2

Chapter 5

Graphs of Trig Functions

Graphing a trig function reveals something the formula obscures: sine and cosine are the shape of every wave in nature. Sound is a sum of sine waves. Light is an electromagnetic sinusoid. AC electricity oscillates at 60 Hz — a sine wave with a fixed amplitude and period. The parameters $A$, $B$, $C$, $D$ give engineers precise control over that wave: how tall, how fast, shifted how far, centered where. Fourier's theorem tells us that any periodic signal — however complex — can be decomposed into sums of these simple curves, which makes this chapter the mathematical foundation of audio engineering, radio transmission, and image compression.

For $y = A\sin(Bx - C) + D$ (or cosine): amplitude $= |A|$, period $= 2\pi / |B|$, phase shift $= C/B$ (right if positive), vertical shift $= D$. The tangent function has period $\pi$.

Key parameters for $y = A\sin(Bx - C) + D$
$$\text{Amplitude} = |A| \qquad \text{Period} = \frac{2\pi}{|B|} \qquad \text{Phase shift} = \frac{C}{B}$$
Worked example — $y = 3\sin(2x)$
Amplitude $= |3| = 3$$A = 3$
Period $= 2\pi/2 = \pi$$B = 2$
Worked example — phase shift of $y = \sin(2x - \pi/2)$
$B = 2$, $C = \pi/2$identify parameters
Phase shift $= C/B = \dfrac{\pi/2}{2} = \dfrac{\pi}{4}$ to the rightpositive → shift right

What each parameter does to the graph of $y = A\sin(Bx - C) + D$: Amplitude $|A|$ controls height — the graph peaks at $A$ and troughs at $-A$. Period $2\pi/|B|$ controls how often the wave repeats — larger $|B|$ compresses the graph horizontally. Phase shift $C/B$ slides the graph left or right — positive means right, negative means left. Vertical shift $D$ moves the midline up ($D>0$) or down ($D<0$).

To sketch one cycle: draw the midline $y = D$; mark amplitude $|A|$ above and below it; find the starting $x$-value from the phase shift; divide the period into four equal parts to locate the maximum, zero crossings, and minimum.

Chapter 6

Inverse Trig Functions

Trig functions answer "given the angle, what is the ratio?" Inverse trig functions answer the reverse: "given the ratio, what is the angle?" That second question is the one that arises in practice. A ramp rises 3 meters over 10 meters of horizontal run — at what angle? A satellite subtends a certain ratio at the horizon — what is its elevation? A robot arm needs its end effector at a specific point — what joint angles produce that? The restricted domains are the price of invertibility: a function with no repeated outputs can be reversed; trig functions repeat endlessly, so we carve out a single clean interval per function.

The inverse trig functions return the angle that produces a given ratio. Because trig functions are not one-to-one, their inverses are defined on restricted domains: $\arcsin$ on $[-\pi/2, \pi/2]$; $\arccos$ on $[0, \pi]$; $\arctan$ on $(-\pi/2, \pi/2)$.

Inverse functions and their ranges
$$y = \arcsin x \iff \sin y = x, \quad y \in [-\tfrac{\pi}{2}, \tfrac{\pi}{2}]$$ $$y = \arccos x \iff \cos y = x, \quad y \in [0, \pi]$$ $$y = \arctan x \iff \tan y = x, \quad y \in (-\tfrac{\pi}{2}, \tfrac{\pi}{2})$$
Graphs of arcsin, arccos, arctan with restricted ranges
— arcsin  [−π/2, π/2] — arccos  [0, π] — arctan  (−π/2, π/2)
Worked example — $\arcsin(1/2)$
We need $y$ such that $\sin y = 1/2$ and $y \in [-\pi/2, \pi/2]$restricted domain
$y = 30° = \pi/6$$\sin(30°) = 1/2$
Worked example — $\arctan(1)$
$\tan(45°) = 1$, so $\arctan(1) = 45° = \pi/4$$\pi/4 \in (-\pi/2, \pi/2)$

Chapter 7

Fundamental Trig Identities

An identity is a rewriting rule: it lets you replace one form of a trig expression with another that is algebraically equivalent but easier to work with. This matters because calculus often needs a specific form — integrating $\sin^2\theta$ directly is impossible, but rewriting it as $\frac{1 - \cos 2\theta}{2}$ makes it trivial. In signal processing and electrical engineering, identities convert between phase-shifted representations of a wave. You don't need to memorize identities by rote; the Pythagorean identity follows immediately from the unit circle equation $x^2 + y^2 = 1$, and the others cascade from it. The skill to develop is recognizing which identity to reach for when an expression looks stuck.

Identities are equations true for all values of $\theta$ in their domains. The three Pythagorean identities follow from $x^2 + y^2 = r^2$. Reciprocal and quotient identities relate the six functions to sine and cosine.

Pythagorean identities
$$\sin^2\theta + \cos^2\theta = 1$$ $$1 + \tan^2\theta = \sec^2\theta$$ $$1 + \cot^2\theta = \csc^2\theta$$
Reciprocal and quotient identities
$$\csc\theta = \frac{1}{\sin\theta} \quad \sec\theta = \frac{1}{\cos\theta} \quad \cot\theta = \frac{1}{\tan\theta}$$ $$\tan\theta = \frac{\sin\theta}{\cos\theta} \qquad \cot\theta = \frac{\cos\theta}{\sin\theta}$$
sin²θ + cos²θ = 1 — geometric proof on the unit circle
Worked example — prove $\sec^2\theta - \tan^2\theta = 1$
$\sec^2\theta - \tan^2\theta = \dfrac{1}{\cos^2\theta} - \dfrac{\sin^2\theta}{\cos^2\theta}$reciprocal and quotient identities
$= \dfrac{1 - \sin^2\theta}{\cos^2\theta} = \dfrac{\cos^2\theta}{\cos^2\theta} = 1$Pythagorean identity in numerator
Worked example — $\csc(30°)$
$\csc(30°) = \dfrac{1}{\sin(30°)} = \dfrac{1}{1/2} = 2$reciprocal identity

Chapter 8

Sum & Difference Formulas

Most real angles aren't in any standard table: you need 75° or 165°, not just 30° or 45°. Sum and difference formulas let you split any angle into a combination of known angles and compute exact values. More deeply, these formulas are the algebraic skeleton of Fourier analysis — the theory that says any periodic signal can be decomposed into a sum of sines and cosines with different phases. The interference patterns of light and sound, the beating effect when two musical notes are close in frequency, the way noise-canceling headphones work — all of these are consequences of the addition formulas applied to waves.

These formulas expand $\sin(A \pm B)$ and $\cos(A \pm B)$ in terms of singleangle functions. They allow exact evaluation at non-standard angles like 15° and 75°.

Sum and difference formulas
$$\sin(A \pm B) = \sin A \cos B \pm \cos A \sin B$$ $$\cos(A \pm B) = \cos A \cos B \mp \sin A \sin B$$ $$\tan(A \pm B) = \frac{\tan A \pm \tan B}{1 \mp \tan A \tan B}$$
Diagram — angle addition on the unit circle
Worked example — $\sin(15°) = \sin(45° - 30°)$
$= \sin(45°)\cos(30°) - \cos(45°)\sin(30°)$difference formula
$= \dfrac{\sqrt{2}}{2} \cdot \dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2} - \dfrac{\sqrt{2}}{2} \cdot \dfrac{1}{2} = \dfrac{\sqrt{6}}{4} - \dfrac{\sqrt{2}}{4}$substitute exact values
$= \dfrac{\sqrt{6} - \sqrt{2}}{4}$combine fractions
Worked example — $\cos(90°)$ via $\cos(45° + 45°)$
$= \cos(45°)\cos(45°) - \sin(45°)\sin(45°) = \tfrac{1}{2} - \tfrac{1}{2} = 0$cosine sum formula

Chapter 9

Double & Half-Angle Formulas

These are not new formulas so much as the most useful specializations of the sum formulas — useful enough to deserve their own chapter. In calculus, integrals of $\sin^2\theta$ and $\cos^2\theta$ arise whenever you compute average power, work done by a varying force, or the expected value of a sinusoidal signal. The power-reduction identities ($\sin^2\theta = \frac{1-\cos 2\theta}{2}$) make these integrals tractable. Half-angle formulas appear in numerical algorithms that need to bisect angles iteratively, and in the derivation of exact trigonometric values for angles like $\pi/8$ and $\pi/12$ that don't appear in standard tables.

Double-angle formulas are special cases of the sum formulas with $A = B = \theta$. Half-angle formulas use the double-angle identities solved for $\sin(\theta/2)$ or $\cos(\theta/2)$; the sign $\pm$ depends on the quadrant.

Double-angle formulas
$$\sin(2\theta) = 2\sin\theta\cos\theta$$ $$\cos(2\theta) = \cos^2\theta - \sin^2\theta = 1 - 2\sin^2\theta = 2\cos^2\theta - 1$$ $$\tan(2\theta) = \frac{2\tan\theta}{1 - \tan^2\theta}$$
Half-angle formulas
$$\sin\!\left(\frac{\theta}{2}\right) = \pm\sqrt{\frac{1 - \cos\theta}{2}} \qquad \cos\!\left(\frac{\theta}{2}\right) = \pm\sqrt{\frac{1 + \cos\theta}{2}}$$
Diagram — θ vs 2θ on the unit circle
Worked example — $\sin(2\theta)$ with $\theta = 30°$
$\sin(60°) = 2\sin(30°)\cos(30°) = 2 \cdot \tfrac{1}{2} \cdot \tfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2} = \dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$double-angle formula
Worked example — $\sin(\pi/8)$ using half-angle
$\sin\!\left(\dfrac{\pi}{8}\right) = \sqrt{\dfrac{1 - \cos(\pi/4)}{2}} = \sqrt{\dfrac{1 - \sqrt{2}/2}{2}}$half-angle; $\pi/8$ is in Q1, sign $+$
$= \sqrt{\dfrac{2 - \sqrt{2}}{4}} = \dfrac{\sqrt{2 - \sqrt{2}}}{2}$simplify

Chapter 10

Solving Trig Equations

Real problems ask "when?" not just "what?" — when does the tide reach a given height? When does the voltage in an AC circuit cross zero? When do two waves interfere destructively? These are trig equations, and because trig functions repeat, each equation typically has infinitely many solutions. The technique of finding a principal value and then adding period multiples captures all of them. This is also the mental model behind phase alignment in signal processing: finding every moment a periodic signal hits a target value is exactly solving a trig equation.

Trig equations are solved by isolating the trig function, finding a reference angle, then listing all solutions in the given interval using the unit circle. For equations with multiple angles (like $\sin(3x) = 0$), find the angle first, then divide.

Worked example — solve $2\cos x - \sqrt{3} = 0$ on $[0, 2\pi)$
$\cos x = \dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$isolate cosine
$x = \dfrac{\pi}{6}$ or $x = \dfrac{11\pi}{6}$cosine is positive in Q1 and Q4
Worked example — solve $2\sin^2 x - \sin x - 1 = 0$ on $[0, 2\pi)$
$(2\sin x + 1)(\sin x - 1) = 0$factor the quadratic
$\sin x = -\tfrac{1}{2}$ → $x = \tfrac{7\pi}{6}, \tfrac{11\pi}{6}$negative in Q3 and Q4
$\sin x = 1$ → $x = \tfrac{\pi}{2}$top of unit circle

General strategy: isolate the trig function, find the principal value using inverse trig, then use reference angles and quadrant signs to list all solutions in $[0, 2\pi)$, then add period multiples ($+\, 2\pi k$ for sin/cos, $+\,\pi k$ for tan) for the general solution.

When identities help: if the equation mixes different trig functions (e.g. sin and cos together), try a Pythagorean or quotient identity to rewrite everything in terms of one function before solving.

Worked example — find the general solution to $\tan(2x) = \sqrt{3}$
Reference angle: $\arctan(\sqrt{3}) = \pi/3$$\tan(\pi/3) = \sqrt{3}$
Tangent is positive in Q1 and Q3, so $2x = \pi/3 + n\pi$period of tangent is $\pi$; add $n\pi$ for all solutions
$x = \dfrac{\pi}{6} + \dfrac{n\pi}{2}$ for any integer $n$divide both sides by 2

Chapter 11

Law of Sines

Most triangles in the real world are not right triangles. Surveyors triangulating irregular land, navigators fixing position by measuring angles to two landmarks, and engineers calculating cable tensions in non-orthogonal structures all need to work with arbitrary triangles. The Law of Sines is the key tool when you know two angles and a side (AAS/ASA), extending the indirect-measurement power of SOH-CAH-TOA to any triangle. It is also the principle behind trilateration — the geometric technique that GPS and cell-tower positioning systems use to compute a location from angle or distance ratios.

For any triangle with sides $a, b, c$ opposite to angles $A, B, C$:

Law of Sines
$$\frac{a}{\sin A} = \frac{b}{\sin B} = \frac{c}{\sin C}$$
Diagram — triangle with labeled sides and opposite angles

Use the Law of Sines when you know: AAS, ASA, or SSA (two angles and one side, or two sides and a non-included angle). The SSA case can have 0, 1, or 2 solutions — the ambiguous case.

Worked example — given $a = 7$, $A = 45°$, $B = 60°$, find $b$
$\dfrac{b}{\sin(60°)} = \dfrac{7}{\sin(45°)}$set up proportion
$b = \dfrac{7\sin(60°)}{\sin(45°)} = \dfrac{7 \cdot \sqrt{3}/2}{\sqrt{2}/2} = \dfrac{7\sqrt{3}}{\sqrt{2}} = \dfrac{7\sqrt{6}}{2}$solve for $b$

Ambiguous case (SSA): when given sides $a$, $b$ and angle $A$ (the angle opposite $a$), compare $a$ to $b\sin A$: if $a < b\sin A$ → no triangle exists; if $a = b\sin A$ → exactly one right triangle; if $b\sin A < a < b$ → two distinct triangles; if $a \ge b$ → exactly one triangle.

Worked example — ambiguous case: $A = 30°$, $a = 6$, $b = 10$
$b\sin A = 10\sin(30°) = 10 \cdot 0.5 = 5$compute the threshold
$b\sin A = 5 < a = 6 < b = 10$, so there are two trianglesSSA ambiguous case condition
$\sin B = \dfrac{b\sin A}{a} = \dfrac{10\sin(30°)}{6} = \dfrac{5}{6}$Law of Sines to find $B$
$B_1 = \arcsin(5/6) \approx 56.4°$, $B_2 = 180° - 56.4° = 123.6°$two supplementary angles share the same sine
Triangle 1: $C_1 = 180° - 30° - 56.4° = 93.6°$ ✓angles sum to 180°; positive so valid
Triangle 2: $C_2 = 180° - 30° - 123.6° = 26.4°$ ✓also positive, so second triangle is valid

Chapter 12

Law of Cosines

The Law of Cosines solves the triangle cases the Law of Sines cannot handle cleanly: two sides and the included angle (SAS), or all three sides and you need an angle (SSS). It shows up in robotics when computing the joint angles that place a robot arm at a desired end-effector position, in 3D graphics when finding the angle between two vectors (dot product is a compact form of this law), and in structural engineering when analyzing forces in trusses where no angles are conveniently right angles.

Use the Law of Cosines when you know SAS (two sides and the included angle) or SSS (all three sides). It generalizes the Pythagorean theorem.

Law of Cosines
$$c^2 = a^2 + b^2 - 2ab\cos C$$ $$\cos C = \frac{a^2 + b^2 - c^2}{2ab}$$

The formula is the Pythagorean theorem plus a correction term, $-2ab\cos C$. Picture sides $a$ and $b$ as two arms that pivot at vertex $C$. When $C = 90°$, $\cos 90° = 0$ and the term disappears entirely — you get $c^2 = a^2 + b^2$, the Pythagorean theorem. A right angle means the arms are perfectly perpendicular, so no correction is needed.

When $C < 90°$ (acute), $\cos C$ is positive, so we subtract a positive number. The arms tilt toward each other, bringing the opposite tips closer together and making $c$ shorter than the right-angle case. When $C > 90°$ (obtuse), $\cos C$ is negative, so subtracting a negative value adds to $c^2$. The arms tilt away from each other, pushing the tips farther apart and making $c$ longer. Try it in the explorer below: slide $C$ through $90°$ and watch $c$ shrink, hit its minimum at $90°$ relative to the Pythagorean baseline, then grow.

The $2ab$ factor scales the correction by both side lengths because a longer arm moves its tip farther for the same angular tilt. You can also read $b\cos C$ as the projection of side $b$ onto the direction of side $a$ — the "horizontal reach" that shifts vertex $A$ closer to or farther from vertex $B$.
