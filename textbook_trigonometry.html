Chapter 1

Angles & Measurement

An angle is formed by two rays sharing a common endpoint (vertex). Angles measured in degrees (°) use 360° for a full rotation. Radians measure angles by arc length on the unit circle: a full rotation is $2\pi$ radians.

Degree–Radian conversion
$$\text{radians} = \text{degrees} \times \frac{\pi}{180} \qquad \text{degrees} = \text{radians} \times \frac{180}{\pi}$$
Arc length and sector area
$$s = r\theta \qquad A = \tfrac{1}{2}r^2\theta \quad (\theta \text{ in radians})$$
Worked example — convert 135° to radians
$135° \times \dfrac{\pi}{180} = \dfrac{135\pi}{180} = \dfrac{3\pi}{4}$multiply by π/180; simplify
Worked example — arc length with $r = 5$, $\theta = \pi/3$
$s = 5 \cdot \dfrac{\pi}{3} = \dfrac{5\pi}{3}$$s = r\theta$
Chapter 2

The Unit Circle

The unit circle has radius 1 and center at the origin. For any angle $\theta$, the point $(\cos\theta, \sin\theta)$ lies on the circle. Key exact values are listed below. Angles in the remaining quadrants follow by symmetry using reference angles.

Exact values at key angles

$\theta = 0$ (0°): $\cos\theta = 1$, $\sin\theta = 0$
$\theta = \pi/6$ (30°): $\cos\theta = \dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$, $\sin\theta = \dfrac{1}{2}$
$\theta = \pi/4$ (45°): $\cos\theta = \dfrac{\sqrt{2}}{2}$, $\sin\theta = \dfrac{\sqrt{2}}{2}$
$\theta = \pi/3$ (60°): $\cos\theta = \dfrac{1}{2}$, $\sin\theta = \dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$
$\theta = \pi/2$ (90°): $\cos\theta = 0$, $\sin\theta = 1$

Worked example — find the coordinates at 270°
$\cos(270°) = 0, \; \sin(270°) = -1$bottom of unit circle
Chapter 3

Right Triangle Trigonometry

For an acute angle $\theta$ in a right triangle, label the sides relative to $\theta$: opposite (opp), adjacent (adj), and hypotenuse (hyp). The six ratios give the six trig functions (SOH-CAH-TOA).

SOH-CAH-TOA and reciprocals
$$\sin\theta = \frac{\text{opp}}{\text{hyp}} \qquad \cos\theta = \frac{\text{adj}}{\text{hyp}} \qquad \tan\theta = \frac{\text{opp}}{\text{adj}}$$ $$\csc\theta = \frac{\text{hyp}}{\text{opp}} \qquad \sec\theta = \frac{\text{hyp}}{\text{adj}} \qquad \cot\theta = \frac{\text{adj}}{\text{opp}}$$
Worked example — right triangle: opp = 3, hyp = 5. Find all six trig ratios.
adj $= \sqrt{5^2 - 3^2} = 4$Pythagorean theorem
$\sin\theta = 3/5, \;\cos\theta = 4/5, \;\tan\theta = 3/4$SOH-CAH-TOA
$\csc\theta = 5/3, \;\sec\theta = 5/4, \;\cot\theta = 4/3$reciprocals
Worked example — hyp = 10, angle = 30°. Find the adjacent side.
$\text{adj} = 10\cos(30°) = 10 \cdot \dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2} = 5\sqrt{3}$$\cos\theta = \text{adj}/\text{hyp}$
Chapter 4

Trig Functions of Any Angle

For any angle $\theta$ in standard position, let $(x, y)$ be a point on its terminal side at distance $r = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}$ from the origin. Then $\sin\theta = y/r$, $\cos\theta = x/r$, $\tan\theta = y/x$.

The reference angle $\hat\theta$ is the acute angle between the terminal side and the $x$-axis. Signs of trig functions by quadrant: Q1 all +, Q2 sin +, Q3 tan +, Q4 cos +. Mnemonic: All Students Take Calculus.

Signs by quadrant (ASTC)
$$\text{Q1: all} + \quad \text{Q2: sin} + \quad \text{Q3: tan} + \quad \text{Q4: cos} +$$
Worked example — find $\sin\theta$ given $\cos\theta = -4/5$ in Q2
$\sin^2\theta = 1 - \cos^2\theta = 1 - 16/25 = 9/25$Pythagorean identity
$\sin\theta = +3/5$positive in Q2

Graphs of Trig Functions

For $y = A\sin(Bx - C) + D$ (or cosine): amplitude $= |A|$, period $= 2\pi / |B|$, phase shift $= C/B$ (right if positive), vertical shift $= D$. The tangent function has period $\pi$.

Key parameters for $y = A\sin(Bx - C) + D$
$$\text{Amplitude} = |A| \qquad \text{Period} = \frac{2\pi}{|B|} \qquad \text{Phase shift} = \frac{C}{B}$$
Worked example — $y = 3\sin(2x)$
Amplitude $= |3| = 3$$A = 3$
Period $= 2\pi/2 = \pi$$B = 2$
Worked example — phase shift of $y = \sin(2x - \pi/2)$
$B = 2$, $C = \pi/2$identify parameters
Phase shift $= C/B = \dfrac{\pi/2}{2} = \dfrac{\pi}{4}$ to the rightpositive → shift right
Chapter 6

Inverse Trig Functions

The inverse trig functions return the angle that produces a given ratio. Because trig functions are not one-to-one, their inverses are defined on restricted domains: $\arcsin$ on $[-\pi/2, \pi/2]$; $\arccos$ on $[0, \pi]$; $\arctan$ on $(-\pi/2, \pi/2)$.

Inverse functions and their ranges
$$y = \arcsin x \iff \sin y = x, \quad y \in [-\tfrac{\pi}{2}, \tfrac{\pi}{2}]$$ $$y = \arccos x \iff \cos y = x, \quad y \in [0, \pi]$$ $$y = \arctan x \iff \tan y = x, \quad y \in (-\tfrac{\pi}{2}, \tfrac{\pi}{2})$$
Worked example — $\arcsin(1/2)$
We need $y$ such that $\sin y = 1/2$ and $y \in [-\pi/2, \pi/2]$restricted domain
$y = 30° = \pi/6$$\sin(30°) = 1/2$
Worked example — $\arctan(1)$
$\tan(45°) = 1$, so $\arctan(1) = 45° = \pi/4$$\pi/4 \in (-\pi/2, \pi/2)$
Chapter 7

Fundamental Trig Identities

Identities are equations true for all values of $\theta$ in their domains. The three Pythagorean identities follow from $x^2 + y^2 = r^2$. Reciprocal and quotient identities relate the six functions to sine and cosine.

Pythagorean identities
$$\sin^2\theta + \cos^2\theta = 1$$ $$1 + \tan^2\theta = \sec^2\theta$$ $$1 + \cot^2\theta = \csc^2\theta$$
Reciprocal and quotient identities
$$\csc\theta = \frac{1}{\sin\theta} \quad \sec\theta = \frac{1}{\cos\theta} \quad \cot\theta = \frac{1}{\tan\theta}$$ $$\tan\theta = \frac{\sin\theta}{\cos\theta} \qquad \cot\theta = \frac{\cos\theta}{\sin\theta}$$
Worked example — prove $\sec^2\theta - \tan^2\theta = 1$
$\sec^2\theta - \tan^2\theta = \dfrac{1}{\cos^2\theta} - \dfrac{\sin^2\theta}{\cos^2\theta}$reciprocal and quotient identities
$= \dfrac{1 - \sin^2\theta}{\cos^2\theta} = \dfrac{\cos^2\theta}{\cos^2\theta} = 1$Pythagorean identity in numerator
Worked example — $\csc(30°)$
$\csc(30°) = \dfrac{1}{\sin(30°)} = \dfrac{1}{1/2} = 2$reciprocal identity
Chapter 8

Sum & Difference Formulas

These formulas expand $\sin(A \pm B)$ and $\cos(A \pm B)$ in terms of singleangle functions. They allow exact evaluation at non-standard angles like 15° and 75°.

Sum and difference formulas
$$\sin(A \pm B) = \sin A \cos B \pm \cos A \sin B$$ $$\cos(A \pm B) = \cos A \cos B \mp \sin A \sin B$$ $$\tan(A \pm B) = \frac{\tan A \pm \tan B}{1 \mp \tan A \tan B}$$
Worked example — $\sin(15°) = \sin(45° - 30°)$
$= \sin(45°)\cos(30°) - \cos(45°)\sin(30°)$difference formula
$= \dfrac{\sqrt{2}}{2} \cdot \dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2} - \dfrac{\sqrt{2}}{2} \cdot \dfrac{1}{2} = \dfrac{\sqrt{6}}{4} - \dfrac{\sqrt{2}}{4}$substitute exact values
$= \dfrac{\sqrt{6} - \sqrt{2}}{4}$combine fractions
Worked example — $\cos(90°)$ via $\cos(45° + 45°)$
$= \cos(45°)\cos(45°) - \sin(45°)\sin(45°) = \tfrac{1}{2} - \tfrac{1}{2} = 0$cosine sum formula
Chapter 9

Double & Half-Angle Formulas

Double-angle formulas are special cases of the sum formulas with $A = B = \theta$. Half-angle formulas use the double-angle identities solved for $\sin(\theta/2)$ or $\cos(\theta/2)$; the sign $\pm$ depends on the quadrant.

Double-angle formulas
$$\sin(2\theta) = 2\sin\theta\cos\theta$$ $$\cos(2\theta) = \cos^2\theta - \sin^2\theta = 1 - 2\sin^2\theta = 2\cos^2\theta - 1$$ $$\tan(2\theta) = \frac{2\tan\theta}{1 - \tan^2\theta}$$
Half-angle formulas
$$\sin\!\left(\frac{\theta}{2}\right) = \pm\sqrt{\frac{1 - \cos\theta}{2}} \qquad \cos\!\left(\frac{\theta}{2}\right) = \pm\sqrt{\frac{1 + \cos\theta}{2}}$$
Worked example — $\sin(2\theta)$ with $\theta = 30°$
$\sin(60°) = 2\sin(30°)\cos(30°) = 2 \cdot \tfrac{1}{2} \cdot \tfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2} = \dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$double-angle formula
Worked example — $\sin(\pi/8)$ using half-angle
$\sin\!\left(\dfrac{\pi}{8}\right) = \sqrt{\dfrac{1 - \cos(\pi/4)}{2}} = \sqrt{\dfrac{1 - \sqrt{2}/2}{2}}$half-angle; $\pi/8$ is in Q1, sign $+$
$= \sqrt{\dfrac{2 - \sqrt{2}}{4}} = \dfrac{\sqrt{2 - \sqrt{2}}}{2}$simplify
Chapter 10

Solving Trig Equations

Trig equations are solved by isolating the trig function, finding a reference angle, then listing all solutions in the given interval using the unit circle. For equations with multiple angles (like $\sin(3x) = 0$), find the angle first, then divide.

Worked example — solve $2\cos x - \sqrt{3} = 0$ on $[0, 2\pi)$
$\cos x = \dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$isolate cosine
$x = \dfrac{\pi}{6}$ or $x = \dfrac{11\pi}{6}$cosine is positive in Q1 and Q4
Worked example — solve $2\sin^2 x - \sin x - 1 = 0$ on $[0, 2\pi)$
$(2\sin x + 1)(\sin x - 1) = 0$factor the quadratic
$\sin x = -\tfrac{1}{2}$ → $x = \tfrac{7\pi}{6}, \tfrac{11\pi}{6}$negative in Q3 and Q4
$\sin x = 1$ → $x = \tfrac{\pi}{2}$top of unit circle

Law of Sines

For any triangle with sides $a, b, c$ opposite to angles $A, B, C$:

Law of Sines
$$\frac{a}{\sin A} = \frac{b}{\sin B} = \frac{c}{\sin C}$$

Use the Law of Sines when you know: AAS, ASA, or SSA (two angles and one side, or two sides and a non-included angle). The SSA case can have 0, 1, or 2 solutions — the ambiguous case.

Worked example — given $a = 7$, $A = 45°$, $B = 60°$, find $b$
$\dfrac{b}{\sin(60°)} = \dfrac{7}{\sin(45°)}$set up proportion
$b = \dfrac{7\sin(60°)}{\sin(45°)} = \dfrac{7 \cdot \sqrt{3}/2}{\sqrt{2}/2} = \dfrac{7\sqrt{3}}{\sqrt{2}} = \dfrac{7\sqrt{6}}{2}$solve for $b$

Law of Cosines

Use the Law of Cosines when you know SAS (two sides and the included angle) or SSS (all three sides). It generalizes the Pythagorean theorem.

Law of Cosines
$$c^2 = a^2 + b^2 - 2ab\cos C$$ $$\cos C = \frac{a^2 + b^2 - c^2}{2ab}$$
Worked example — $a = 5$, $b = 7$, $C = 60°$. Find $c$.
$c^2 = 25 + 49 - 2(5)(7)\cos(60°) = 74 - 70 \cdot \tfrac{1}{2} = 74 - 35 = 39$Law of Cosines
$c = \sqrt{39}$take the positive square root
Worked example — find angle $C$ when $a=8$, $b=6$, $c=7$
$\cos C = \dfrac{64 + 36 - 49}{2(8)(6)} = \dfrac{51}{96} = \dfrac{17}{32}$rearranged form
$C = \arccos\!\left(\dfrac{17}{32}\right) \approx 57.9°$apply inverse cosine
