Chapter 1

Points, Lines & Angles

A point has no size — it marks a location. A line extends infinitely in both directions through two points. A line segment has two endpoints. A ray starts at a point and extends infinitely in one direction. Two rays sharing a common endpoint form an angle, measured in degrees.

Angle types by measure: acute ($0° < \theta < 90°$), right ($\theta = 90°$), obtuse ($90° < \theta < 180°$), straight ($\theta = 180°$).

Two angles are complementary if they sum to $90°$ and supplementary if they sum to $180°$. Vertical angles are formed by two intersecting lines and are always equal.

Key relationships
$$\angle A + \angle B = 90° \quad \text{(complementary)}$$ $$\angle A + \angle B = 180° \quad \text{(supplementary)}$$ $$\text{Vertical angles are equal: } \angle 1 = \angle 3, \quad \angle 2 = \angle 4$$
Worked example — complement of 35°
$90° - 35° = 55°$complementary angles sum to 90°
Worked example — supplement of 120°
$180° - 120° = 60°$supplementary angles sum to 180°
Worked example — vertical angles at an intersection
if $\angle 1 = 63°$, then $\angle 3 = 63°$vertical angles are equal
$\angle 2 = 180° - 63° = 117°$$\angle 1$ and $\angle 2$ are supplementary

Chapter 2

Parallel Lines & Transversals

Two lines are parallel ($\ell_1 \parallel \ell_2$) if they never intersect. A transversal is a line that crosses two or more other lines, creating eight angles at the two intersection points.

When the two crossed lines are parallel, special angle pairs are equal or supplementary:

Angle relationships with parallel lines cut by a transversal

Corresponding angles — same position at each intersection → equal
Alternate interior angles — between the lines, opposite sides → equal
Alternate exterior angles — outside the lines, opposite sides → equal
Co-interior (same-side interior) angles — between the lines, same side → supplementary (sum = 180°)

Worked example — find the missing angle
Parallel lines cut by a transversal; one angle = $65°$
Alternate interior angle $= 65°$alternate interior angles are equal
Co-interior angle $= 180° - 65° = 115°$co-interior angles are supplementary

Chapter 3

Triangles

A triangle has three sides and three interior angles that always sum to $180°$.

By sides: equilateral (all sides equal, all angles $60°$), isosceles (two equal sides, base angles equal), scalene (no equal sides). By angles: acute (all angles $< 90°$), right (one $90°$ angle), obtuse (one angle $> 90°$).

The exterior angle theorem states that an exterior angle of a triangle equals the sum of the two non-adjacent interior angles. The triangle inequality requires that the sum of any two sides must be greater than the third side.

Key facts
$$\angle A + \angle B + \angle C = 180° \quad \text{(angle sum)}$$ $$\text{exterior angle} = \angle A + \angle B \quad \text{(exterior angle theorem)}$$
Worked example — find the missing angle
$40° + 65° + \theta = 180°$angle sum in a triangle
$\theta = 180° - 105° = 75°$subtract the known angles

The Isosceles Triangle Theorem states that if two sides of a triangle are equal, the angles opposite those sides (the base angles) are also equal. The converse holds too: if two angles are equal, the sides opposite them are equal. This property appears constantly in proofs — the moment you spot two equal sides you can immediately mark two angles as equal, and vice versa.

The Exterior Angle Theorem follows directly from the angle sum rule. An exterior angle and its adjacent interior angle are supplementary (sum to $180°$), and the three interior angles also sum to $180°$. Combining those two facts, the exterior angle must equal the sum of the two non-adjacent interior angles. This gives a quick shortcut: you can find an exterior angle without first solving for all three interior angles.

Worked example — isosceles $\triangle ABC$ with $AB = AC$, $\angle A = 40°$
base angles equal: $\angle B = \angle C$Isosceles Triangle Theorem
$40° + \angle B + \angle C = 180°$angle sum in a triangle
$40° + 2\angle B = 180°$substitute $\angle C = \angle B$
$\angle B = \angle C = 70°$solve: $2\angle B = 140°$
Worked example — exterior angle $= 120°$, one non-adjacent interior $= 45°$
exterior angle $=$ sum of non-adjacent interior anglesExterior Angle Theorem
$120° = 45° + x$substitute the known angle
$x = 75°$subtract $45°$ from both sides

Chapter 4

Congruence & Similarity

Two figures are congruent ($\cong$) if one can be mapped onto the other by rigid motions (rotation, reflection, translation) — same shape and size. Two figures are similar ($\sim$) if one is a scaled version of the other — same shape, proportional sides.

Triangle congruence criteria: SSS (three sides), SAS (two sides and included angle), ASA (two angles and included side), AAS (two angles and non-included side), HL (hypotenuse-leg in right triangles).

Triangle similarity criteria: AA (two angles equal), SSS (all three side ratios equal), SAS (two sides proportional, included angle equal). The ratio of corresponding sides is the scale factor.

Similar triangles — corresponding sides
$$\frac{a_1}{a_2} = \frac{b_1}{b_2} = \frac{c_1}{c_2} = k \quad \text{(scale factor } k\text{)}$$
Worked example — find the missing side
$\triangle ABC \sim \triangle DEF$ with sides $3, 4, 5$ and scale factor $k = 2$$k = 6/3 = 2$
missing side $= 2 \times 5 = 10$multiply each side by $k$

Here is why each congruence criterion works. SSS — three sides uniquely determine a triangle because the triangle inequality locks the third vertex in place. SAS — the included angle pins the two sides together so only one triangle shape is possible. ASA — two angles fix the third (angle sum), and the included side then sets the scale. AAS — two angles determine all three angles; any one side then fixes the size. HL — in a right triangle, knowing the hypotenuse and one leg determines the other leg by the Pythagorean theorem.

SSA does not prove congruence (the "ambiguous case"). Given two sides and a non-included angle, two geometrically different triangles can satisfy those measurements — the free side can swing to reach the opposite base in two positions. This is why SSA is absent from the congruence criteria list.

For similar triangles, the AA criterion is sufficient: if two angles of one triangle match two angles of another, the third angles must also match (since all three sum to $180°$), guaranteeing the same shape with proportional sides.

Worked example — SAS congruence
$AB = DE = 5$, $BC = EF = 8$, $\angle B = \angle E = 60°$given information
$\angle B$ is between $AB$ and $BC$; $\angle E$ is between $DE$ and $EF$verify the angle is included
$\triangle ABC \cong \triangle DEF$ by SAStwo sides and the included angle are equal
Worked example — similar triangle proportion ($\triangle PQR \sim \triangle XYZ$)
$PQ = 6$, $QR = 8$, $XY = 9$; find $YZ$given information
$\dfrac{PQ}{XY} = \dfrac{QR}{YZ}$corresponding sides are proportional
$\dfrac{6}{9} = \dfrac{8}{YZ}$substitute known values
$YZ = \dfrac{8 \times 9}{6} = 12$cross-multiply and solve

Chapter 5

Pythagorean Theorem

In any right triangle, the square of the hypotenuse (the side opposite the right angle) equals the sum of the squares of the two legs. The converse is also true: if $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$, the triangle is a right triangle.

Special right triangles have side ratios you can memorize:

Key formulas
$$a^2 + b^2 = c^2 \quad \text{(Pythagorean theorem, } c \text{ is hypotenuse)}$$ $$\textbf{45-45-90:} \quad \text{legs } a : a : a\sqrt{2} \quad \text{(hyp} = \text{leg} \times \sqrt{2}\text{)}$$ $$\textbf{30-60-90:} \quad \text{sides } a : a\sqrt{3} : 2a \quad \text{(hyp} = 2 \times \text{short leg)}$$
Worked example — find hypotenuse for legs 3, 4
$c^2 = 3^2 + 4^2 = 9 + 16 = 25$Pythagorean theorem
$c = \sqrt{25} = 5$take the square root
Worked example — 45-45-90 with leg = 5
$c^2 = 5^2 + 5^2 = 50$both legs equal
$c = \sqrt{50} = 5\sqrt{2}$or use: hyp $=$ leg $\times \sqrt{2}$
Worked example — 30-60-90 with shorter leg = 4
hypotenuse $= 2 \times 4 = 8$hyp $= 2 \times$ short leg
longer leg $= 4\sqrt{3}$long leg $=$ short leg $\times \sqrt{3}$

Chapter 6

Quadrilaterals & Polygons

A polygon is a closed figure with straight sides. The sum of interior angles of an $n$-gon is $(n-2) \times 180°$. For a regular polygon (all sides and angles equal), each interior angle equals $\dfrac{(n-2) \times 180°}{n}$, and each exterior angle equals $\dfrac{360°}{n}$.

Key quadrilaterals: parallelogram (opposite sides parallel and equal), rectangle (parallelogram with four right angles), rhombus (parallelogram with four equal sides), square (rectangle and rhombus), trapezoid (exactly one pair of parallel sides), kite (two pairs of consecutive equal sides).

Interior angle formulas
$$\text{Sum} = (n-2) \times 180° \qquad \text{(any } n\text{-gon)}$$ $$\text{Each interior angle} = \frac{(n-2) \times 180°}{n} \qquad \text{(regular polygon)}$$ $$\text{Each exterior angle} = \frac{360°}{n} \qquad \text{(regular polygon)}$$
Worked example — interior angle sum of a pentagon ($n = 5$)
$(5-2) \times 180° = 3 \times 180° = 540°$angle sum formula
Worked example — each interior angle of a regular octagon ($n = 8$)
$\dfrac{(8-2) \times 180°}{8} = \dfrac{1080°}{8} = 135°$regular polygon formula
Worked example — find $n$ when each exterior angle $= 24°$
$n = \dfrac{360°}{24°} = 15$each exterior angle $= 360°/n$

Chapter 7

Perimeter & Area

Perimeter is the total length around a figure. Area is the amount of space inside, measured in square units.

Key formulas
$$\text{Rectangle: } P = 2(l+w), \quad A = lw$$ $$\text{Square: } P = 4s, \quad A = s^2$$ $$\text{Triangle: } A = \tfrac{1}{2}bh$$ $$\text{Parallelogram: } A = bh$$ $$\text{Trapezoid: } A = \tfrac{1}{2}(b_1 + b_2)h$$ $$\text{Rhombus/Kite: } A = \tfrac{1}{2}d_1 d_2$$ $$\text{Circle: } C = 2\pi r, \quad A = \pi r^2$$ $$\text{Regular hexagon: } A = \tfrac{3\sqrt{3}}{2}s^2$$ $$\text{Equilateral triangle: } A = \tfrac{\sqrt{3}}{4}s^2$$
Worked example — area of a triangle with $b=10$, $h=6$
$A = \tfrac{1}{2}(10)(6) = 30$triangle area formula
Worked example — circumference of circle with $r=3$
$C = 2\pi(3) = 6\pi \approx 18.85$$C = 2\pi r$

The intuition behind area formulas: a rectangle has area $\text{base} \times \text{height}$ by definition — you are counting rows of unit squares. A triangle is always exactly half a rectangle: enclose any triangle in the smallest rectangle and the triangle fills exactly half of it. A parallelogram is a "sheared" rectangle — tilt a rectangle sideways and the base and perpendicular height stay the same, so area $= bh$ is unchanged.

For composite shapes, decompose the figure into basic shapes (rectangles, triangles, circles), label each piece with its dimensions, compute each area separately, then add areas together or subtract cut-out holes.

Worked example — area of an L-shaped figure
outer rectangle $8 \times 10$; a $3 \times 4$ corner is cut from the top-rightidentify the composite shape
full rectangle area $= 8 \times 10 = 80$$A = lw$
cut-out area $= 3 \times 4 = 12$$A = lw$ for the removed piece
L-shape area $= 80 - 12 = 68$subtract the hole from the whole

Chapter 8

Surface Area & Volume

Volume measures three-dimensional space in cubic units. Surface area is the total area of all faces of a solid.

Volume formulas
$$\text{Rectangular prism (box): } V = lwh$$ $$\text{Cube: } V = s^3$$ $$\text{Cylinder: } V = \pi r^2 h$$ $$\text{Cone: } V = \tfrac{1}{3}\pi r^2 h$$ $$\text{Sphere: } V = \tfrac{4}{3}\pi r^3$$ $$\text{Square pyramid: } V = \tfrac{1}{3}s^2 h$$
Surface area formulas
$$\text{Rectangular prism: } SA = 2(lw + lh + wh)$$ $$\text{Cube: } SA = 6s^2$$ $$\text{Cylinder: } SA = 2\pi r(r + h)$$ $$\text{Sphere: } SA = 4\pi r^2$$ $$\text{Cylinder lateral SA: } A = 2\pi rh$$
Worked example — volume of cylinder with $r=4$, $h=10$
$V = \pi(4)^2(10) = 160\pi$$V = \pi r^2 h$
Worked example — surface area of cylinder with $r=3$, $h=8$
$SA = 2\pi(3)(3+8) = 2\pi(3)(11) = 66\pi$$SA = 2\pi r(r+h)$

Cavalieri's Theorem unifies all prism and cylinder volume formulas as $V = B \times h$, where $B$ is the base area. Every horizontal slice at every height has the same cross-sectional area, so you are simply stacking identical layers. A rectangular prism uses $B = lw$, a triangular prism uses $B = \tfrac{1}{2}bh_{\text{base}}$, and a cylinder uses $B = \pi r^2$ — the single formula $V = Bh$ covers all three.

Volume (cubic units) answers "how much material fills this solid?" — relevant for capacity, weight, or material cost. Surface area (square units) answers "how much wraps the outside?" — relevant for painting, coating, packaging, or heat transfer. Always confirm which quantity the problem is asking for before computing.

Worked example — cylindrical can, diameter $8$ cm, height $12$ cm
$r = 8 \div 2 = 4$ cmradius is half the diameter
$V = \pi r^2 h = \pi (4)^2 (12) = 192\pi \approx 603 \text{ cm}^3$$V = \pi r^2 h$
Lateral $SA = 2\pi r h = 2\pi (4)(12) = 96\pi \approx 301 \text{ cm}^2$lateral surface only (no top or bottom)

Chapter 9

Circles

A circle is the set of all points equidistant from a center. The radius $r$ goes from center to edge; the diameter $d = 2r$ passes through the center. A chord connects two points on the circle. A tangent touches the circle at exactly one point (perpendicular to the radius at that point).

A central angle has its vertex at the center; its measure equals the arc it intercepts. An inscribed angle has its vertex on the circle; its measure is half the intercepted arc.

Arc and sector formulas (angle $\theta$ in radians)
$$\text{Arc length: } s = r\theta$$ $$\text{Sector area: } A = \tfrac{1}{2}r^2\theta$$ $$\text{Inscribed angle} = \tfrac{1}{2} \times \text{central angle (same arc)}$$

Degrees0.0° Radians0 cos θ1.000 sin θ0.000
Worked example — arc length with $r=10$, $\theta = \pi/3$
$s = 10 \cdot \dfrac{\pi}{3} = \dfrac{10\pi}{3}$$s = r\theta$
Worked example — sector area with $r=6$, $\theta = \pi/3$
$A = \tfrac{1}{2}(6)^2\left(\dfrac{\pi}{3}\right) = \tfrac{1}{2}(36)\left(\dfrac{\pi}{3}\right) = 6\pi$$A = \frac{1}{2}r^2\theta$

The Central Angle Theorem: a central angle (vertex at the center) equals its intercepted arc in degrees. An inscribed angle (vertex on the circle) equals half its intercepted arc. Therefore, when a central angle and an inscribed angle both subtend the same arc, the inscribed angle is exactly half the central angle.

Key circle theorems: (1) Angle in a semicircle — any inscribed angle that subtends a diameter equals $90°$, because the central angle is $180°$ and the inscribed angle is half of that. (2) Angles in the same segment — all inscribed angles subtending the same arc are equal. (3) Cyclic quadrilateral — opposite angles of a quadrilateral inscribed in a circle sum to $180°$.

Worked example — central $\angle AOB = 80°$; find inscribed $\angle ACB$ on the same arc
$\angle AOB$ and $\angle ACB$ both subtend arc $AB$identify the shared arc
inscribed angle $= \tfrac{1}{2} \times$ central angleCentral Angle Theorem
$\angle ACB = \tfrac{1}{2} \times 80° = 40°$substitute and compute

Chapter 10

Coordinate Geometry

The coordinate plane (Cartesian plane) locates every point with an ordered pair $(x, y)$. The distance formula between two points comes from the Pythagorean theorem. The midpoint formula finds the point halfway between two endpoints.

Key formulas
$$d = \sqrt{(x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2} \qquad \text{(distance)}$$ $$M = \left(\frac{x_1+x_2}{2},\, \frac{y_1+y_2}{2}\right) \qquad \text{(midpoint)}$$
Worked example — distance from $(0, 0)$ to $(3, 4)$
$d = \sqrt{3^2 + 4^2} = \sqrt{9 + 16} = \sqrt{25} = 5$distance formula
Worked example — midpoint of $(0, 0)$ and $(6, 4)$
$M = \left(\dfrac{0+6}{2},\, \dfrac{0+4}{2}\right) = (3, 2)$midpoint formula
Worked example — distance from $(1, 2)$ to $(7, 10)$
$d = \sqrt{(7-1)^2 + (10-2)^2} = \sqrt{36+64} = \sqrt{100} = 10$subtract coordinates, square, add, root

Chapter 11

Transformations

A transformation maps each point of a figure to a new location. Rigid motions (isometries) preserve shape and size:

Translation: slides every point by a fixed vector $(a, b)$; $(x, y) \mapsto (x+a, y+b)$. Reflection: flips over a line of symmetry; reflecting over the $x$-axis gives $(x, y) \mapsto (x, -y)$; over the $y$-axis gives $(x, y) \mapsto (-x, y)$. Rotation: turns around a center point by an angle $\theta$; a $90°$ counterclockwise rotation about the origin gives $(x, y) \mapsto (-y, x)$.

Dilation is not rigid — it scales the figure by a factor $k$ from a center: $(x, y) \mapsto (kx, ky)$. If $k > 1$ the figure enlarges; if $0 < k < 1$ it shrinks.

Worked example — translate $(3, -1)$ by vector $(2, 5)$
$(3+2,\, -1+5) = (5, 4)$add the vector to each coordinate
Worked example — reflect $(4, 3)$ over the $x$-axis
$(4, -3)$negate the $y$-coordinate

Chapter 12

Law of Sines & Cosines

These laws extend the Pythagorean theorem to any triangle, not just right triangles. Use Law of Sines when you know two angles and a side (AAS/ASA) or two sides and a non-included angle (SSA). Use Law of Cosines when you know two sides and the included angle (SAS) or all three sides (SSS).

Law of Sines
$$\frac{a}{\sin A} = \frac{b}{\sin B} = \frac{c}{\sin C}$$
Law of Cosines
$$c^2 = a^2 + b^2 - 2ab\cos C$$ $$\cos C = \frac{a^2 + b^2 - c^2}{2ab}$$
Worked example (Law of Sines) — find $b$ given $a=10$, $A=90°$, $B=45°$
$\dfrac{b}{\sin 45°} = \dfrac{10}{\sin 90°} = 10$law of sines
$b = 10\sin 45° = 10 \cdot \dfrac{\sqrt{2}}{2} = 5\sqrt{2}$multiply both sides by $\sin 45°$
Worked example (Law of Cosines) — find $c$ given $a=7$, $b=8$, $C=60°$
$c^2 = 49 + 64 - 2(7)(8)\cos 60°$law of cosines
$= 113 - 112 \cdot \tfrac{1}{2} = 113 - 56 = 57$$\cos 60° = \frac{1}{2}$
$c = \sqrt{57}$take the square root
Worked example (Law of Sines) — find angle $B$ given $a=4$, $A=90°$, $b=2$
$\sin B = \dfrac{b \sin A}{a} = \dfrac{2 \cdot 1}{4} = \dfrac{1}{2}$rearrange law of sines
$B = \arcsin\!\left(\tfrac{1}{2}\right) = 30°$inverse sine
