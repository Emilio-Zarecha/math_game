Chapter 1

Whole Numbers & Place Value

Place value determines a digit's worth by its position. From right to left: ones, tens, hundreds, thousands, ten-thousands, … To round a number, look at the digit immediately to the right of the target place: if it is 5 or more, round up; otherwise round down.

Order of operations (PEMDAS) governs all arithmetic expressions: Parentheses → Exponents → Multiplication & Division (left to right) → Addition & Subtraction (left to right).

Basic operations

Addition: $a + b = b + a$ (commutative)
Subtraction: $a - b$ (not commutative)
Multiplication: $a \times b = b \times a$
Division: $a \div b = \dfrac{a}{b}$ ($b \ne 0$)

Worked example — PEMDAS: $3^2 + 4 \times (8-5) \div 6$
$= 3^2 + 4 \times 3 \div 6$parentheses first: $8-5=3$
$= 9 + 4 \times 3 \div 6$exponent: $3^2=9$
$= 9 + 12 \div 6$multiply: $4 \times 3=12$
$= 9 + 2 = 11$divide then add
Worked example — round $4{,}367$ to the nearest ten
ones digit $= 7 \geq 5$, so round uplook one place right of target
$4{,}370$tens digit increases by 1; ones become 0
Chapter 2

Integers & Absolute Value

Integers extend the whole numbers to include negatives: $\ldots, -3, -2, -1, 0, 1, 2, 3, \ldots$ The absolute value $|a|$ gives the distance from $a$ to zero — always non-negative.

Adding integers: same sign → add and keep the sign; different signs → subtract the smaller absolute value from the larger and keep the sign of the larger. Subtracting: $a - b = a + (-b)$ — add the opposite. Multiplying/dividing: same signs → positive result; opposite signs → negative result.

Sign rules for multiplication and division
$$(+)(+) = + \qquad (-)(-)= + \qquad (+)(-) = - \qquad (-)(+) = -$$
Worked example — $-8 + 3$
Different signs: $|{-8}| - |3| = 8 - 3 = 5$subtract smaller from larger
$= -5$keep sign of $-8$ (larger absolute value)
Worked example — $-5 - (-3)$
$= -5 + 3$subtracting a negative = adding a positive
$= -2$$|-5| - |3| = 2$; sign of $-5$
Chapter 3

Fractions

A fraction $\dfrac{a}{b}$ represents $a$ parts of a whole divided into $b$ equal parts ($b \ne 0$). An improper fraction has $a \geq b$; a mixed number combines a whole number and a proper fraction: $2\frac{1}{3} = \dfrac{7}{3}$.

To add or subtract fractions, find the LCD (least common denominator), convert each fraction, then operate on the numerators. To multiply, multiply numerators and denominators separately. To divide, multiply by the reciprocal: $\dfrac{a}{b} \div \dfrac{c}{d} = \dfrac{a}{b} \times \dfrac{d}{c}$.

Key rules
$$\frac{a}{b} + \frac{c}{b} = \frac{a+c}{b} \qquad \text{(same denominator)}$$ $$\frac{a}{b} \times \frac{c}{d} = \frac{ac}{bd} \qquad \frac{a}{b} \div \frac{c}{d} = \frac{a}{b} \times \frac{d}{c}$$
Worked example — $\dfrac{3}{4} + \dfrac{1}{6}$
LCD $= 12$smallest multiple of 4 and 6
$\dfrac{9}{12} + \dfrac{2}{12} = \dfrac{11}{12}$convert then add numerators
Worked example — $\dfrac{2}{3} \div \dfrac{4}{5}$
$= \dfrac{2}{3} \times \dfrac{5}{4} = \dfrac{10}{12} = \dfrac{5}{6}$multiply by the reciprocal
Worked example — add mixed numbers $2\frac{1}{3} + 1\frac{3}{4}$
$\dfrac{7}{3} + \dfrac{7}{4} = \dfrac{28}{12} + \dfrac{21}{12} = \dfrac{49}{12}$convert to improper; LCD = 12
$= 4\dfrac{1}{12}$$49 = 4 \times 12 + 1$
Chapter 4

Decimals

Decimals extend place value to the right of the decimal point: tenths ($0.1$), hundredths ($0.01$), thousandths ($0.001$), … To convert a decimal to a fraction, write the decimal digits over the appropriate power of 10 and simplify. To convert a fraction to a decimal, divide the numerator by the denominator.

A repeating decimal has a block of digits that cycles forever, denoted with a bar: $0.\overline{3} = 0.3333\ldots = \dfrac{1}{3}$. To convert, let $x$ equal the decimal, multiply by a power of 10 to shift one full cycle, subtract the original equation, and solve for $x$.

Worked example — convert $0.75$ to a fraction
$0.75 = \dfrac{75}{100} = \dfrac{3}{4}$two decimal places → denominator 100; simplify by GCF 25
Worked example — convert $\dfrac{5}{4}$ to a decimal
$5 \div 4 = 1.25$$4$ goes into $5$ once; remainder $1 \to 10 \div 4 = 2$ remainder $2 \to 20 \div 4 = 5$
Worked example — convert $0.\overline{3}$ to a fraction
let $x = 0.\overline{3}$; then $10x = 3.\overline{3}$multiply by 10 to shift one cycle
$10x - x = 3 \Rightarrow 9x = 3 \Rightarrow x = \dfrac{1}{3}$subtract; solve
Chapter 5

Ratios & Proportions

A ratio compares two quantities: $a : b$ or $\dfrac{a}{b}$. Simplify ratios by dividing both terms by their GCF. A rate compares quantities with different units (e.g., miles per hour). A unit rate has denominator 1 (e.g., $60$ mph).

A proportion states two ratios are equal: $\dfrac{a}{b} = \dfrac{c}{d}$. Solve by cross-multiplying: $ad = bc$.

Cross-multiplication
$$\frac{a}{b} = \frac{c}{d} \implies ad = bc$$
Worked example — simplify $12:18$
$\text{GCF}(12,18) = 6$largest factor dividing both
$12:18 = 2:3$divide both by 6
Worked example — solve $\dfrac{3}{4} = \dfrac{x}{20}$
$4x = 3 \times 20 = 60$cross-multiply
$x = 15$divide both sides by 4
Worked example — unit rate: 5 apples cost $\$2.50$
$\$2.50 \div 5 = \$0.50$ per appledivide total cost by quantity
$8 \text{ apples} = 8 \times \$0.50 = \$4.00$multiply unit rate by quantity
Chapter 6

Percents

Percent means "per hundred": $p\% = \dfrac{p}{100}$. To find a percent of a number, convert the percent to a decimal and multiply. Percent change compares a change to the original value.

Key formulas
$$\text{Percent of:} \quad p\% \times \text{whole} = \text{part}$$ $$\text{Percent change:} \quad \frac{\text{new} - \text{old}}{\text{old}} \times 100\%$$ $$\text{Discount price:} \quad \text{original} \times (1 - r) \quad \text{where } r = \text{rate as decimal}$$
Worked example — $15\%$ of $240$
$0.15 \times 240 = 36$convert 15% to 0.15; multiply
Worked example — percent increase from $\$40$ to $\$50$
$\dfrac{50 - 40}{40} \times 100\% = \dfrac{10}{40} \times 100\% = 25\%$change ÷ original × 100%
Worked example — $20\%$ off $\$85$
discount $= 0.20 \times 85 = 17$find the discount amount
price $= 85 - 17 = \$68$subtract discount from original
Chapter 7

Exponents & Roots

$a^n$ means $a$ multiplied by itself $n$ times. A negative exponent means the reciprocal: $a^{-n} = \dfrac{1}{a^n}$. The square root $\sqrt{a}$ is the non-negative number whose square is $a$. More generally, $\sqrt[n]{a} = a^{1/n}$.

Exponent rules
$$a^m \cdot a^n = a^{m+n} \qquad \frac{a^m}{a^n} = a^{m-n} \qquad (a^m)^n = a^{mn}$$ $$a^0 = 1 \quad (a \ne 0) \qquad a^{-n} = \frac{1}{a^n}$$
Worked example — $2^8$
$2^8 = 2^4 \times 2^4 = 16 \times 16 = 256$split into two equal halves
Worked example — $2^{-3}$
$2^{-3} = \dfrac{1}{2^3} = \dfrac{1}{8}$negative exponent = reciprocal
Worked example — $\sqrt{169}$
$13^2 = 169$, so $\sqrt{169} = 13$find the number whose square is 169
Chapter 8

Number Theory

A prime number has exactly two factors: 1 and itself. A composite number has more than two factors. Prime factorization writes a composite as a product of primes.

The GCF (greatest common factor) of two numbers is the largest factor they share. The LCM (least common multiple) is the smallest multiple they share. From prime factorizations: GCF uses the lowest power of each common prime; LCM uses the highest power of each prime that appears.

GCF and LCM from prime factorizations
$$\text{GCF: lowest powers of common primes}$$ $$\text{LCM: highest powers of all primes}$$
Worked example — prime factorization of $60$
$60 = 2 \times 30 = 2 \times 2 \times 15 = 2^2 \times 3 \times 5$divide by smallest prime repeatedly
Worked example — GCF and LCM of $24$ and $36$
$24 = 2^3 \times 3$, $\quad 36 = 2^2 \times 3^2$prime factorizations
$\text{GCF} = 2^2 \times 3 = 12$lowest powers of common primes
$\text{LCM} = 2^3 \times 3^2 = 72$highest powers of all primes
Chapter 9

Scientific Notation

Scientific notation writes a number as $a \times 10^n$ where $1 \leq |a| < 10$ and $n$ is an integer. Move the decimal point: each place to the left increases $n$ by 1; each place to the right decreases $n$ by 1.

To multiply in scientific notation, multiply the coefficients and add the exponents. To divide, divide the coefficients and subtract the exponents. Adjust if the result's coefficient is not between 1 and 10.

Arithmetic in scientific notation
$$(a \times 10^m)(b \times 10^n) = ab \times 10^{m+n}$$ $$\frac{a \times 10^m}{b \times 10^n} = \frac{a}{b} \times 10^{m-n}$$
Worked example — write $45{,}000$ in scientific notation
$45{,}000 = 4.5 \times 10^4$move decimal 4 places left; exponent $= 4$
Worked example — $(3 \times 10^4) \times (2 \times 10^3)$
$= (3 \times 2) \times 10^{4+3} = 6 \times 10^7$multiply coefficients; add exponents
Worked example — write $0.00052$ in scientific notation
$= 5.2 \times 10^{-4}$move decimal 4 places right; exponent $= -4$
Chapter 10

Factorials & Counting

The factorial $n!$ is the product of all positive integers up to $n$: $n! = n \times (n-1) \times \cdots \times 2 \times 1$, with $0! = 1$. Factorials count permutations — ordered arrangements.

The number of ways to arrange $r$ items chosen from $n$ (order matters) is $P(n,r) = \dfrac{n!}{(n-r)!}$. The number of ways to choose $r$ from $n$ (order does not matter) is $\dbinom{n}{r} = \dfrac{n!}{r!(n-r)!}$.

Key formulas
$$n! = n \times (n-1) \times \cdots \times 1, \qquad 0! = 1$$ $$P(n,r) = \frac{n!}{(n-r)!} \qquad \binom{n}{r} = \frac{n!}{r!(n-r)!}$$
Worked example — $5!$
$5! = 5 \times 4 \times 3 \times 2 \times 1 = 120$multiply all integers from 1 to 5
Worked example — $\dfrac{6!}{4!}$
$= \dfrac{6 \times 5 \times 4!}{4!} = 6 \times 5 = 30$cancel the $4!$ in numerator and denominator
Worked example — $4! + 3!$
$24 + 6 = 30$$4!=24$, $3!=6$; add
