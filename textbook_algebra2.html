Chapter 1

Complex Numbers

When the quadratic formula yields a negative discriminant, real numbers aren't enough. Complex numbers were invented so that every polynomial has a solution — the Fundamental Theorem of Algebra guarantees exactly $n$ roots in $\mathbb{C}$ for any degree-$n$ polynomial. In electrical engineering they model AC circuits: resistance and reactance combine into a single complex impedance $Z = R + jX$, and the phase angle between voltage and current is the argument of $Z$.

Diagram — complex plane

The imaginary unit $i = \sqrt{-1}$, so $i^2 = -1$. A complex number is $a + bi$ where $a$ (real part) and $b$ (imaginary part) are real numbers. Complex numbers are added component-wise, multiplied by distributing (and using $i^2 = -1$), and divided by multiplying by the complex conjugate $\overline{a+bi} = a - bi$.

Key operations
$$(a+bi)(c+di) = (ac-bd) + (ad+bc)i$$ $$\frac{a+bi}{c+di} = \frac{(a+bi)(c-di)}{c^2+d^2}$$
Worked example — $\sqrt{-9}$
$\sqrt{-9} = \sqrt{9} \cdot \sqrt{-1} = 3i$factor out $-1$; $\sqrt{-1} = i$
Worked example — divide $(3+2i) \div (1-i)$
$= \dfrac{(3+2i)(1+i)}{(1-i)(1+i)} = \dfrac{3+3i+2i+2i^2}{1+1} = \dfrac{1+5i}{2}$multiply by conjugate; $i^2 = -1$
$= \dfrac{1}{2} + \dfrac{5}{2}i$write in $a + bi$ form

Chapter 2

Quadratic Equations & Functions

The parabola is the universal shape of any phenomenon with a maximum or minimum: a thrown ball's peak height, a business's profit curve, a suspension bridge cable. The vertex reveals the optimum. The discriminant $\Delta = b^2 - 4ac$ tells you immediately how many real solutions exist — negative means the parabola never crosses the $x$-axis, which in a profit model means the product always makes money or always loses it.

Diagram — three discriminant cases

A quadratic function $f(x) = ax^2 + bx + c$ has a parabola as its graph. The vertex is at $\left(-\dfrac{b}{2a},\, f\!\left(-\dfrac{b}{2a}\right)\right)$, and the axis of symmetry is $x = -\dfrac{b}{2a}$.

Vertex form: $f(x) = a(x-h)^2 + k$ where $(h,k)$ is the vertex. The discriminant $\Delta = b^2 - 4ac$ determines the nature of the roots: $\Delta > 0$ → two real roots; $\Delta = 0$ → one real root; $\Delta < 0$ → two complex roots.

Quadratic formula and discriminant
$$x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a} \qquad \Delta = b^2 - 4ac$$
Worked example — complete the square for $x^2 + 6x + 5 = 0$
$(x+3)^2 - 9 + 5 = 0 \Rightarrow (x+3)^2 = 4$add and subtract $(b/2)^2 = 9$
$x + 3 = \pm 2 \Rightarrow x = -1$ or $x = -5$take square root of both sides

Graphically, the discriminant reveals the parabola's relationship to the $x$-axis: $\Delta > 0$ means the parabola crosses the $x$-axis at two distinct points; $\Delta = 0$ means it is tangent to the $x$-axis (touches at exactly one point — the vertex); $\Delta < 0$ means the parabola lies entirely above or below the $x$-axis with no real intercepts.

Vertex form $f(x) = a(x-h)^2 + k$ lets you read off the vertex $(h, k)$ and direction of opening at a glance: opens upward when $a > 0$ (minimum at vertex) and downward when $a < 0$ (maximum at vertex). Converting to vertex form by completing the square also makes it easy to solve the equation by taking square roots directly.

Worked example — vertex and axis of symmetry for $f(x) = 2x^2 - 8x + 3$
$h = -\dfrac{b}{2a} = -\dfrac{-8}{2(2)} = 2$axis of symmetry formula
$f(2) = 2(4) - 8(2) + 3 = 8 - 16 + 3 = -5$evaluate $f$ at $h$ to find $k$
Vertex $= (2, -5)$; axis of symmetry: $x = 2$write the results
Worked example — nature of roots for $x^2 + 4x + 5 = 0$
$\Delta = b^2 - 4ac = (4)^2 - 4(1)(5) = 16 - 20 = -4$$a = 1,\ b = 4,\ c = 5$
$\Delta < 0$ → two complex (non-real) roots; no $x$-interceptsparabola lies entirely above the $x$-axis

Chapter 3

Polynomial Functions

Every smooth process with a finite number of peaks, valleys, and crossings can be modeled by a polynomial of appropriate degree. The Fundamental Theorem guarantees all roots exist in $\mathbb{C}$; the Rational Root Theorem narrows the search for rational ones; synthetic division checks candidates efficiently. End behavior — controlled entirely by the leading term — tells you what happens at the extremes without any computation.

Diagram — end behavior patterns

A polynomial of degree $n$ has at most $n$ real roots and at most $n-1$ turning points. The Fundamental Theorem of Algebra guarantees exactly $n$ roots in $\mathbb{C}$.

Rational Root Theorem: if $p/q$ (in lowest terms) is a rational root of $a_n x^n + \cdots + a_0$, then $p | a_0$ and $q | a_n$. The Remainder Theorem says the remainder of $f(x) \div (x-c)$ equals $f(c)$.

End behavior is determined by the leading term $a_n x^n$. When $n$ is even: both ends of the graph point in the same direction — up ($y \to +\infty$) if $a_n > 0$, down ($y \to -\infty$) if $a_n < 0$. When $n$ is odd: the ends go in opposite directions — down-left and up-right if $a_n > 0$; up-left and down-right if $a_n < 0$. A degree-$n$ polynomial has at most $n - 1$ turning points.

The Factor Theorem is a direct consequence of the Remainder Theorem: $(x - c)$ is a factor of $f(x)$ if and only if $f(c) = 0$. This turns root-finding into a factoring problem — once you find a root $c$ (e.g., by testing rational root candidates), divide out $(x - c)$ and work with the reduced polynomial.

Binomial theorem & Remainder/Factor Theorem
$$(a+b)^n = \sum_{k=0}^n \binom{n}{k} a^{n-k} b^k$$ $$\text{Remainder Theorem: remainder of } f(x) \div (x-c) \text{ equals } f(c)$$ $$\text{Factor Theorem: } (x-c) \mid f(x) \iff f(c) = 0$$
Worked example — Remainder Theorem: $f(x) = x^3 - 2x + 1$ at $x = 2$
$f(2) = 8 - 4 + 1 = 5$the remainder when $f(x) \div (x-2)$ is $f(2) = 5$
Worked example — end behavior of $f(x) = -2x^4 + 3x^2 - x + 1$
Degree $n = 4$ (even); leading coefficient $a = -2 < 0$identify leading term
Both ends point down: $f(x) \to -\infty$ as $x \to \pm\infty$even degree, negative leading coefficient
At most $n - 1 = 3$ turning pointsdegree-4 polynomial
Worked example — rational roots of $f(x) = 2x^3 - 3x^2 - 8x + 12$
Candidates $p/q$: $p \mid 12$, $q \mid 2$ → $\pm 1, \pm 2, \pm 3, \pm 4, \pm 6, \pm 12, \pm\tfrac{1}{2}, \pm\tfrac{3}{2}$Rational Root Theorem
Test $x = 2$: $2(8) - 3(4) - 8(2) + 12 = 16 - 12 - 16 + 12 = 0$ ✓$f(2) = 0$ so $(x-2)$ is a factor
$f(x) = (x-2)(2x^2 + x - 6) = (x-2)(2x-3)(x+2)$polynomial division; then factor the quadratic
Roots: $x = 2,\quad x = \tfrac{3}{2},\quad x = -2$set each factor to zero

Chapter 4

Rational Expressions & Equations

Rational functions describe rates that depend on variables: concentration, efficiency, gearing ratio. A chemist mixing solutions, an engineer designing feedback, a pharmacist modeling drug elimination — all use rational expressions. The asymptotes (vertical at domain restrictions, horizontal from end behavior) show the physical limits: what concentration can never be reached, what speed is theoretically impossible.

Diagram — y = 1/x with asymptotes

A rational expression is a quotient of polynomials. Simplify by factoring numerator and denominator, then canceling common factors. To add/subtract, find the LCD; to multiply/divide, factor first. Always state the values of $x$ that make the denominator zero.

Domain restrictions: identify every value that makes any denominator zero — these are excluded from the domain whether or not they cancel out. For example, in $\dfrac{x+1}{(x-2)(x+3)}$ the domain excludes $x = 2$ and $x = -3$.

To add or subtract rational expressions, first find the least common denominator (LCD), rewrite each fraction with the LCD as its denominator, then add or subtract the numerators only — the denominator stays the same. Simplify the result and state all domain restrictions.

To solve a rational equation, multiply both sides by the LCD to clear all fractions, producing a polynomial equation. Solve that polynomial, then check every solution against the original domain restrictions. Any solution that makes a denominator zero is extraneous and must be discarded.

Worked example — simplify $\dfrac{x^2 - 4}{x - 2}$ $(x \ne 2)$
$= \dfrac{(x-2)(x+2)}{x-2} = x+2$difference of squares; cancel $(x-2)$
Worked example — add $\dfrac{1}{x} + \dfrac{2}{x+1}$
LCD $= x(x+1)$denominators share no common factors
$= \dfrac{x+1}{x(x+1)} + \dfrac{2x}{x(x+1)} = \dfrac{(x+1)+2x}{x(x+1)} = \dfrac{3x+1}{x(x+1)}$convert each fraction; add numerators
Domain: $x \ne 0$ and $x \ne -1$original denominators cannot be zero
Worked example — solve $\dfrac{1}{x-2} + \dfrac{1}{3} = \dfrac{5}{x-2}$
Multiply both sides by $3(x-2)$: restriction $x \ne 2$LCD clears all fractions
$3 + (x-2) = 15 \Rightarrow x + 1 = 15 \Rightarrow x = 14$distribute and simplify
Check: $x = 14 \ne 2$ ✓ — not extraneousverify against domain restriction

Chapter 5

Radical Equations

Square roots and cube roots appear whenever you invert an area or volume formula: from $A = \pi r^2$ comes $r = \sqrt{A/\pi}$; the period of a pendulum is $T = 2\pi\sqrt{L/g}$; orbital speed is $v = \sqrt{GM/r}$. Checking for extraneous solutions is essential — squaring both sides forgets the sign, and ignoring this mistake produces physically impossible answers.

Diagram — y = √x (domain x ≥ 0)

Isolate the radical, then raise both sides to the appropriate power to eliminate it. Always check for extraneous solutions — squaring can introduce solutions that don't satisfy the original equation.

Worked example — solve $\sqrt{x+3} = 4$
$(\sqrt{x+3})^2 = 4^2 \Rightarrow x + 3 = 16 \Rightarrow x = 13$square both sides; solve
Check: $\sqrt{13+3} = \sqrt{16} = 4$ ✓verify no extraneous solution
Worked example — solve $\sqrt{2x-3} = x-3$
$2x-3 = (x-3)^2 = x^2 - 6x + 9$square both sides
$x^2 - 8x + 12 = 0 \Rightarrow (x-2)(x-6) = 0$rearrange; factor
$x = 2$ fails check ($\sqrt{1} \ne -1$); $x = 6$ ✓extraneous: x=2 rejected

Chapter 6

Exponential Functions

Any process with a constant percentage change per unit time is exponential: compound interest, bacterial growth, radioactive decay. The base $b$ encodes the growth factor — $b = 1.05$ means 5% growth per period. All exponential functions share the same shape: they never reach zero (horizontal asymptote at $y = 0$), they pass through $(0, a)$, and they grow or decay at an accelerating rate.

Diagram — y = 2ˣ and y = (½)ˣ (growth vs. decay)

An exponential function $f(x) = a \cdot b^x$ has constant ratio per unit increase in $x$. The base $b > 0$, $b \ne 1$. The natural exponential $e^x$ uses Euler's number $e \approx 2.718$.

To solve exponential equations, write both sides with the same base and equate exponents, or take logarithms of both sides.

Exponent laws (review)
$$b^m \cdot b^n = b^{m+n} \qquad \frac{b^m}{b^n} = b^{m-n} \qquad (b^m)^n = b^{mn}$$
Worked example — solve $3^{2x} = 81$
$3^{2x} = 3^4 \Rightarrow 2x = 4 \Rightarrow x = 2$81 = 3⁴; matching bases
Worked example — solve $2^x = 5$ (not a perfect power)
$x = \log_2(5) = \dfrac{\ln 5}{\ln 2} \approx 2.322$take log₂; change of base for calculator

Chapter 7

Logarithmic Functions

Logarithms compress exponentially large ranges into manageable numbers. The Richter scale turns earthquake energies spanning twelve orders of magnitude into a 1–9 scale. Decibels convert acoustic power ratios of $10^{12}$ to a 0–120 scale. pH expresses hydrogen ion concentrations from $10^{-1}$ to $10^{-14}$ as integers from 1 to 14. Whenever a quantity spans many powers of ten, the logarithm is the right tool to make it legible.

Diagram — y = log₂(x), inverse of y = 2ˣ

$\log_b(x) = y \iff b^y = x$. The natural log $\ln x = \log_e x$. Logarithm and exponential are inverse functions: $b^{\log_b x} = x$ and $\log_b(b^x) = x$.

Logarithm properties
$$\log_b(MN) = \log_b M + \log_b N$$ $$\log_b\!\left(\frac{M}{N}\right) = \log_b M - \log_b N$$ $$\log_b(M^k) = k\log_b M$$ $$\log_b M = \frac{\ln M}{\ln b} \quad \text{(change of base)}$$
Worked example — evaluate $\log_2(8)$
$\log_2(8) = \log_2(2^3) = 3$$8 = 2^3$; power rule
Worked example — simplify $\log_2(32/4)$
$= \log_2(32) - \log_2(4) = 5 - 2 = 3$quotient property; $32 = 2^5$, $4 = 2^2$

Chapter 8

Conic Sections

Conics are the shapes produced when a plane cuts a cone at different angles — and they appear throughout physics and astronomy. Planets orbit in ellipses; comets travel on hyperbolic or parabolic paths. Parabolic reflectors focus parallel light to a single point (satellite dishes, car headlights, telescopes). The same family of curves describes projectile trajectories, suspension bridge cables, and charged particles in a magnetic field.

Diagram — four conic sections

Conics are curves formed by the intersection of a plane and a double cone.

Standard forms (center at origin)

Circle: $x^2 + y^2 = r^2$ — center $(0,0)$, radius $r$
Ellipse: $\dfrac{x^2}{a^2} + \dfrac{y^2}{b^2} = 1$ — semi-axes $a, b$; major axis along $x$ if $a > b$
Hyperbola (horizontal): $\dfrac{x^2}{a^2} - \dfrac{y^2}{b^2} = 1$ — vertices $(\pm a, 0)$; $c^2 = a^2 + b^2$
Parabola: $y = ax^2 + bx + c$ or $x = ay^2 + by + c$

Worked example — circle $x^2 + y^2 = 16$: find center and radius
$r^2 = 16 \Rightarrow r = 4$; center $= (0, 0)$standard form
Worked example — ellipse $\dfrac{x^2}{9} + \dfrac{y^2}{4} = 1$: find semi-axes
$a^2 = 9 \Rightarrow a = 3$; $b^2 = 4 \Rightarrow b = 2$read from standard form

Chapter 9

Systems of Equations

When two constraints are both nonlinear, their solutions are geometric intersection points. In computer graphics, ray-sphere intersection (does this light ray hit this sphere?) reduces exactly to the algebra in this chapter. In structural analysis, the intersection of a load curve with a material response curve gives the operating point of the system. The number of solutions equals the number of intersection points — which geometry makes visually obvious.

Diagram — circle x²+y²=25 and line y=x+1 intersecting

A nonlinear system can have 0, 1, or more solutions. To solve, substitute one equation into the other (or use elimination), then solve the resulting equation. Always substitute back to find the full solution $(x, y)$.

Nonlinear systems often arise from geometric intersections: a line meeting a circle gives at most 2 solutions; a parabola meeting a line gives at most 2 solutions; two circles can intersect in 0, 1, or 2 points. The number of intersection points equals the number of solutions of the system.

The standard strategy is substitution: isolate one variable in the simpler equation and substitute into the more complex one. This reduces the system to a single-variable equation — often quadratic — that you can solve with standard techniques.

Worked example — solve $x^2 + y^2 = 25$ and $y = x + 1$
$x^2 + (x+1)^2 = 25 \Rightarrow 2x^2 + 2x + 1 = 25$substitute $y = x+1$
$x^2 + x - 12 = 0 \Rightarrow (x+4)(x-3) = 0$simplify; factor
$(x,y) = (-4,-3)$ or $(3, 4)$$y = x + 1$
Worked example — solve $y = x^2$ and $y = 2x + 3$
$x^2 = 2x + 3 \Rightarrow x^2 - 2x - 3 = 0$substitute $y = x^2$ into the linear equation
$(x-3)(x+1) = 0 \Rightarrow x = 3$ or $x = -1$factor
$x = 3 \Rightarrow y = 9$; $\quad x = -1 \Rightarrow y = 1$substitute back into $y = x^2$
Solutions: $(3, 9)$ and $(-1, 1)$verify in both original equations

Chapter 10

Sequences & Series

Arithmetic and geometric sequences are the two fundamental patterns of change: add the same amount each step (arithmetic — a fixed salary raise) versus multiply by the same factor (geometric — compound interest). The infinite geometric series formula $S_\infty = a/(1-r)$ is how banks value annuities, how engineers analyze filter circuits, and how mathematicians proved that $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} 1/n^2 = \pi^2/6$.

Diagram — arithmetic vs. geometric spacing

Use an arithmetic sequence when consecutive differences are constant (e.g., a salary that increases by the same dollar amount each year, uniform motion). Use a geometric sequence when consecutive ratios are constant (e.g., compound interest, population growth, radioactive decay). The quick diagnostic: subtract consecutive terms — if the result is the same number, arithmetic; divide consecutive terms — if the result is the same number, geometric.

An arithmetic sequence has constant difference $d$: $a_n = a_1 + (n-1)d$. A geometric sequence has constant ratio $r$: $a_n = a_1 \cdot r^{n-1}$.

Sum formulas
$$S_n^{\text{arith}} = \frac{n}{2}(a_1 + a_n) \qquad S_n^{\text{geo}} = \frac{a_1(1-r^n)}{1-r} \quad (r \ne 1)$$ $$S_\infty^{\text{geo}} = \frac{a_1}{1-r} \quad (|r| < 1)$$

The infinite geometric series formula $S_\infty = \dfrac{a_1}{1-r}$ follows from the partial-sum formula $S_n = \dfrac{a_1(1 - r^n)}{1 - r}$. When $|r| < 1$, the term $r^n \to 0$ as $n \to \infty$, so $S_n$ approaches $\dfrac{a_1}{1-r}$. When $|r| \ge 1$ the series diverges — the partial sums grow without bound.

Worked example — arithmetic sequence: $a_1 = 5$, $d = 3$
$a_{10} = a_1 + (n-1)d = 5 + 9(3) = 5 + 27 = 32$$n$-th term formula with $n = 10$
$S_{10} = \dfrac{10}{2}(a_1 + a_{10}) = 5(5 + 32) = 5(37) = 185$arithmetic sum formula
Worked example — infinite geometric series $1 + 1/2 + 1/4 + \cdots$
$a_1 = 1$, $r = 1/2$; $S_\infty = \dfrac{1}{1-1/2} = 2$$|r| < 1$, so it converges

Chapter 11

Matrices

A matrix is a compact way to represent a linear transformation or a system of equations. In computer graphics, every rotation, scaling, and shearing of an image is a matrix multiplication applied to coordinates. The determinant tells you whether the transformation is invertible (det $\ne 0$) and by what factor it scales area ($|\det|$). Systems of hundreds of equations in hundreds of unknowns — impossible by hand — are solved by matrix methods in milliseconds.

Diagram — 2 × 2 matrix and its effect on a square

A matrix is a rectangular array of numbers. Matrices of the same size are added element-wise. Matrix multiplication $AB$: entry $(i,j)$ is the dot product of row $i$ of $A$ and column $j$ of $B$ — only defined when the number of columns of $A$ equals the number of rows of $B$.

For a $2\times 2$ matrix $M = \begin{pmatrix}a&b\\c&d\end{pmatrix}$: $\det M = ad - bc$; $M^{-1} = \dfrac{1}{\det M}\begin{pmatrix}d&-b\\-c&a\end{pmatrix}$ when $\det M \ne 0$.

Worked example — multiply $\begin{pmatrix}1&2\\3&4\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}2&0\\1&1\end{pmatrix}$
Row 1: $[1(2)+2(1),\; 1(0)+2(1)] = [4, 2]$dot product of row 1 with each column
Row 2: $[3(2)+4(1),\; 3(0)+4(1)] = [10, 4]$dot product of row 2 with each column
Result: $\begin{pmatrix}4&2\\10&4\end{pmatrix}$assemble rows
Worked example — inverse of $\begin{pmatrix}1&2\\3&4\end{pmatrix}$
$\det = 1(4) - 2(3) = -2$$ad - bc$
$M^{-1} = \dfrac{1}{-2}\begin{pmatrix}4&-2\\-3&1\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}-2&1\\\frac{3}{2}&-\frac{1}{2}\end{pmatrix}$swap diagonal, negate off-diagonal, multiply by $1/\det$

Chapter 12

Counting & Probability

Counting — permutations and combinations — is the foundation of probability, and probability is the mathematics of uncertainty. Insurance companies use it to price risk. Clinical trials use it to determine whether a treatment works. Cryptographers use combinatorial complexity to make codes hard to break. The binomial formula governs any repeated yes/no trial with a fixed success probability: coin flips, defective parts on an assembly line, patient responses to a drug.

Diagram — tree of ordered vs. unordered selections

The Fundamental Counting Principle: if event $A$ can occur in $m$ ways and event $B$ in $n$ ways, together they can occur in $mn$ ways. A permutation counts ordered arrangements: $P(n,r) = \dfrac{n!}{(n-r)!}$. A combination counts unordered selections: $\dbinom{n}{r} = \dfrac{n!}{r!(n-r)!}$.

Probability of event $E$: $P(E) = \dfrac{\text{favorable outcomes}}{\text{total outcomes}}$. For independent events: $P(A \text{ and } B) = P(A) \cdot P(B)$.

Binomial probability
$$P(X = k) = \binom{n}{k} p^k (1-p)^{n-k}$$

The binomial probability formula applies when: (1) there are $n$ independent trials, (2) each trial has exactly two outcomes (success or failure), and (3) the probability of success $p$ is constant across all trials. Here $\dbinom{n}{k}$ counts the arrangements of $k$ successes among $n$ trials, $p^k$ is the probability of those successes, and $(1-p)^{n-k}$ is the probability of the remaining failures.

Whether events are independent or dependent changes how you compute joint probabilities. For independent events, $P(A \text{ and } B) = P(A) \cdot P(B)$ because the outcome of $A$ gives no information about $B$. For dependent events (e.g., drawing cards without replacement), use $P(A \text{ and } B) = P(A) \cdot P(B \mid A)$, where $P(B \mid A)$ is the conditional probability of $B$ given that $A$ has occurred.

Worked example — how many ways to choose 3 from 5?
$\dbinom{5}{3} = \dfrac{5!}{3!\,2!} = \dfrac{5 \times 4}{2} = 10$combination formula; order doesn't matter
Worked example — coin flipped 4 times: $P(\text{exactly 2 heads})$
$n = 4,\ k = 2,\ p = 0.5$4 independent trials; success = heads
$P(X=2) = \dbinom{4}{2}(0.5)^2(0.5)^2 = 6 \times 0.0625 = 0.375$$\dbinom{4}{2} = 6$; $(0.5)^4 = 0.0625$
Worked example — independent events: $P(A) = 0.3$, $P(B) = 0.5$
$P(A \text{ and } B) = 0.3 \times 0.5 = 0.15$independent: multiply probabilities
$P(A \text{ or } B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \text{ and } B) = 0.3 + 0.5 - 0.15 = 0.65$addition rule for probability
