Player Manual — Chapter 1: Solving a Puzzle

Choosing a Puzzle

The picker at the top of the screen has two browse modes, selected with the By Topic and By Concept toggle. In By Topic mode, choose a subject — Algebra, Algebra 2, Arithmetic, Intermediate Arithmetic, Combinatorics, Geometry, Trigonometry, Calculus, Statistics, Linear Algebra, or Graph Theory — then pick a specific problem from the list. Problems are labeled easy, medium, or hard, and a puzzle you've already solved is marked with a checkmark. Press Load Puzzle to begin. The question appears in the upper panel, and a diagram is shown for geometry, trigonometry, and calculus puzzles.

Browsing by Concept

Switch to By Concept mode to find every puzzle that involves a particular mathematical idea — for example, selecting Cosine surfaces problems from both Trigonometry and Calculus in a single list, because cosine appears throughout both subjects. Each entry is prefixed with [TRIG], [CALC], or [ALG2] so you always know its origin. Available concepts include Sine, Cosine, Tangent, Pythagorean Identity, Double-Angle Formula, Derivative, Integral, Limit, Chain Rule, Exponential, Logarithm, Quadratic Formula, Complex Numbers, Factoring, Completing the Square, Sequences & Series, Function Composition, and more. Switching back to By Topic restores the normal subject list.

Reading the Step

Every puzzle is broken into a series of steps that walk you through the solution one move at a time. The current step is displayed in the large equation panel. Informational steps show a completed transformation — press → or Next → to continue when you are ready. Interactive steps contain one or more dashed-border drop zones inside the equation, waiting for you to fill them in. The step description below the equation tells you what the move is trying to accomplish. Hover over (or tap) any symbol in the equation to see what it means — toggle this off with the 💡 Symbol tips button if you'd rather work without them.

Making a Move

The Palette on the left holds the pieces available for this step. On a mouse, drag the correct piece and drop it onto the matching drop zone in the equation. On a touch device (or with a mouse, if you prefer), tap a palette piece to select it — it gets a highlighted border — then tap the drop zone you want to place it in. If your choice is right the zone turns green; if it is wrong it flashes red with a short message telling you to check the step description, and you can try again. Some steps require you to drop the same piece into more than one zone — the game will prompt you when another zone still needs to be filled. Once all drop zones in a step are satisfied the puzzle advances to the next step on its own.

Keyboard Navigation and the Animated Demo

You can move through the puzzle entirely with the keyboard. Every step — informational or interactive — waits for you before advancing. Press → to move forward: on an informational step it simply reveals the next transformation; on an interactive step it triggers the animated demo — the correct palette piece is highlighted, the target drop zone glows, and the piece flies into place over two seconds. Press ← at any time to retreat one step, cancelling any animation in progress. The Next → and ← Prev buttons behave identically to the arrow keys. Use Give Up This Step if you are stuck and want the answer revealed — this increments your hint count.

Finishing and Scoring

After the last step the completion screen appears. Press Final Answer at any point to jump straight there if you are confident you understand the solution. Your score bar at the top tracks puzzles solved, hints used, your current streak, and your best streak — these are saved in the browser, so they carry over even after you close the tab or reload. Use Reset to restart the current puzzle from the beginning whenever you want more practice. To try a different problem, just pick a new one from the dropdowns and load it — your totals carry over.

Summation Walkthrough

When a puzzle step contains a sigma (∑) expression with numeric bounds, a ∑ Expand button appears below the main controls. Clicking it opens the Summation Walkthrough modal, which steps through every term of the sum one at a time. The top panel shows the current value of the index variable and the growing partial-sum equation — for example, at k = 4 it displays 1 + 2 + 3 + 4 = 10. The full term table below lets you see all values at a glance, with the current row highlighted. Use the ← Prev and Next → buttons, click any row directly, or press ↑ / ↓ to move through the steps. Press Esc or click outside the modal to close it and return to the puzzle. The normal puzzle arrow-key navigation is paused while the modal is open.

The Algebra 2 topic includes 16 dedicated sigma puzzles spread across all three difficulty levels. Easy puzzles practice expanding a sum by listing its terms. Medium puzzles apply closed-form identities such as the arithmetic series formula and the sum of the first n odd numbers equaling n². Hard puzzles cover the sum-of-squares formula n(n+1)(2n+1)/6, the sum-of-cubes identity [n(n+1)/2]², geometric series (ratios 2 and 3), and the telescoping product ∑k(k+1). The ∑ Expand walkthrough is available on every one of these puzzles.

Taking Notes

Every puzzle has its own private notepad. Click the 📝 Notes button in the controls bar to open it — you can jot down working steps, formulas, observations, or links to reference material. Notes save automatically as you type, so there is no Save button to press. An amber dot on the Notes button signals that the current puzzle has notes stored. You can clear a puzzle's notes at any time with the Clear notes button, or download every note you've written across every puzzle as one text file with ↓ Export all. Press Esc or click outside the notepad to close it without losing anything.

The notepad has two tabs. Edit is a plain text area where you write freely. Switch to Preview to see the note rendered with any URLs converted into clickable links that open in a new browser tab — useful for pasting a Khan Academy page, a Wikipedia article, or any other reference you want to revisit later. Switching to a new puzzle always returns the notepad to Edit mode.

Player Manual — Chapter 2: Using the Textbooks

Reading a Textbook

Every subject has a full textbook — reach one from the game's header links, from the "📖 Read about this topic →" link that appears once you load a puzzle, or from the tab bar of another textbook. That tab bar sits below the title on every textbook page and lets you jump straight from one subject to another; it scrolls horizontally if it doesn't fit your screen. Each textbook has a table of contents you can jump back to at any time, and if you follow a puzzle's deep link into the middle of a chapter, a "↩ Back to where I was" button appears so you can return to your place in the puzzle afterward.

Glossary Terms

Key terms are underlined throughout each textbook. On a desktop, hover over one to see its definition; on a touch device, tap the term once to open the definition and tap again (or tap anywhere else) to close it.

Sticky Notes

Every formula, example, and definition box has a small ✎ button in its corner. Click it to open a note for just that box — useful for writing a reminder, a worked-out variant, or a question to come back to. Notes save automatically as you type and persist in your browser between visits. A toolbar on the page lets you download every note you've written in that textbook as a single text file with the ↓ Export button.

Ask AI Tutor

Click the Ask AI button in a textbook's header to open a chat modal that already knows which chapter section you're currently reading. The first time you use it, you'll be asked to paste in a free Gemini API key from aistudio.google.com/apikey — it's stored only in your browser and sent directly to Google, never through this site. Your conversation is kept if you close the modal and reopen it later in the same visit, so you can step away to re-read a formula without losing the thread. Click new conversation in the modal's footer whenever you want to start over.

Diagram Pages

Several subjects — Arithmetic, Intermediate Arithmetic, Calculus, Combinatorics, Graph Theory, Linear Algebra, and Statistics — have a companion diagrams page with interactive, step-through visual walkthroughs of key ideas. Reach one from its textbook's "Diagrams →" header link, or browse every subject's diagrams from the Diagrams hub.
