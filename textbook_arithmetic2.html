Chapter 1

Fraction Operations

A fraction represents a part of a whole. The numerator (top) counts the parts you have, and the denominator (bottom) tells how many equal parts make one whole. Adding or subtracting fractions requires a least common denominator (LCD) — the smallest number that every denominator divides into evenly. Once fractions share the same denominator, add or subtract numerators directly and keep the denominator unchanged. Always reduce the result to lowest terms by dividing numerator and denominator by their GCF.

Multiplying fractions is more forgiving than addition: simply multiply numerators together and denominators together. You can often cross-cancel shared factors before multiplying to keep the numbers small. Dividing by a fraction means multiplying by its reciprocal — flip the divisor and proceed as multiplication. A complex fraction is one whose numerator or denominator (or both) contains a fraction; treat the main bar as division and simplify each part before combining.

A key skill is finding the LCD quickly. The LCD equals the LCM of all denominators. For small denominators, listing multiples works; for larger ones, use prime factorization and take each prime at its highest power. Fraction fluency built here pays dividends throughout algebra, since every rational expression obeys the same rules.

Key Formulas — Fraction Operations

Same denominator: $\dfrac{a}{c} \pm \dfrac{b}{c} = \dfrac{a \pm b}{c}$
Different denominators: convert to LCD first, then add/subtract numerators.
Multiply: $\dfrac{a}{b} \times \dfrac{c}{d} = \dfrac{ac}{bd}$
Divide: $\dfrac{a}{b} \div \dfrac{c}{d} = \dfrac{a}{b} \times \dfrac{d}{c} = \dfrac{ad}{bc}$

Example 1 — Adding Like Fractions: $\tfrac{3}{8}+\tfrac{5}{8}$
$\dfrac{3}{8} + \dfrac{5}{8}$ Same denominator — add numerators directly
$= \dfrac{3+5}{8} = \dfrac{8}{8}$ Sum the numerators; keep denominator 8
$= 1$ $\frac{8}{8} = 1$; result is a whole number
Example 2 — Adding Unlike Fractions (LCD): $\tfrac{2}{3}+\tfrac{1}{4}$
$\dfrac{2}{3} + \dfrac{1}{4}$ Denominators differ — must find LCD first
Multiples of 3: $3,6,9,12,\ldots$; multiples of 4: $4,8,12,\ldots$ List multiples until you find a common one
$\text{LCD}(3,4) = 12$ 12 is the smallest shared multiple
$\dfrac{2}{3} = \dfrac{8}{12}$, $\quad \dfrac{1}{4} = \dfrac{3}{12}$ Multiply top and bottom of each fraction by the same factor
$\dfrac{8}{12} + \dfrac{3}{12} = \dfrac{11}{12}$ Add numerators; denominator stays 12. Cannot reduce further.
Example 3 — Dividing Fractions: $\tfrac{5}{6} \div \tfrac{5}{9}$
$\dfrac{5}{6} \div \dfrac{5}{9}$ Division: multiply by the reciprocal of the divisor
$= \dfrac{5}{6} \times \dfrac{9}{5}$ Flip $\frac{5}{9}$ to get reciprocal $\frac{9}{5}$
$= \dfrac{\cancel{5}}{6} \times \dfrac{9}{\cancel{5}} = \dfrac{9}{6}$ Cross-cancel the 5s before multiplying
$= \dfrac{3}{2}$ Reduce $\frac{9}{6}$ by dividing numerator and denominator by GCF 3

Chapter 2

Mixed Numbers

A mixed number combines a whole-number part and a proper fraction part, written together: $2\tfrac{3}{4}$ means two wholes plus three-quarters. Mixed numbers arise naturally whenever a quantity doesn't fit into neat whole units — $1\tfrac{1}{2}$ cups of flour, $3\tfrac{3}{8}$ inches. They are entirely equivalent to improper fractions (where the numerator is greater than or equal to the denominator), and converting between the two forms is a foundational skill.

To add or subtract mixed numbers, work with the whole parts and fraction parts separately, then combine. When the fraction being subtracted is larger than the fraction you're subtracting from, you must borrow 1 from the whole-number part: rewrite that 1 as $\frac{d}{d}$ (where $d$ is the common denominator) and add it to the existing fraction numerator before subtracting. This is analogous to regrouping in whole-number subtraction.

For multiplication and division, always convert mixed numbers to improper fractions first. To convert: multiply the whole number by the denominator, add the numerator, place that sum over the original denominator. After computing, convert back to a mixed number if appropriate: divide numerator by denominator, the quotient is the whole part, and the remainder becomes the new numerator. Fully simplify the fraction part.

Key Formulas — Mixed Numbers

To improper fraction: $a\dfrac{b}{c} = \dfrac{a \cdot c + b}{c}$
Back to mixed number: $\dfrac{n}{d} \;\Rightarrow\; \left\lfloor \dfrac{n}{d} \right\rfloor \dfrac{n \bmod d}{d}$
Borrowing: $n\dfrac{p}{d} = (n-1)\dfrac{p+d}{d}$ (when $p < $ the fraction being subtracted)

Example 1 — Adding Mixed Numbers: $2\tfrac{3}{4}+1\tfrac{5}{8}$
$2\tfrac{3}{4} + 1\tfrac{5}{8}$ Add whole parts and fraction parts separately
$\text{LCD}(4,8)=8$; $\;\tfrac{3}{4} = \tfrac{6}{8}$ Convert $\frac{3}{4}$ to eighths so denominators match
$(2+1)+\!\left(\tfrac{6}{8}+\tfrac{5}{8}\right) = 3+\tfrac{11}{8}$ Fraction sum exceeds 1 — need to carry over
$= 3 + 1\tfrac{3}{8} = 4\tfrac{3}{8}$ $\frac{11}{8} = 1\frac{3}{8}$; add the 1 to the whole part
Example 2 — Subtracting with Borrowing: $5\tfrac{1}{3}-2\tfrac{3}{4}$
$5\tfrac{1}{3} - 2\tfrac{3}{4}$ Find LCD$(3,4)=12$ and convert fractions
$5\tfrac{4}{12} - 2\tfrac{9}{12}$ $\frac{1}{3}=\frac{4}{12}$; $\frac{3}{4}=\frac{9}{12}$
$\tfrac{4}{12} < \tfrac{9}{12}$ — must borrow Can't subtract a larger fraction from a smaller one
$= 4\tfrac{16}{12} - 2\tfrac{9}{12}$ Borrow 1 from 5: $5\frac{4}{12} = 4\frac{4+12}{12} = 4\frac{16}{12}$
$= 2\tfrac{7}{12}$ Subtract whole parts $(4-2=2)$ and fractions $(\frac{16}{12}-\frac{9}{12}=\frac{7}{12})$
Example 3 — Dividing Mixed Numbers: $4\tfrac{2}{5} \div 1\tfrac{1}{3}$
$4\tfrac{2}{5} \div 1\tfrac{1}{3}$ Convert both to improper fractions before dividing
$4\tfrac{2}{5} = \tfrac{4 \cdot 5+2}{5} = \tfrac{22}{5}$ $(4 \times 5)+2 = 22$; place over 5
$1\tfrac{1}{3} = \tfrac{1 \cdot 3+1}{3} = \tfrac{4}{3}$ $(1 \times 3)+1 = 4$; place over 3
$\tfrac{22}{5} \div \tfrac{4}{3} = \tfrac{22}{5} \times \tfrac{3}{4} = \tfrac{66}{20}$ Multiply by reciprocal $\frac{3}{4}$
$= \tfrac{33}{10} = 3\tfrac{3}{10}$ Reduce by GCF 2; $33 \div 10 = 3$ remainder $3$

Chapter 3

Ratios, Rates & Proportions

A ratio compares two quantities of the same kind and can be written as a fraction, with a colon ($a{:}b$), or with the word "to." Ratios are dimensionless — they describe relative size, not absolute size. A rate compares two quantities of different kinds, such as miles per hour, dollars per pound, or calories per serving. When the denominator of a rate simplifies to 1, it is called a unit rate, which is the most convenient form for comparisons.

A proportion asserts that two ratios are equal: $\frac{a}{b} = \frac{c}{d}$. When one of the four values is unknown, you can find it by cross-multiplying: set the product of the means equal to the product of the extremes ($ad = bc$), then solve for the variable. Before accepting an answer, verify that it satisfies the original proportion and makes sense in context.

The distance formula $d = rt$ is a proportion disguised as a formula. Any two of the three quantities determine the third: $r = d/t$ and $t = d/r$. Temperature conversion between Celsius and Fahrenheit uses a linear formula that combines a ratio (the $\frac{9}{5}$ scaling) with a shift ($+32$). Memorizing $25°\text{C} = 77°\text{F}$ and $0°\text{C} = 32°\text{F}$ provides useful anchors.

Key Formulas — Rates & Proportions

Unit rate: simplify ratio so the denominator equals 1.
Cross-multiply: $\dfrac{a}{b} = \dfrac{c}{d} \;\Rightarrow\; ad = bc$
Distance: $d = r \cdot t$   ($r = d/t$; $t = d/r$)
Fahrenheit: $F = \dfrac{9}{5}C + 32$  ·  Celsius: $C = \dfrac{5}{9}(F - 32)$

Example 1 — Unit Rate: 240 miles in 4 hours
$r = \dfrac{d}{t} = \dfrac{240\text{ mi}}{4\text{ hr}}$ Set up the distance formula solved for rate
$= 60\text{ mph}$ $240 \div 4 = 60$; the unit rate is 60 miles per hour
Example 2 — Solving a Proportion: $\tfrac{3}{5}=\tfrac{x}{40}$
$\dfrac{3}{5} = \dfrac{x}{40}$ One unknown — cross-multiply to solve
$3 \times 40 = 5 \times x$ Extremes $\times$ means: $ad = bc$
$120 = 5x$ Left side: $3 \times 40 = 120$
$x = \dfrac{120}{5} = 24$ Divide both sides by 5. Check: $\frac{3}{5} = \frac{24}{40}$ ✓
Example 3 — Temperature Conversion: $25°\text{C} \to °\text{F}$
$F = \dfrac{9}{5}C + 32$ Use the Celsius-to-Fahrenheit formula
$= \dfrac{9}{5}(25) + 32$ Substitute $C = 25$
$= 45 + 32$ $\frac{9}{5} \times 25 = \frac{225}{5} = 45$
$= 77°\text{F}$ 25°C is a warm day at 77°F — a useful anchor to memorize

Chapter 4

Percent Applications

Percent means "per hundred" and is a ratio with an implied denominator of 100. Converting a percent to a decimal: divide by 100 (shift the decimal two places left). Converting a decimal to percent: multiply by 100. To find a percent of a number, convert the percent to a decimal and multiply: $25\%$ of $80 = 0.25 \times 80 = 20$. Percent change expresses how much a quantity has grown or shrunk relative to where it started — positive for an increase, negative for a decrease.

Retail math uses two direct applications. A markup adds a percentage of cost to the cost itself, giving the selling price: selling price $= \text{cost} \times (1 + r)$. A discount reduces the original price by a percentage: sale price $= \text{original} \times (1 - r)$. Simple interest calculates interest earned on a principal at a fixed annual rate over time with the formula $I = Prt$, where $r$ is expressed as a decimal and $t$ is in years.

The most common mistake in percent problems is confusing the original and the new value. A reverse percent problem gives you the final amount and asks for the original. If a value after a 25% increase is $\$150$, divide by $1.25$, not by $1.75$ or some other number. The structure is always: final $=$ original $\times$ factor, so original $=$ final $\div$ factor. Setting up the equation carefully before computing prevents this error.

Key Formulas — Percent Applications

Percent change: $\%\text{ change} = \dfrac{\text{new} - \text{original}}{\text{original}} \times 100$
Markup: $\text{price} = \text{cost} \times (1 + r)$  ·  Discount: $\text{price} = \text{original} \times (1 - r)$
Simple interest: $I = P \cdot r \cdot t$  ·  Total: $A = P(1 + rt)$

Example 1 — Percent Decrease: $80 \to 60$
Original $= 80$; New $= 60$ Price dropped — calculate the percent change
$\%\text{ change} = \dfrac{60 - 80}{80} \times 100$ Apply percent change formula; change = new $-$ original
$= \dfrac{-20}{80} \times 100 = -25\%$ Negative sign confirms a decrease; it dropped 25%
Example 2 — Markup: $\$120$ cost at $25\%$ markup
$\text{Selling price} = 120 \times (1 + 0.25)$ Multiply cost by $(1 + \text{markup rate})$
$= 120 \times 1.25 = \$150$ 25% of $120 is $30; $120 + $30 = $150
Example 3 — Simple Interest: $P=\$500$, $r=4\%$, $t=3$ yr
$I = P \cdot r \cdot t = 500 \times 0.04 \times 3$ Convert rate to decimal: $4\% = 0.04$; apply $I = Prt$
$= \$60$ $500 \times 0.04 = \$20$ per year; $\$20 \times 3 = \$60$ total interest
$A = P + I = 500 + 60 = \$560$ Total amount = principal plus all interest earned

Chapter 5

Integer Operations

Integers extend the natural numbers to include negatives and zero: $\ldots, -3, -2, -1, 0, 1, 2, 3, \ldots$. On the number line, positive integers lie to the right of zero and negative integers lie to the left. The sign rules for multiplication and division are symmetric and easy to remember: same signs yield a positive result, different signs yield a negative result. These rules extend naturally to fractions and decimals.

Addition and subtraction of integers are most clearly understood on the number line. Adding a positive number moves right; adding a negative number moves left. Subtracting is always equivalent to adding the opposite: $a - b = a + (-b)$. This identity eliminates subtraction as a separate operation — everything reduces to signed addition. Multi-step problems benefit from explicit rewriting with this rule before computing.

Absolute value $|a|$ gives the magnitude of $a$ — its distance from zero — always a non-negative result. The expression $|a - b|$ represents the distance between the points $a$ and $b$ on the number line, making it useful for error bounds, differences in elevation or temperature, and any context where direction doesn't matter and only the gap between two values counts.

Key Rules — Integer Operations

Sign rules: $(+)(+)=+$; $(-)(-)=+$; $(+)(-)=-$; $(-)(+)=-$
Subtraction: $a - b = a + (-b)$
Absolute value: $|a| = a$ if $a \ge 0$; $|a| = -a$ if $a < 0$
Distance on number line: $|a - b|$ (always $\ge 0$)

Example 1 — Multiplication: $(-3)\times(-4)$
$(-3) \times (-4)$ Two negative factors — apply sign rule
Same signs $\Rightarrow$ positive Negative $\times$ negative always gives positive
$= +12$ $3 \times 4 = 12$; result is positive 12
Example 2 — Absolute Value as Distance: $|3-8|+|2-(-1)|$
$|3 - 8| + |2 - (-1)|$ Evaluate each absolute value separately
$|3 - 8| = |-5| = 5$ $3 - 8 = -5$; absolute value removes the sign
$|2 - (-1)| = |2 + 1| = |3| = 3$ Subtracting a negative: $2 - (-1) = 2 + 1 = 3$
$5 + 3 = 8$ Add the two distances
Example 3 — Adding Negative Fractions: $-\tfrac{3}{4}+(-1\tfrac{1}{2})$
$-\dfrac{3}{4} + \left(-1\dfrac{1}{2}\right)$ Both terms are negative; convert the mixed number first
$-1\tfrac{1}{2} = -\tfrac{3}{2}$ $(1 \times 2) + 1 = 3$; place over 2 and keep the negative
$\text{LCD}(4,2) = 4$; $\;-\tfrac{3}{2} = -\tfrac{6}{4}$ Convert $\frac{3}{2}$ to fourths
$-\tfrac{3}{4} + \left(-\tfrac{6}{4}\right) = -\tfrac{9}{4} = -2\tfrac{1}{4}$ Add numerators (both negative); convert to mixed number

Chapter 6

Order of Operations

PEMDAS (Parentheses, Exponents, Multiplication/Division, Addition/Subtraction) is the universal convention that ensures every arithmetic expression has exactly one correct value. Without this agreement, $2 + 3 \times 4$ could equal either $20$ (add first) or $14$ (multiply first) depending on the reader. With PEMDAS, multiplication wins and the answer is always $14$. Every calculator, spreadsheet, and programming language follows the same rules.

Multiplication and division are at the same priority level and are evaluated strictly left to right as they appear. Addition and subtraction share the lowest priority and are also evaluated left to right. Nested parentheses are processed from the innermost pair outward. Fraction bars act as grouping symbols — fully simplify numerator and denominator independently before dividing. Exponents apply only to the base they are directly written on; a negative sign without parentheses is not part of the base: $-3^2 = -(3^2) = -9$, whereas $(-3)^2 = 9$.

The most frequent errors involve skipping exponents or evaluating left-to-right without respecting priority. A reliable strategy: scan the entire expression for parentheses and exponents, resolve those completely, then scan for multiplication and division, and finally handle addition and subtraction. Writing out each step explicitly catches almost every mistake.

Order of Operations — PEMDAS

1. Parentheses — innermost first, work outward
2. Exponents — left to right (stacked exponents right to left)
3. Multiplication & Division — equal priority, left to right
4. Addition & Subtraction — equal priority, left to right

Example 1 — Mixed Expression: $3\times(4+2^2)-5$
$3 \times (4 + 2^2) - 5$ Start inside parentheses; exponent before addition
$= 3 \times (4 + 4) - 5$ $2^2 = 4$ — exponent evaluated first inside parens
$= 3 \times 8 - 5$ $4 + 4 = 8$; parentheses resolved
$= 24 - 5 = 19$ Multiply before subtract: $3 \times 8 = 24$
Example 2 — Nested Expression: $(2^3+4)\times(6-2^2)\div 6$
$(2^3 + 4) \times (6 - 2^2) \div 6$ Two separate parenthesized groups — resolve each first
$= (8 + 4) \times (6 - 4) \div 6$ $2^3 = 8$; $2^2 = 4$ — exponents inside each group
$= 12 \times 2 \div 6$ $8+4=12$; $6-4=2$ — arithmetic inside each group
$= 24 \div 6 = 4$ Left to right: $12 \times 2 = 24$; then $24 \div 6 = 4$

Chapter 7

Divisibility Rules & Factors

Divisibility rules are shortcuts that tell you — without performing long division — whether a whole number is divisible by a small number. For example, to test for divisibility by 3, add all the digits of the number; if that digit sum is divisible by 3, so is the original. These shortcuts speed up fraction reduction, LCD finding, and mental factoring of large numbers considerably.

Every whole number greater than 1 factors into primes in exactly one way (ignoring order) — this is the Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic. The Greatest Common Factor (GCF) of two or more numbers is the product of the prime factors they share, each taken at the lowest power it appears. The Least Common Multiple (LCM) uses the highest power of every prime that appears across all the numbers. GCF is used to reduce fractions; LCM gives the LCD needed to add or subtract them.

The identity $\text{GCF}(a,b) \times \text{LCM}(a,b) = a \times b$ links the two concepts and provides a calculation shortcut. For three or more numbers, this identity doesn't extend as simply, so it is best to use prime factorization directly. With practice, prime factorization of numbers up to a few hundred can be done mentally.

Divisibility Rules and Factor Formulas

÷2: last digit even  ·  ÷3: digit sum $\div 3$  ·  ÷4: last two digits $\div 4$  ·  ÷5: ends in 0 or 5
÷6: passes both ÷2 and ÷3 tests  ·  ÷8: last three digits $\div 8$  ·  ÷9: digit sum $\div 9$  ·  ÷10: ends in 0
GCF $\times$ LCM $= a \times b$ (two numbers)

Example 1 — GCF by Prime Factorization: GCF(36, 48)
$36 = 2^2 \times 3^2$ $36 = 4 \times 9$; factor each completely
$48 = 2^4 \times 3^1$ $48 = 16 \times 3$
Shared primes: $2$ and $3$; lowest powers: $2^2$ and $3^1$ GCF takes the minimum exponent for each shared prime
$\text{GCF}(36,48) = 4 \times 3 = 12$ Both 36 and 48 are divisible by 12, and 12 is the largest such number
Example 2 — LCM of Three Numbers: LCM(6, 10, 15)
$6 = 2 \cdot 3$; $\quad 10 = 2 \cdot 5$; $\quad 15 = 3 \cdot 5$ Prime factorize each number
Primes appearing: $2^1, 3^1, 5^1$ Take each prime at its highest power across all three numbers
$\text{LCM}(6,10,15) = 2 \times 3 \times 5 = 30$ 30 is the smallest number divisible by 6, 10, and 15
Example 3 — LCM(12, 18, 24)
$12 = 2^2 \cdot 3$; $\quad 18 = 2 \cdot 3^2$; $\quad 24 = 2^3 \cdot 3$ Prime factorize all three
Highest powers: $2^3$ (from 24) and $3^2$ (from 18) LCM takes the maximum exponent for each prime
$\text{LCM}(12,18,24) = 8 \times 9 = 72$ 72 is divisible by 12, 18, and 24 — and is the smallest such number

Chapter 8

Estimation & Rounding

Rounding replaces a number with a nearby value at a specified level of precision. Identify the target place value, then inspect the digit one position to the right: if that digit is 5 or greater, increase the target digit by 1 (round up); if it is 4 or less, leave the target digit unchanged (round down). Replace all digits to the right of the target with zeros (for integers) or drop them (after the decimal point). Rounding is used constantly in measurements, finance, and scientific reporting.

Significant figures (sig figs) express the precision of a measurement. The rules: every non-zero digit is significant; zeros sandwiched between non-zero digits are significant; trailing zeros after the decimal point are significant; leading zeros are never significant. To round to $n$ significant figures, count from the first non-zero digit and apply the standard rounding rule at the $n$-th position. For example, $4.8763$ rounded to 3 sig figs: count $4$ (1st), $8$ (2nd), $7$ (3rd); the next digit is $6 \ge 5$, so round up: $4.88$.

Front-end estimation retains only the leading digit of each number (treating the rest as zero) and adds those simplified values. This gives a rough lower bound and is fast for checking order of magnitude. Compatible numbers adjusts each operand to a nearby number that is convenient to compute mentally — for instance, rounding $382$ to $400$ and $519$ to $500$ to quickly estimate a sum of $900$, which is very close to the exact answer of $901$.

Key Rounding Rules

Round to place $p$: examine the digit at position $p+1$.
If $\ge 5$: add 1 to position $p$. If $\le 4$: keep position $p$ unchanged.
Sig figs: count from first non-zero digit; apply rounding at position $n$.
Compatible numbers: adjust to nearby round values; compute mentally; compare.

Example 1 — Rounding to 3 Significant Figures: $4.8763$
$4.8763$ — round to 3 sig figs Count sig figs from the leftmost non-zero digit
1st: $4$; 2nd: $8$; 3rd: $7$ The 3rd significant figure is 7
4th sig fig is $6 \ge 5$ Round the 7 up to 8
$4.8763 \approx 4.88$ Drop all digits after the 3rd sig fig
Example 2 — Compatible Numbers Estimation: $382 + 519$
$382 \approx 400$; $\quad 519 \approx 500$ Round each to the nearest hundred for easy mental math
$400 + 500 = 900$ Quick mental addition
Actual: $382 + 519 = 901$ — estimate is accurate to within 0.1% Compatible numbers gave an excellent estimate here

Chapter 9

Mean, Median, Mode, Range

Descriptive statistics summarize a set of numbers with a small number of representative values. The mean (arithmetic average) is the sum of all values divided by the count. It accounts for every data point and is the most commonly reported average, but it is sensitive to extreme outliers — a single very large value can pull the mean far above what most data points suggest as "typical." The mean is best used when data is roughly symmetric and has no extreme outliers.

The median is the middle value of the sorted data set. With an odd number of values, it is the value at the exact center position. With an even number, it is the mean of the two central values. The median is resistant to outliers and is preferred for reporting incomes, housing prices, and other skewed distributions. The mode is the most frequently occurring value; a set may have no mode, one mode, or several modes. The range (max $-$ min) is the simplest measure of spread.

A weighted mean assigns different importance (weights) to different data values. Multiply each value by its weight, sum those products, then divide by the sum of all weights. GPA calculations use credit hours as weights; graded course assessments weight each category by its percentage of the total grade. When all weights are equal, the weighted mean reduces to the ordinary arithmetic mean.

Key Formulas — Descriptive Statistics

Mean: $\bar{x} = \dfrac{\sum x_i}{n}$
Median: middle value of sorted data; if $n$ even, average the two center values
Range: $\max - \min$
Weighted mean: $\bar{x}_w = \dfrac{\sum w_i x_i}{\sum w_i}$

Example 1 — Mean: $\{8, 12, 15, 9, 11\}$
$\bar{x} = \dfrac{8+12+15+9+11}{5}$ Sum all values; divide by the count (5)
$= \dfrac{55}{5} = 11$ The mean of the data set is 11
Sorted: $8, 9, \mathbf{11}, 12, 15$ → Median $= 11$ Middle value of 5 items is the 3rd: 11
Range $= 15 - 8 = 7$ Spread from lowest (8) to highest (15)
Example 2 — Median (Even Count): $\{5, 8, 11, 14, 17, 22\}$
Data already sorted; $n = 6$ (even) With an even count, average the two middle values
3rd value: $11$; 4th value: $14$ Positions 3 and 4 are the central pair in a 6-item list
Median $= \dfrac{11+14}{2} = \dfrac{25}{2} = 12.5$ Average the two middle values
Example 3 — Weighted Mean: scores 70, 80, 90 with weights 1, 2, 3
$\sum w_i x_i = 70(1) + 80(2) + 90(3)$ Multiply each score by its weight
$= 70 + 160 + 270 = 500$ Sum the weighted values
$\sum w_i = 1 + 2 + 3 = 6$ Total weight
$\bar{x}_w = \dfrac{500}{6} \approx 83.3$ Higher-weighted high scores pull the mean above 80

Chapter 10

Unit Conversion

Dimensional analysis (also called the factor-label method) converts between units by multiplying by conversion factors written as fractions equal to 1. Because the numerator and denominator of a conversion factor represent the same physical quantity in different units, multiplying does not change the underlying value — only the label changes. Units cancel the same way common factors cancel in fractions. Setting up the chain of conversion fractions before computing prevents errors.

The metric system (SI) is built on powers of 10, making conversions a matter of moving the decimal point. Key prefixes to memorize: kilo- ($\times 10^3$), hecto- ($\times 10^2$), centi- ($\div 10^2$), milli- ($\div 10^3$), micro- ($\div 10^6$). The U.S. customary (imperial) system requires memorized conversion facts: $12\text{ in} = 1\text{ ft}$; $3\text{ ft} = 1\text{ yd}$; $5280\text{ ft} = 1\text{ mi}$; $16\text{ oz} = 1\text{ lb}$; $4\text{ qt} = 1\text{ gal}$.

Speed conversions between miles per hour and feet per second are a common multi-step application. Since $1\text{ mi} = 5280\text{ ft}$ and $1\text{ hr} = 3600\text{ s}$, the multiplicative factor is $\frac{5280}{3600} = \frac{22}{15}$, so $60\text{ mph} \times \frac{22}{15} = 88\text{ ft/s}$ exactly. Temperature conversion uses the formula $F = \frac{9}{5}C + 32$, combining both a ratio and an offset — dimensional analysis alone does not apply to temperature because of that additive shift.

Key Conversion Facts

Metric: $1\text{ km} = 1000\text{ m}$; $1\text{ m} = 100\text{ cm}$; $1\text{ cm} = 10\text{ mm}$
Imperial length: $12\text{ in} = 1\text{ ft}$; $5280\text{ ft} = 1\text{ mi}$
Time: $60\text{ min} = 1\text{ hr}$; $1\text{ hr} = 3600\text{ s}$
Speed: $1\text{ mph} = \dfrac{22}{15}\text{ ft/s}$  ·  Temp: $F = \dfrac{9}{5}C + 32$; $\;C = \dfrac{5}{9}(F-32)$

Example 1 — Metric Length: $3.2\text{ km} \to \text{m}$
$3.2\text{ km} \times \dfrac{1000\text{ m}}{1\text{ km}}$ Write the conversion factor so "km" labels cancel
$= 3200\text{ m}$ $3.2 \times 1000 = 3200$; move decimal 3 places right
Example 2 — Temperature: $25°\text{C} \to °\text{F}$
$F = \dfrac{9}{5}(25) + 32$ Apply $F = \frac{9}{5}C + 32$ with $C = 25$
$= 45 + 32 = 77°\text{F}$ $\frac{9 \times 25}{5} = \frac{225}{5} = 45$; add 32
Example 3 — Speed: $60\text{ mph} \to \text{ft/s}$
$60\,\dfrac{\text{mi}}{\text{hr}} \times \dfrac{5280\text{ ft}}{1\text{ mi}} \times \dfrac{1\text{ hr}}{3600\text{ s}}$ Chain two conversion factors; miles and hours cancel
$= \dfrac{60 \times 5280}{3600}\,\dfrac{\text{ft}}{\text{s}} = \dfrac{316{,}800}{3600}$ Only ft/s units remain after cancellation
$= 88\text{ ft/s}$ 60 mph is exactly 88 feet per second — a useful fact for physics problems
