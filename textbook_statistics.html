Chapter 1

Sampling and Data

Statistics is the science of collecting, organizing, and interpreting data to draw conclusions about the world. Because studying an entire population is often impossible, we study a sample and use it to make inferences — but only if the sample is collected carefully.

Key Terms

Definitions
  • Population — the entire group of interest (e.g., all U.S. adults).
  • Sample — a subset of the population used to draw conclusions.
  • Parameter — a numerical summary of a population (e.g., $\mu$, $\sigma$). Usually unknown.
  • Statistic — a numerical summary of a sample (e.g., $\bar x$, $s$). Used to estimate the parameter.
  • Variable — a characteristic measured on each individual in the study.

Types of Data

Quantitative (numerical): measured on a numeric scale. Can be discrete (counts: 0, 1, 2, …) or continuous (measurements that can take any value in a range, like height or temperature).

Qualitative (categorical): describes a category or group (eye color, zip code, political party). Arithmetic on these values is meaningless — you cannot average zip codes.

Levels of Measurement

Knowing the level of measurement tells you which statistical operations are valid:

  • Nominal — categories only, no ordering (eye color, blood type). Only counts and mode are meaningful.
  • Ordinal — categories with a meaningful order, but spacing is unequal (letter grades, Likert scales). Median is meaningful; arithmetic mean is not.
  • Interval — equal spacing, but no true zero (temperature in °C, years AD). Mean and standard deviation are valid; ratios are not ("60°C is not twice as hot as 30°C").
  • Ratio — equal spacing with a true zero (height, weight, time, counts). All operations valid, including ratios.

Sampling Methods

The goal is a sample that accurately represents the population. A biased sample systematically over- or under-represents some group.

  • Simple Random Sample (SRS) — every individual and every group of size $n$ has an equal chance. The gold standard for avoiding bias.
  • Stratified — divide population into homogeneous subgroups (strata: age, gender, region), then SRS within each stratum. Guarantees representation of each group.
  • Cluster — divide into naturally occurring groups (schools, city blocks), randomly select whole clusters. Practical when a complete list of individuals doesn't exist.
  • Systematic — select every $k$-th individual from a list after a random start. Simple to implement; watch for hidden periodic patterns in the list.
  • Convenience — use whoever is easiest to reach. Non-random and prone to serious bias. Avoid for any inferential purpose.

Observational Studies vs. Experiments

In an observational study, the researcher records data without intervening. These can show association but cannot prove causation because of potential confounding variables — hidden factors that influence both the explanatory variable and the response.

In an experiment, the researcher randomly assigns subjects to treatment groups. Randomization balances out confounders, making causation claims much stronger. The "gold standard" is a randomized controlled trial (RCT) with a control group and, ideally, blinding.

Example — Classifying Variables and Levels

A study of 200 college students collects: GPA (quantitative, continuous, ratio), class year (Freshman/…/Senior — qualitative, ordinal), number of siblings (quantitative, discrete, ratio), hometown zip code (qualitative, nominal).

Example — Identifying Confounding

Observation: cities with more hospitals have higher death rates. Conclusion? Not "hospitals cause death." The confounder is population size — larger cities have both more hospitals and more deaths. Always ask: "What third variable could explain this relationship?"

Descriptive Statistics — Measures of Center

A measure of center tries to capture a single "typical" value for a dataset. There is no universally best choice — the right measure depends on the data's shape and the presence of outliers.

Mean

What it is. The arithmetic mean is the sum of all values divided by the count. Think of it as the balance point of the distribution: if you placed equal weights on a number line at each data value, the mean is where the line would balance. Because it depends on every value, a single extreme outlier can pull it far from the center of the bulk of the data.

Population Mean & Sample Mean
$$\mu = \frac{\sum x_i}{N} \qquad \bar{x} = \frac{\sum x_i}{n}$$
Example — How an Outlier Distorts the Mean
Data (no outlier)$\{4, 6, 5, 7, 8\}$; mean $= 30/5 = 6$typical center
Add outlier$\{4, 6, 5, 7, 8, 100\}$; mean $= 130/6 \approx 21.7$one extreme value drags mean far right
MedianSorted: $4,5,6,7,8,100$ → median $= (6+7)/2 = 6.5$barely affected; more representative here

Median

What it is. The median is the middle value of sorted data — half the values fall below it, half above. Because it only uses the middle position (not the actual values of all data points), it is resistant to outliers and skewness. For an even number of values, average the two middle values.

Finding it: sort the data, then go to position $\frac{n+1}{2}$. For $n=5$, position 3. For $n=6$, average positions 3 and 4.

Example — Odd and Even Count
Sorted (odd, $n=5$)$2, 3, \boldsymbol{5}, 7, 9$position $(5+1)/2 = 3$; median = 5
Sorted (even, $n=4$)$2, \boldsymbol{4, 6}, 8$average positions 2 and 3: $(4+6)/2 = 5$

Mode

The mode is the value (or values) that appear most often. A dataset can be unimodal (one peak), bimodal (two peaks), or multimodal. The mode is the only measure of center applicable to nominal data — you can find the most common eye color, but not the "average" eye color.

Weighted Mean

When different values carry different importance, multiply each value by its weight and divide by the total weight. The ordinary mean is the special case where all weights are equal.

Weighted Mean
$$\bar{x}_w = \frac{\sum w_i x_i}{\sum w_i}$$
Example — Course GPA (weights = credit hours)
Scores / weights80 (wt 0.2), 90 (wt 0.5), 70 (wt 0.3)weights must sum to 1 here
Weighted products$80(0.2)+90(0.5)+70(0.3) = 16+45+21 = 82$heavier weight on 90 pulls mean above simple average of 80

Choosing the Right Measure

  • Symmetric, no outliers: mean ≈ median; either works. Mean uses all information.
  • Skewed or outliers present: median is more representative. Income distributions are almost always reported with median for this reason.
  • Categorical / nominal data: mode only.
  • Skew direction clue: if mean > median, distribution is right-skewed (long tail to the right); if mean < median, left-skewed.
Chapter 3

Descriptive Statistics — Measures of Spread

A measure of center tells you where the data is located; a measure of spread tells you how much the data varies around that center. Two datasets can have the same mean but completely different spreads — and that difference matters enormously for interpretation.

Range

Range
$$\text{Range} = \text{max} - \text{min}$$

Simple and easy to compute, but it only uses two data points and is completely dominated by a single extreme outlier. Use it as a quick sanity check, not a primary measure.

The Five-Number Summary and IQR

The five-number summary — minimum, $Q_1$, median ($Q_2$), $Q_3$, maximum — gives a compact picture of the entire distribution. Quartiles divide sorted data into four equal parts: 25% of values fall below $Q_1$, 50% below $Q_2$, 75% below $Q_3$.

Interquartile Range (IQR)
$$IQR = Q_3 - Q_1$$

The IQR measures the spread of the middle 50% of the data. Because it ignores the top and bottom 25%, it is resistant to outliers — just like the median. The standard outlier rule: any value below $Q_1 - 1.5 \cdot IQR$ or above $Q_3 + 1.5 \cdot IQR$ is flagged as a potential outlier.

Example — Five-Number Summary and Outlier Check
Sorted data$2, 5, 7, 9, 12, 15, 18, 22, 100$$n = 9$
$Q_1$median of lower half $\{2,5,7,9\}= (5+7)/2 = 6$lower 25%
Median $Q_2$middle value = $12$position 5
$Q_3$median of upper half $\{15,18,22,100\} = (18+22)/2 = 20$upper 25%
IQR$20 - 6 = 14$
Outlier fenceupper: $20 + 1.5(14) = 41$; $100 > 41$ → outlier100 is flagged

Variance and Standard Deviation

Why we square the deviations. To measure spread, start with each value's distance from the mean: $x_i - \bar{x}$. We can't simply average these — they always sum to zero (positive and negative deviations cancel). Squaring solves two problems at once: it makes all terms positive, and it penalizes large deviations more than small ones. Taking the square root at the end (to get $s$) brings the units back to the original scale.

Why divide by $n-1$. When we compute the sample mean $\bar{x}$ first and use it to compute deviations, one degree of freedom is "used up" — the last deviation is completely determined by the others (they must sum to zero). Dividing by $n-1$ instead of $n$ corrects for this and makes $s^2$ an unbiased estimator of the population variance $\sigma^2$.

Population Variance and Standard Deviation
$$\sigma^2 = \frac{\sum (x_i - \mu)^2}{N}, \qquad \sigma = \sqrt{\sigma^2}$$
Sample Variance and Standard Deviation
$$s^2 = \frac{\sum (x_i - \bar{x})^2}{n-1}, \qquad s = \sqrt{s^2}$$
Example — Sample Standard Deviation of $\{2, 4, 6\}$
Mean$\bar{x} = (2+4+6)/3 = 4$
Deviations$2-4=-2,\;\; 4-4=0,\;\; 6-4=2$each value minus mean
Squared$4,\; 0,\; 4$squaring removes negatives
$s^2$$\dfrac{4+0+4}{3-1} = \dfrac{8}{2} = 4$divide by $n-1=2$
$s$$\sqrt{4} = 2$typical value is 2 units from the mean

Coefficient of Variation (CV)

CV
$$CV = \frac{s}{\bar{x}} \times 100\%$$

The CV expresses standard deviation as a percentage of the mean, making it unitless. This allows meaningful comparison of variability across different scales or units — for example, comparing the consistency of two manufacturing processes that measure different quantities.

Example — Which process is more consistent?
Process A$\bar{x}=100\text{ g},\; s=5\text{ g}$; $CV = 5\%$
Process B$\bar{x}=20\text{ g},\; s=3\text{ g}$; $CV = 15\%$B has smaller $s$ but is 3× more variable relative to its mean
Chapter 4

Data Visualization

Frequency Tables

A frequency table lists each value (or class interval) and how many times it appears. A relative frequency is the proportion of the total:

Relative Frequency
$$\text{Relative frequency} = \frac{\text{frequency}}{n}$$

Histograms

A histogram displays quantitative data in adjacent bars. The horizontal axis shows class intervals; the vertical axis shows frequency or relative frequency. The shape of a histogram reveals:

  • Symmetric (bell-shaped): mean ≈ median.
  • Right-skewed (tail to the right): mean > median.
  • Left-skewed (tail to the left): mean < median.

Box Plots (Box-and-Whisker)

A box plot displays the five-number summary: min, $Q_1$, median, $Q_3$, max. The box spans the IQR; whiskers extend to the data extremes (excluding outliers, which are plotted individually).

Stem-and-Leaf Plots

Splits each value into a stem (leading digit(s)) and a leaf (last digit). Preserves individual data values while showing the distribution shape.

Ogives and Cumulative Frequency

An ogive plots cumulative relative frequency against the upper class boundary. Used to find percentiles and compare distributions.

Example — Relative Frequency
Freq15 students passed out of 60raw count
Rel. freq.$\dfrac{15}{60} = 0.25 = 25\%$divide by total
Chapter 5

Probability Fundamentals

Probability is the mathematical language of uncertainty. It assigns numbers between 0 and 1 to events: 0 means impossible, 1 means certain. All of statistics rests on this foundation.

Basic Definitions

Key Terms
  • Experiment — a process with uncertain outcome (rolling a die, measuring a person's height).
  • Sample Space $S$ — the set of all possible outcomes. For a coin flip: $S = \{H, T\}$.
  • Event $A$ — any subset of $S$. "Rolling an even number" = $\{2, 4, 6\}$.
  • Probability $P(A)$ — a number in $[0,1]$; all probabilities in $S$ sum to 1.
Classical Probability (equally likely outcomes)
$$P(A) = \frac{\text{number of outcomes in } A}{\text{total outcomes in } S}$$

Complement Rule

The complement $A^c$ (also written $A'$ or $\bar{A}$) contains every outcome not in $A$. Since $A$ and $A^c$ together cover all of $S$:

Complement Rule
$$P(A^c) = 1 - P(A)$$

When to use it: "at least one" problems. Computing $P(\text{at least one success})$ directly is tedious; $1 - P(\text{no successes})$ is almost always easier.

Addition Rules

Intuition: $P(A \cup B)$ = probability of $A$ or $B$ or both. Simply adding $P(A) + P(B)$ double-counts the overlap $A \cap B$, so we subtract it once.

General Addition Rule
$$P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B)$$
Mutually Exclusive (no overlap: $P(A \cap B) = 0$)
$$P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B)$$
Example — Drawing a Card
$P(\text{Heart})$$13/52$13 hearts in 52 cards
$P(\text{Face card})$$12/52$J, Q, K in each of 4 suits
$P(\text{Heart AND face})$$3/52$J♥, Q♥, K♥
$P(\text{Heart OR face})$$13/52 + 12/52 - 3/52 = 22/52$subtract overlap once

Conditional Probability

Intuition: $P(A|B)$ — "the probability of $A$ given we know $B$ happened" — restricts our sample space to just the outcomes in $B$, then asks what fraction of those are also in $A$.

Conditional Probability
$$P(A|B) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)}, \quad P(B) > 0$$

Notice: if knowing $B$ gives no information about $A$, then $P(A|B) = P(A)$ — that's the definition of independence.

Multiplication Rule and Independence

General Multiplication Rule
$$P(A \cap B) = P(A|B) \cdot P(B)$$
Independent Events (knowing $B$ doesn't change $A$)
$$P(A \cap B) = P(A) \cdot P(B)$$

Check for independence: compute $P(A) \cdot P(B)$ and compare to $P(A \cap B)$. If they're equal, the events are independent; if not, they're dependent. Do not assume independence — it must be verified.

Bayes' Theorem

What it does. Bayes' theorem "reverses" conditional probability. If you know $P(\text{test positive} | \text{disease})$, it lets you find $P(\text{disease} | \text{test positive})$ — the far more useful quantity.

Bayes' Theorem
$$P(A|B) = \frac{P(B|A)\cdot P(A)}{P(B)}$$

The denominator $P(B)$ can be expanded using the law of total probability: $P(B) = P(B|A)\cdot P(A) + P(B|A^c)\cdot P(A^c)$.

Example — Medical Test (classic Bayes)
SetupDisease prevalence: $P(D) = 0.01$ (1%)prior probability
Test sensitivity: $P(+|D) = 0.95$true positive rate
False positive rate: $P(+|D^c) = 0.05$5% of healthy people test positive
$P(+)$$0.95(0.01) + 0.05(0.99) = 0.0095 + 0.0495 = 0.059$total probability of a positive test
$P(D|+)$$\dfrac{0.95 \times 0.01}{0.059} \approx 0.161$only ~16% chance of disease given positive test!

The result surprises most people. Because the disease is rare ($P(D)=0.01$), most positives are false alarms. Low prior probability dramatically reduces the posterior probability even with a 95%-sensitive test.

Chapter 6

Counting Methods

Counting methods let us calculate probabilities by counting outcomes without listing them all. The key question is always: does order matter?

Factorials

$n!$ ("$n$ factorial") is the product of all positive integers up to $n$: $n! = n \times (n-1) \times \cdots \times 2 \times 1$. It counts the number of ways to arrange $n$ distinct objects in a row. By definition, $0! = 1$.

Example — Arranging 4 books on a shelf
$4! = 4 \times 3 \times 2 \times 1 = 24$ arrangements4 choices for 1st slot, 3 for 2nd, etc.

Fundamental Counting Principle

If event $A$ can occur in $m$ ways and — independently of $A$ — event $B$ can occur in $n$ ways, then together they can occur in $m \times n$ ways. Extend to any number of independent choices by multiplying.

Example — How many 3-character passwords using 26 letters + 10 digits?
Each slot36 choices (26 letters + 10 digits)with repetition allowed
Total$36 \times 36 \times 36 = 36^3 = 46{,}656$multiply choices for each slot

Permutations — Order Matters

When to use: selecting and arranging items where position matters — first place vs. second place, seating arrangements, a 4-digit PIN.

Permutations: $r$ items chosen from $n$, order matters
$$P(n,r) = \frac{n!}{(n-r)!}$$

Why: you have $n$ choices for the first slot, $n-1$ for the second, …, $n-r+1$ for the last. That product equals $\dfrac{n!}{(n-r)!}$.

Example — Gold/Silver/Bronze from 8 athletes
$P(8,3)$$\dfrac{8!}{5!} = 8 \times 7 \times 6 = 336$order matters: gold ≠ silver ≠ bronze

Combinations — Order Does Not Matter

When to use: choosing a group where arrangement doesn't matter — a committee, a hand of cards, a subset of questions on an exam.

Combinations: $r$ items chosen from $n$, order irrelevant
$$\binom{n}{r} = \frac{n!}{r!\,(n-r)!}$$

Why: start with $P(n,r)$ ordered arrangements, then divide by $r!$ (the number of ways to rearrange those $r$ chosen items — all of which produce the same group).

Example — Choosing 3 committee members from 8 people
$\binom{8}{3}$$\dfrac{8!}{3!\,5!} = \dfrac{8 \times 7 \times 6}{3 \times 2 \times 1} = \dfrac{336}{6} = 56$order doesn't matter; divide by $3! = 6$

Decision Guide: Permutation or Combination?

  • Ask: "If I swap two of my chosen items, do I get a different outcome?"
  • Yes → permutation: president and treasurer are different roles.
  • No → combination: a committee of Alice, Bob, Carol is the same regardless of the order you list them.
Example — 5-card hand from a 52-card deck
$\binom{52}{5} = 2{,}598{,}960$ possible handsorder of cards in your hand doesn't matter → combination

Discrete Probability Distributions

A random variable $X$ assigns a number to each outcome of an experiment. A discrete random variable takes on a countable set of values (0, 1, 2, …). Its probability distribution is a complete description of all possible values and how likely each is.

Probability Distribution

A valid probability distribution must satisfy two rules: every probability is non-negative, and all probabilities sum to exactly 1.

Rules for a Valid Distribution
$$P(x) \geq 0 \text{ for all } x, \qquad \sum_{\text{all } x} P(x) = 1$$

Expected Value

What it means. $E(X)$ is the long-run average of $X$ over infinitely many repetitions of the experiment. It is a weighted average of the possible values, where each value is weighted by its probability. It is not necessarily a value $X$ can actually take.

Expected Value (Mean)
$$E(X) = \mu = \sum x \cdot P(x)$$
Variance
$$Var(X) = \sigma^2 = \sum (x - \mu)^2 P(x)$$
Example — Expected Winnings on a Game
OutcomesWin $10 with $P=0.3$; Win $0 with $P=0.5$; Lose $5 with $P=0.2$
$E(X)$$10(0.3) + 0(0.5) + (-5)(0.2) = 3 + 0 - 1 = 2$expected gain = $2 per play on average

Linear Transformation Rules

These rules avoid recomputing from scratch when you scale or shift a random variable.

Properties of $aX + b$
$$E(aX + b) = a\,E(X) + b \qquad Var(aX + b) = a^2\,Var(X)$$

Intuition: adding $b$ shifts every outcome by the same amount — the mean shifts, but spread doesn't change. Multiplying by $a$ stretches outcomes; variance scales by $a^2$ because variance is in squared units.

Sum of Independent Random Variables

Independent $X$ and $Y$
$$E(X+Y) = E(X) + E(Y) \qquad Var(X+Y) = Var(X) + Var(Y)$$

Variances add only when $X$ and $Y$ are independent. Note: standard deviations do not add — $\sigma_{X+Y} = \sqrt{\sigma_X^2 + \sigma_Y^2}$.

Binomial Distribution

The four conditions (BINS): a binomial situation requires all four:

  1. Binary outcomes — each trial is success or failure.
  2. Independent trials — the result of one trial doesn't affect others.
  3. Number of trials $n$ is fixed in advance.
  4. Same probability $p$ of success on every trial.

If any condition fails, the binomial model does not apply. (Sampling without replacement from a small population violates independence, for instance.)

Binomial PMF — probability of exactly $k$ successes in $n$ trials
$$P(X=k) = \binom{n}{k} p^k (1-p)^{n-k}$$

Reading the formula: $\binom{n}{k}$ counts the number of ways to choose which $k$ trials are successes; $p^k$ is the probability of those $k$ successes; $(1-p)^{n-k}$ is the probability of the remaining failures.

Binomial Mean and Standard Deviation
$$\mu = np, \qquad \sigma = \sqrt{np(1-p)}$$
Example — Free-throw shooter (70% success rate) takes 5 shots. $P(\text{exactly 3 made})$?
Check BINSBinary ✓, Independent ✓, $n=5$ fixed ✓, $p=0.7$ constant ✓binomial applies
$P(X=3)$$\binom{5}{3}(0.7)^3(0.3)^2$$k=3$, $n=5$, $p=0.7$
$= 10 \times 0.343 \times 0.09 = 0.3087$$\binom{5}{3}=10$; about 30.9%
$\mu$$5(0.7) = 3.5$ made shots on averagelong-run expected value

Normal Distribution and Z-Scores

The normal distribution is the most important probability distribution in statistics. It describes countless natural phenomena — heights, measurement errors, IQ scores — and, thanks to the Central Limit Theorem (Chapter 9), it underlies nearly all inferential statistics.

The Normal Distribution

The normal distribution is a symmetric, bell-shaped, continuous distribution fully determined by two parameters: its mean $\mu$ (the center) and standard deviation $\sigma$ (the spread). Changing $\mu$ slides the bell left or right; changing $\sigma$ makes it wider or narrower.

Normal PDF
$$f(x) = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} \, e^{-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}}$$

The total area under the curve equals 1 (all probabilities must sum to 1). The curve is perfectly symmetric about $\mu$, so mean = median = mode for any normal distribution.

The Standard Normal and Z-Scores

Every normal distribution can be converted to the standard normal $Z \sim N(0, 1)$ by standardizing. The z-score tells you how many standard deviations a value is above (positive) or below (negative) the mean — making any two normal distributions directly comparable.

Standardizing: Z-Score
$$z = \frac{x - \mu}{\sigma}$$
Reverse: Raw Score from Z
$$x = \mu + z\,\sigma$$

Finding probabilities. Once you have a z-score, look up the corresponding area to the left in a standard normal table (or calculator). That area equals the probability $P(Z \leq z)$.

  • $P(Z \leq z)$ — look up directly from the table.
  • $P(Z \geq z) = 1 - P(Z \leq z)$ — complement rule.
  • $P(z_1 \leq Z \leq z_2) = P(Z \leq z_2) - P(Z \leq z_1)$ — subtract two table values.

The Empirical Rule (68–95–99.7)

For any normal distribution, these percentages hold exactly:

  • $\approx 68\%$ of data fall within $\mu \pm 1\sigma$ (z-scores between $-1$ and $1$).
  • $\approx 95\%$ of data fall within $\mu \pm 2\sigma$ (z-scores between $-2$ and $2$).
  • $\approx 99.7\%$ of data fall within $\mu \pm 3\sigma$ (z-scores between $-3$ and $3$).

A value with $|z| > 3$ is extremely rare (less than 0.3% of a normal population). This is why $z = \pm 1.96$ corresponds to the middle 95% — the standard benchmark for 95% confidence intervals.

Example — Probability from a Z-Score
GivenExam scores $\sim N(\mu=75, \sigma=5)$. Find $P(X \leq 85)$.
Standardize$z = \dfrac{85 - 75}{5} = 2.00$85 is 2 SD above the mean
Table lookup$P(Z \leq 2.00) = 0.9772$from standard normal table
InterpretAbout 97.7% of students scored at or below 85.
Example — Between two scores
Find $P(70 \leq X \leq 80)$ with $\mu=75,\; \sigma=5$
$z_1$$(70-75)/5 = -1.00$ → $P(Z \leq -1) = 0.1587$
$z_2$$(80-75)/5 = 1.00$ → $P(Z \leq 1) = 0.8413$
$P$$0.8413 - 0.1587 = 0.6826 \approx 68\%$confirms the empirical rule for $\pm 1\sigma$
Chapter 9

Sampling Distributions and the Central Limit Theorem

This chapter contains the most important theoretical result in all of statistics. Understanding it is the key to understanding why every confidence interval and hypothesis test in the following chapters works.

Sampling Distribution of $\bar{x}$

Imagine taking a random sample of size $n$, computing $\bar{x}$, writing it down, then repeating this process thousands of times. The distribution of all those $\bar{x}$ values is the sampling distribution of $\bar{x}$. It describes how much $\bar{x}$ varies from sample to sample — which determines how much we can trust any single sample mean.

Mean and Standard Error of $\bar{x}$
$$\mu_{\bar x} = \mu \qquad \sigma_{\bar x} = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}$$
  • $\mu_{\bar x} = \mu$: on average, $\bar{x}$ hits the population mean. ($\bar{x}$ is an unbiased estimator.)
  • $\sigma_{\bar x} = \sigma/\sqrt{n}$: the standard error (SE). It shrinks as $n$ grows — larger samples produce more consistent, tightly clustered estimates. Quadrupling $n$ halves the SE.

Central Limit Theorem (CLT)

The statement. For sufficiently large $n$ (rule of thumb: $n \geq 30$), the sampling distribution of $\bar{x}$ is approximately normal — regardless of the shape of the original population. The population can be right-skewed, left-skewed, bimodal, or uniform. Average it enough times, and the averages themselves become normally distributed.

Why it works intuitively. When you average many values, extremely high values and extremely low values tend to cancel each other out. The remaining variation is small and symmetric, which is the hallmark of a normal distribution. Large $n$ gives extreme outcomes fewer chances to dominate the average.

Why it matters. The CLT is why we can use normal-based z-scores and t-scores for inference — even when the data itself isn't normal. It transforms a complex real-world question ("what is the true population mean?") into a manageable calculation involving the normal distribution.

CLT: Standardized Sample Mean
$$z = \frac{\bar{x} - \mu}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}}$$
Example — How likely is an average this extreme?
GivenPopulation: $\mu=50,\; \sigma=10$. Sample $n=25$, observe $\bar{x}=55$.
SE$\sigma/\sqrt{n} = 10/\sqrt{25} = 2$standard error of $\bar{x}$
$z$$(55-50)/2 = 2.5$2.5 SEs above the hypothesized mean
Interpret$P(Z \geq 2.5) \approx 0.006$only 0.6% chance of observing $\bar{x} \geq 55$ by random chance alone — strong evidence against $\mu=50$
Example — Effect of Sample Size on SE
$n=4$$SE = 10/2 = 5$high variability; estimates spread widely
$n=25$$SE = 10/5 = 2$more precise
$n=100$$SE = 10/10 = 1$very tight; quadrupling $n$ halved SE each time
Chapter 10

Confidence Intervals

What Is a Confidence Interval?

A confidence interval (CI) is a range of plausible values for the unknown population parameter, constructed from sample data. The confidence level (typically 90%, 95%, or 99%) describes the long-run reliability of the method.

Correct interpretation: "We are 95% confident that the true parameter lies within this interval." This means: if we constructed 100 such intervals using 100 different samples, approximately 95 of those intervals would contain the true parameter. The parameter is fixed — it is the interval that varies from sample to sample.

Common misconception: "There is a 95% probability that $\mu$ is in this interval." Wrong — $\mu$ either is or isn't in any specific interval; probability only applies before the interval is computed.

CI for a Population Mean ($\sigma$ known)

Confidence Interval for $\mu$ (z-interval)
$$\bar{x} \pm z^* \cdot \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}$$
Margin of Error
$$ME = z^* \cdot \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}$$

Common critical values: $z^*_{90\%} = 1.645$, $\;z^*_{95\%} = 1.96$, $\;z^*_{99\%} = 2.576$. A higher confidence level requires a larger $z^*$ → wider interval.

Example — 95% CI for a mean
Given$\bar{x}=50,\; \sigma=8,\; n=16,\; z^*=1.96$
SE$8/\sqrt{16} = 2$standard error
ME$1.96 \times 2 = 3.92$half-width of the interval
CI$(50 - 3.92,\; 50 + 3.92) = (46.08,\; 53.92)$we are 95% confident $\mu$ is in this range

CI for a Population Mean ($\sigma$ unknown — the $t$-interval)

In practice, $\sigma$ is almost never known. We estimate it with $s$ and replace $z^*$ with $t^*$, the critical value from the $t$-distribution with $n-1$ degrees of freedom. The $t$-distribution has heavier tails than the normal — reflecting extra uncertainty from estimating $\sigma$ — but approaches the normal as $n$ grows.

$t$-Interval for $\mu$ ($\sigma$ unknown)
$$\bar{x} \pm t^*_{n-1} \cdot \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}$$

Use the $z$-interval only when $\sigma$ is known (rare). Use the $t$-interval whenever you estimate $\sigma$ with $s$ from the sample.

CI for a Proportion

CI for Proportion $p$
$$\hat{p} \pm z^* \sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}{n}}$$

Valid when $n\hat{p} \geq 10$ and $n(1-\hat{p}) \geq 10$ (enough successes and failures for the normal approximation to hold).

Example — Polling: 52% approval from 600 surveyed
Given$\hat{p}=0.52,\; n=600,\; z^*=1.96$
SE$\sqrt{0.52(0.48)/600} = \sqrt{0.000416} \approx 0.0204$
ME$1.96 \times 0.0204 \approx 0.040$ (±4%)the "margin of error" in news reports
CI$(0.480,\; 0.560)$true approval rate is between 48% and 56% with 95% confidence

Factors That Affect the Margin of Error

  • Larger $n$ → smaller ME: more data = more precision. ME shrinks by $1/\sqrt{n}$.
  • Higher confidence level → larger ME: more certainty requires a wider net.
  • Larger $\sigma$ (or $s$) → larger ME: more variable data → less precise estimates.

Required Sample Size

Minimum $n$ for a target ME (mean)
$$n \geq \left(\frac{z^* \cdot \sigma}{ME}\right)^2$$

Always round up. If $\sigma$ is unknown, use a pilot study, prior research, or the conservative estimate $\sigma \approx \text{range}/4$.

Hypothesis Testing

The Logic of Hypothesis Testing

Hypothesis testing is a formal procedure for deciding whether sample data provides strong enough evidence to reject a default assumption. The logic is proof-by-contradiction: assume the null is true, then ask "how likely is data this extreme under that assumption?" If very unlikely, reject the assumption.

We set up two competing hypotheses:

  • Null hypothesis $H_0$: the default/skeptical claim (no effect, no difference, no change). Always contains an equality: $\mu = \mu_0$, $p = p_0$, etc.
  • Alternative hypothesis $H_a$: what we're trying to show evidence for. Can be two-sided ($\mu \ne \mu_0$) or one-sided ($\mu > \mu_0$ or $\mu < \mu_0$).

We never "prove" $H_0$. We either reject $H_0$ (strong evidence against it) or fail to reject $H_0$ (insufficient evidence). "Fail to reject" is not the same as "accept" — absence of evidence is not evidence of absence.

The Five-Step Procedure

  1. State the hypotheses: write $H_0$ and $H_a$ in terms of the parameter.
  2. Choose significance level $\alpha$: commonly 0.05. This is the maximum acceptable probability of a Type I error.
  3. Compute the test statistic: measure how many standard errors the sample is from $H_0$.
  4. Find the p-value: the probability of observing a test statistic at least as extreme, assuming $H_0$ is true.
  5. Make a decision and interpret: if $p \leq \alpha$, reject $H_0$; state the conclusion in context.

One- vs. Two-Tailed Tests

  • Two-tailed ($H_a: \mu \ne \mu_0$): evidence in either direction counts. P-value = $2 \times P(Z \geq |z|)$.
  • Right-tailed ($H_a: \mu > \mu_0$): only unusually large values count. P-value = $P(Z \geq z)$.
  • Left-tailed ($H_a: \mu < \mu_0$): only unusually small values count. P-value = $P(Z \leq z)$.

Z-Test for a Mean

Z-Test Statistic
$$z = \frac{\bar{x} - \mu_0}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}}$$
Example — Full five-step test
Step 1$H_0: \mu=50$; $H_a: \mu \ne 50$ (two-tailed)testing whether mean differs from 50
Step 2$\alpha = 0.05$5% significance level
Step 3$z = (52-50)/(4/\sqrt{16}) = 2/1 = 2.00$$\bar x=52, \mu_0=50, \sigma=4, n=16$
Step 4$p = 2 \times P(Z \geq 2.00) = 2(0.0228) = 0.0456$two-tailed; area in both tails
Step 5$p = 0.046 < 0.05$ → reject $H_0$statistically significant evidence that $\mu \ne 50$

P-Value Interpretation

The p-value is the probability — under the assumption that $H_0$ is true — of observing a test statistic at least as extreme as what was computed. A small p-value means the observed data would be very rare if $H_0$ were true, so we doubt $H_0$.

Common misconception: the p-value is not the probability that $H_0$ is true, nor the probability the results are due to chance alone in a general sense. It assumes $H_0$ is true and asks about the data.

Type I and Type II Errors

$H_0$ is True $H_0$ is False
Reject $H_0$ Type I error ($\alpha$) — false positive Correct! (Power = $1-\beta$)
Fail to reject $H_0$ Correct! Type II error ($\beta$) — false negative

Reducing $\alpha$ (fewer false positives) increases $\beta$ (more false negatives) for fixed $n$. The remedy is to increase $n$, which increases power without inflating $\alpha$.

Chi-Square Goodness-of-Fit

Tests whether observed categorical frequencies match a claimed distribution. The statistic measures total discrepancy between observed ($O$) and expected ($E$) counts; larger $\chi^2$ = bigger departure from $H_0$.

Chi-Square Statistic
$$\chi^2 = \sum \frac{(O - E)^2}{E}, \qquad df = k - 1$$

where $k$ is the number of categories. Requires $E \geq 5$ for each cell.

Example — Is a die fair? Roll 60 times; $H_0$: all faces equally likely
Expected each face$E = 60/6 = 10$ per faceequally likely under $H_0$
Observed$\{10, 8, 12, 14, 7, 9\}$actual counts
$\chi^2$$\frac{0}{10}+\frac{4}{10}+\frac{4}{10}+\frac{16}{10}+\frac{9}{10}+\frac{1}{10} = 3.4$$(O-E)^2/E$ per face, summed
Decision$\chi^2 = 3.4$ with $df=5$; critical value at $\alpha=0.05$ is $11.07$ → fail to rejectno significant evidence the die is unfair

Linear Regression and Correlation

Regression and correlation let us quantify and model the relationship between two quantitative variables — useful for prediction, understanding trends, and identifying how strongly two things move together.

Scatterplots: Describing Association

Always start with a scatterplot of $(x, y)$ pairs. Describe what you see in terms of:

  • Direction: positive (both increase together) or negative (one increases as the other decreases).
  • Form: linear, curved, or no pattern.
  • Strength: how closely the points follow the pattern.
  • Outliers: unusual points that deviate from the overall pattern.

Pearson Correlation Coefficient $r$

$r$ measures the strength and direction of the linear association between $x$ and $y$. It is unit-free and always in $[-1, 1]$.

Pearson Correlation
$$r = \frac{\sum (x_i - \bar{x})(y_i - \bar{y})}{(n-1)\,s_x\,s_y} = \frac{Cov(X,Y)}{s_x s_y}$$
  • $r = +1$: perfect positive linear relationship.
  • $r = -1$: perfect negative linear relationship.
  • $r = 0$: no linear association (could still be a strong nonlinear relationship).
  • $|r| \geq 0.8$: usually considered strong; $0.5 \leq |r| < 0.8$: moderate; $|r| < 0.5$: weak.

Critical warning: Correlation does not imply causation. A high $r$ between ice cream sales and drowning deaths does not mean ice cream causes drowning — both are caused by a confounder (hot weather). Always consider third-variable explanations before claiming a cause-effect relationship.

Covariance

Sample Covariance
$$Cov(X,Y) = \frac{\sum (x_i - \bar{x})(y_i - \bar{y})}{n-1}$$

Covariance measures the direction of association but its magnitude depends on the units of $x$ and $y$, making direct comparisons hard. Dividing by $s_x s_y$ standardizes it into the unit-free correlation $r$.

Least-Squares Regression Line

The regression line $\hat{y} = b_0 + b_1 x$ is the unique line that minimizes the sum of squared vertical distances between the data points and the line (the sum of squared residuals). It always passes through $(\bar{x}, \bar{y})$.

Slope and Intercept
$$b_1 = r \cdot \frac{s_y}{s_x}, \qquad b_0 = \bar{y} - b_1 \bar{x}$$

Interpreting the slope: $b_1$ is the predicted change in $y$ for each one-unit increase in $x$. Interpreting the intercept: $b_0$ is the predicted $y$ when $x = 0$ — only meaningful if $x = 0$ is a plausible value in context.

Example — Building and interpreting a regression line
Given$r=0.8,\; s_y=5,\; s_x=2,\; \bar{x}=10,\; \bar{y}=30$
$b_1$$0.8 \times \dfrac{5}{2} = 2$each 1-unit increase in $x$ predicts 2-unit increase in $y$
$b_0$$30 - 2(10) = 10$predicted $y$ when $x=0$
Equation$\hat{y} = 10 + 2x$
Predict $x=12$$\hat{y} = 10 + 2(12) = 34$interpolation: $x=12$ is within the data range

Coefficient of Determination $r^2$

$r^2$ is the proportion of the total variation in $y$ that is explained by the linear regression model. The remaining $1 - r^2$ is unexplained (due to other variables or randomness).

Coefficient of Determination
$$r^2 = (r)^2, \quad 0 \leq r^2 \leq 1$$

If $r = 0.8$, then $r^2 = 0.64$: the regression model explains 64% of the variability in $y$. The remaining 36% is due to factors not captured by $x$.

Residuals and Model Assumptions

A residual is the vertical distance between an observed point and the regression line: $e_i = y_i - \hat{y}_i$. The sum of all residuals is always zero for least-squares regression.

Always plot residuals (vs. $x$ or vs. fitted values) to check the four assumptions of simple linear regression:

  1. Linearity — the relationship is actually linear (residual plot should show no curved pattern).
  2. Independence — observations are independent of each other.
  3. Constant variance (homoscedasticity) — residual spread is the same for all $x$ values.
  4. Normality — residuals are approximately normally distributed.

Extrapolation — A Critical Warning

Using the regression line to predict $y$ for $x$ values outside the range of the data is called extrapolation. The linear relationship observed in the data may not hold beyond that range, making extrapolated predictions unreliable or meaningless. Always state whether a prediction involves interpolation (safe) or extrapolation (risky).

Example — Residual calculation
Regression line$\hat{y} = 10 + 2x$
Observed point$(x=5, y=22)$
Predicted$\hat{y} = 10 + 2(5) = 20$
Residual$e = 22 - 20 = +2$positive: actual is 2 above the line
