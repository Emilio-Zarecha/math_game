Chapter 1

Sampling and Data

Key Terms

Definitions
  • Population — the entire group of interest.
  • Sample — a subset of the population used to draw conclusions.
  • Parameter — a numerical summary of a population (e.g., $\mu$, $\sigma$).
  • Statistic — a numerical summary of a sample (e.g., $\bar x$, $s$).
  • Variable — a characteristic measured on each individual.

Types of Data

Quantitative (numerical): measured on a numeric scale. Can be discrete (counts, like number of children) or continuous (measurements, like height).

Qualitative (categorical): describes a category or group (e.g., eye color, zip code). Cannot be averaged.

Sampling Methods

  • Simple Random Sample (SRS) — every individual and every group of size $n$ has an equal chance of being selected.
  • Stratified — divide population into homogeneous groups (strata), then SRS within each stratum.
  • Cluster — divide into groups (clusters), randomly select whole clusters.
  • Systematic — select every $k$-th individual from a list.
  • Convenience — use whoever is easiest to reach (non-random; prone to bias).

Levels of Measurement

Nominal — categories with no order (eye color). Ordinal — categories with order but unequal spacing (letter grades). Interval — equal spacing, no true zero (temperature in °C). Ratio — equal spacing with a true zero (height, weight).

Example — Classifying Variables

In a study of 200 college students, data collected includes: GPA (quantitative, continuous, ratio), class year (qualitative, ordinal), number of siblings (quantitative, discrete, ratio).

Chapter 2

Descriptive Statistics — Measures of Center

Mean

The arithmetic mean is the sum of all values divided by the count. It is the balance point of a distribution and is sensitive to extreme values (outliers).

Population Mean
$$\mu = \frac{\sum x_i}{N}$$
Sample Mean
$$\bar{x} = \frac{\sum x_i}{n}$$
Example — Computing the Mean
Data$\{4, 8, 6, 10, 2\}$5 values
Sum$4+8+6+10+2 = 30$add all values
Mean$\bar{x} = \dfrac{30}{5} = 6$divide by $n=5$

Median

The median is the middle value when data are sorted. It is resistant to outliers. For an even number of values, average the two middle values.

Example — Odd and Even Count
Sorted (odd)$2, 3, \boldsymbol{5}, 7, 9$middle is 5th position, value = 5
Sorted (even)$2, \boldsymbol{4, 6}, 8$average middle two: $(4+6)/2 = 5$

Mode

The mode is the value that appears most often. A distribution may be unimodal, bimodal, or have no mode. It is the only measure of center applicable to nominal data.

Weighted Mean

When values have different importance (weights $w_i$), use:

Weighted Mean
$$\bar{x}_w = \frac{\sum w_i x_i}{\sum w_i}$$
Example — Course GPA
DataScores 80, 90, 70; weights 0.2, 0.5, 0.3weights sum to 1
Products$80(0.2)+90(0.5)+70(0.3) = 16+45+21$multiply each score by weight
Weighted mean$= 82$sum the products

Choosing the Right Measure

  • Symmetric distributions: mean ≈ median; either works.
  • Skewed distributions or outliers: median is more representative.
  • Categorical data: mode only.
Chapter 3

Descriptive Statistics — Measures of Spread

Range

Range
$$\text{Range} = \text{max} - \text{min}$$

Simple but sensitive to extreme values.

Interquartile Range (IQR)

Quartiles divide sorted data into four equal parts: $Q_1$ (25th percentile), $Q_2$ (median, 50th), $Q_3$ (75th).

IQR
$$IQR = Q_3 - Q_1$$

The IQR is resistant to outliers. An observation is an outlier if it falls below $Q_1 - 1.5 \cdot IQR$ or above $Q_3 + 1.5 \cdot IQR$.

Variance and Standard Deviation

The variance measures the average squared distance from the mean. The standard deviation is its square root — in the same units as the data.

Population Variance and Standard Deviation
$$\sigma^2 = \frac{\sum (x_i - \mu)^2}{N}, \qquad \sigma = \sqrt{\sigma^2}$$
Sample Variance and Standard Deviation
$$s^2 = \frac{\sum (x_i - \bar{x})^2}{n-1}, \qquad s = \sqrt{s^2}$$

We divide by $n-1$ (not $n$) to make $s^2$ an unbiased estimator of $\sigma^2$.

Example — Sample Variance of $\{2, 4, 6\}$, $\bar{x}=4$
Deviations$2-4=-2,\; 4-4=0,\; 6-4=2$each value minus mean
Squared dev.$(-2)^2=4,\; 0^2=0,\; 2^2=4$square each deviation
Sum$4+0+4=8$sum of squared deviations
$s^2$$\dfrac{8}{3-1} = 4$divide by $n-1=2$
$s$$\sqrt{4} = 2$take square root

Coefficient of Variation

CV
$$CV = \frac{s}{\bar{x}} \times 100\%$$

Expresses standard deviation as a percent of the mean. Useful for comparing variability across different units or scales.

Chapter 4

Data Visualization

Frequency Tables

A frequency table lists each value (or class interval) and how many times it appears. A relative frequency is the proportion of the total:

Relative Frequency
$$\text{Relative frequency} = \frac{\text{frequency}}{n}$$

Histograms

A histogram displays quantitative data in adjacent bars. The horizontal axis shows class intervals; the vertical axis shows frequency or relative frequency. The shape of a histogram reveals:

  • Symmetric (bell-shaped): mean ≈ median.
  • Right-skewed (tail to the right): mean > median.
  • Left-skewed (tail to the left): mean < median.

Box Plots (Box-and-Whisker)

A box plot displays the five-number summary: min, $Q_1$, median, $Q_3$, max. The box spans the IQR; whiskers extend to the data extremes (excluding outliers, which are plotted individually).

Stem-and-Leaf Plots

Splits each value into a stem (leading digit(s)) and a leaf (last digit). Preserves individual data values while showing the distribution shape.

Ogives and Cumulative Frequency

An ogive plots cumulative relative frequency against the upper class boundary. Used to find percentiles and compare distributions.

Example — Relative Frequency
Freq15 students passed out of 60raw count
Rel. freq.$\dfrac{15}{60} = 0.25 = 25\%$divide by total
Chapter 5

Probability Fundamentals

Basic Definitions

Key Terms
  • Experiment — a process with uncertain outcome.
  • Sample Space $S$ — set of all possible outcomes.
  • Event $A$ — a subset of $S$.
  • Probability — a number in $[0,1]$ measuring likelihood.
Classical Probability
$$P(A) = \frac{\text{number of outcomes in } A}{\text{total outcomes in } S}$$

Complement Rule

Complement
$$P(A^c) = 1 - P(A)$$

The complement of event $A$ is everything that is not $A$.

Addition Rules

General Addition Rule
$$P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B)$$
Mutually Exclusive Events (no overlap)
$$P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B)$$

Conditional Probability

The probability of $A$ given that $B$ has occurred:

Conditional Probability
$$P(A|B) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)}, \quad P(B) > 0$$

Multiplication Rule and Independence

General Multiplication Rule
$$P(A \cap B) = P(A|B) \cdot P(B)$$
Independent Events
$$P(A \cap B) = P(A) \cdot P(B)$$

Events $A$ and $B$ are independent when knowing $B$ occurred does not change the probability of $A$: $P(A|B) = P(A)$.

Bayes' Theorem

Bayes' Theorem
$$P(A|B) = \frac{P(B|A)\cdot P(A)}{P(B)}$$
Example — Bayes' Theorem
Given$P(B|A)=0.9,\; P(A)=0.1,\; P(B)=0.15$
Numerator$P(B|A)\cdot P(A) = 0.9 \times 0.1 = 0.09$joint probability
$P(A|B)$$\dfrac{0.09}{0.15} = 0.6$divide by $P(B)$
Chapter 6

Counting Methods

Fundamental Counting Principle

If event $A$ can occur in $m$ ways and event $B$ in $n$ ways, together they can occur in $m \times n$ ways.

Permutations — Order Matters

Permutations of $n$ things taken $r$ at a time
$$P(n,r) = \frac{n!}{(n-r)!}$$
Example
$P(5,2)$$\dfrac{5!}{3!} = 5 \times 4 = 20$ordered arrangements of 2 from 5

Combinations — Order Does Not Matter

Combinations of $n$ things taken $r$ at a time
$$\binom{n}{r} = \frac{n!}{r!\,(n-r)!}$$
Example
$\binom{5}{2}$$\dfrac{5!}{2!\,3!} = \dfrac{120}{12} = 10$unordered groups of 2 from 5

When to Use Which

  • Permutations: rankings, sequences, passwords where order matters.
  • Combinations: committees, teams, hands of cards where order doesn't matter.
Chapter 7

Discrete Probability Distributions

Probability Distribution

A probability distribution lists all possible values of a discrete random variable $X$ and their probabilities, satisfying: $P(x) \geq 0$ for all $x$ and $\sum P(x) = 1$.

Expected Value and Variance

Expected Value (Mean)
$$E(X) = \mu = \sum x \cdot P(x)$$
Variance
$$Var(X) = \sigma^2 = \sum (x - \mu)^2 P(x)$$

Linear Transformation Rules

Properties of $aX + b$
$$E(aX + b) = a\,E(X) + b$$ $$Var(aX + b) = a^2\,Var(X)$$

Adding a constant shifts the mean but does not affect variance. Multiplying by $a$ scales variance by $a^2$.

Sum of Independent Random Variables

Independent $X$ and $Y$
$$E(X+Y) = E(X) + E(Y)$$ $$Var(X+Y) = Var(X) + Var(Y)$$

Binomial Distribution

Use the binomial when: fixed number of trials $n$, each trial is success/failure, trials are independent, constant probability of success $p$.

Binomial PMF
$$P(X=k) = \binom{n}{k} p^k (1-p)^{n-k}$$
Binomial Mean and Variance
$$E(X) = np, \qquad Var(X) = np(1-p)$$
Example — Binomial $n=3,\, p=0.5,\, k=2$
Formula$\binom{3}{2}(0.5)^2(0.5)^1$choose 2 successes from 3 trials
Evaluate$3 \times 0.25 \times 0.5$$\binom{3}{2}=3$; $(0.5)^2=0.25$
Result$= 0.375$37.5% probability
Chapter 8

Normal Distribution and Z-Scores

The Normal Distribution

The normal distribution is a symmetric, bell-shaped continuous distribution characterized by its mean $\mu$ and standard deviation $\sigma$. Its PDF is:

Normal PDF
$$f(x) = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}}$$

The total area under the curve equals 1. The distribution is completely symmetric about $\mu$.

The Standard Normal

A normal distribution with $\mu=0$ and $\sigma=1$ is the standard normal, denoted $Z \sim N(0,1)$. Any normal variable can be standardized:

Z-Score (standardizing)
$$z = \frac{x - \mu}{\sigma}$$

The z-score tells how many standard deviations $x$ is above (positive) or below (negative) the mean. To recover $x$ from $z$:

Reverse: Raw Score from Z
$$x = \mu + z\,\sigma$$

The Empirical Rule (68–95–99.7)

For any normal distribution:

  • Approximately $68\%$ of data fall within $\mu \pm 1\sigma$.
  • Approximately $95\%$ of data fall within $\mu \pm 2\sigma$.
  • Approximately $99.7\%$ of data fall within $\mu \pm 3\sigma$.
Example — Finding a Z-Score
Given$x=85,\; \mu=75,\; \sigma=5$
$z$$\dfrac{85-75}{5} = \dfrac{10}{5} = 2$85 is 2 standard deviations above the mean
Example — Raw Score from Z
Given$z=2,\; \mu=50,\; \sigma=10$
$x$$50 + 2(10) = 70$use $x = \mu + z\sigma$
Chapter 9

Sampling Distributions and the Central Limit Theorem

Sampling Distribution of $\bar{x}$

When we take repeated random samples of size $n$ from a population with mean $\mu$ and standard deviation $\sigma$, the sampling distribution of the sample mean has:

Mean and Standard Error of $\bar{x}$
$$\mu_{\bar x} = \mu, \qquad \sigma_{\bar x} = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}$$

$\sigma_{\bar x}$ is called the standard error (SE). As $n$ increases, SE decreases — larger samples give more precise estimates.

Central Limit Theorem (CLT)

For sufficiently large $n$ (typically $n \geq 30$), the sampling distribution of $\bar{x}$ is approximately normal, regardless of the population's shape. This is the most important theorem in statistics.

CLT: Z-score for a Sample Mean
$$z = \frac{\bar{x} - \mu}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}}$$
Example — Z-Score for $\bar{x}$
Given$\bar{x}=55,\; \mu=50,\; \sigma=10,\; n=25$
SE$\dfrac{10}{\sqrt{25}} = \dfrac{10}{5} = 2$standard error
$z$$\dfrac{55-50}{2} = 2.5$2.5 SE above mean
Chapter 10

Confidence Intervals

What Is a Confidence Interval?

A confidence interval (CI) is a range of values that is likely to contain the true population parameter. A 95% CI means: if we repeated the sampling procedure many times, 95% of the intervals produced would contain the true parameter.

CI for a Population Mean ($\sigma$ known)

Confidence Interval for $\mu$
$$\bar{x} \pm z^* \cdot \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}$$

Common critical values: $z^*_{90\%} = 1.645$, $z^*_{95\%} = 1.96$, $z^*_{99\%} = 2.576$.

Margin of Error
$$ME = z^* \cdot \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}$$
Example — 95% CI
Given$\bar{x}=50,\; \sigma=8,\; n=16,\; z^*=1.96$
SE$8/\sqrt{16} = 8/4 = 2$standard error
ME$1.96 \times 2 = 3.92$margin of error
CI$(50-3.92,\; 50+3.92) = (46.08,\; 53.92)$interval estimate

CI for a Proportion

CI for Proportion $p$
$$\hat{p} \pm z^* \sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}{n}}$$
Example — Proportion CI
Given$\hat{p}=0.5,\; n=400,\; z^*=1.96$
SE$\sqrt{0.5(0.5)/400} = \sqrt{0.000625} = 0.025$
ME$1.96 \times 0.025 = 0.049$
CI$(0.451,\; 0.549)$contains true proportion with 95% confidence

Required Sample Size

Minimum $n$ for desired ME
$$n \geq \left(\frac{z^* \cdot \sigma}{ME}\right)^2$$

Always round up to ensure the margin of error is at most the specified value.

Chapter 11

Hypothesis Testing

The Logic of Hypothesis Testing

We start with a null hypothesis $H_0$ (a claim of no effect / no difference) and an alternative hypothesis $H_a$. We use sample data to decide whether to reject $H_0$.

One-Sample Z-Test for a Mean

Z-Test Statistic
$$z = \frac{\bar{x} - \mu_0}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}}$$
Example
Given$\bar{x}=52,\; \mu_0=50,\; \sigma=4,\; n=16$testing $H_0{:}\,\mu=50$
SE$4/\sqrt{16} = 1$
$z$$(52-50)/1 = 2$2 SE above the hypothesized mean
Decision$|z|=2 > 1.96$ → reject $H_0$ at $\alpha=0.05$strong evidence against $H_0$

P-Value and Significance Level

The p-value is the probability of observing a test statistic at least as extreme as the one computed, assuming $H_0$ is true.

  • If $p \leq \alpha$: reject $H_0$ (results are statistically significant).
  • If $p > \alpha$: fail to reject $H_0$ (insufficient evidence).

Type I and Type II Errors

  • Type I error ($\alpha$) — rejecting a true $H_0$ ("false positive").
  • Type II error ($\beta$) — failing to reject a false $H_0$ ("false negative").
  • Power $= 1 - \beta$ — probability of correctly rejecting a false $H_0$.

Chi-Square Goodness-of-Fit

Tests whether observed frequencies match expected frequencies.

Chi-Square Statistic
$$\chi^2 = \sum \frac{(O - E)^2}{E}$$
Example — $\chi^2$ with Observed $\{10,20,30\}$, Expected $\{20,20,20\}$
Compute$\dfrac{(10-20)^2}{20} + \dfrac{(20-20)^2}{20} + \dfrac{(30-20)^2}{20}$one term per category
$= 5 + 0 + 5 = 10$$\chi^2 = 10$

Linear Regression and Correlation

Scatterplots and Correlation

A scatterplot displays paired data $(x, y)$. We look for direction (positive/negative), form (linear/curved), and strength. The Pearson correlation coefficient $r$ measures linear association:

Pearson Correlation $r$
$$r = \frac{\sum (x_i - \bar{x})(y_i - \bar{y})}{(n-1)\,s_x\,s_y} = \frac{Cov(X,Y)}{s_x s_y}$$

$r$ is always in $[-1, 1]$. $r = \pm 1$ is a perfect linear relationship; $r = 0$ means no linear association.

Covariance

Sample Covariance
$$Cov(X,Y) = \frac{\sum (x_i - \bar{x})(y_i - \bar{y})}{n-1}$$

Least-Squares Regression Line

The regression line $\hat{y} = b_0 + b_1 x$ minimizes the sum of squared residuals.

Slope and Intercept
$$b_1 = r \cdot \frac{s_y}{s_x}, \qquad b_0 = \bar{y} - b_1 \bar{x}$$
Example — Finding the Regression Line
Given$r=0.8,\; s_y=5,\; s_x=2,\; \bar{x}=10,\; \bar{y}=30$
$b_1$$0.8 \times \dfrac{5}{2} = 2$slope
$b_0$$30 - 2(10) = 10$y-intercept
Line$\hat{y} = 10 + 2x$regression equation
Predict $x=3$$10 + 2(3) = 16$plug in $x$

Coefficient of Determination $r^2$

$r^2$ is the proportion of the total variation in $y$ explained by the linear relationship with $x$. If $r = 0.8$, then $r^2 = 0.64$: the regression model accounts for 64% of the variability in $y$.

Coefficient of Determination
$$r^2 = (r)^2, \quad 0 \leq r^2 \leq 1$$

Residuals and Regression Assumptions

A residual is $e_i = y_i - \hat{y}_i$. The regression model assumes: linearity, independence, constant variance (homoscedasticity), and normally distributed residuals. Always plot residuals to check assumptions.

Example — Prediction
Line$\hat{y} = 2x + 5$given regression equation
Predict$\hat{y} = 2(3) + 5 = 6 + 5 = 11$substitute $x = 3$
